In a labor market with cheap talk, employers send messages about their willingness to pay for higher-ability workers, which job-seekers use to direct their search and adjust wage bids. Theoretically, under certain conditions, an informative separating equilibrium exists. Such cheap-talk improves sorting and efficiency, and wages adjust. This is borne out in an experiment in an online freelance labor market: only some employers' stated preferences for experienced workers were revealed to job-seekers. The revelation led job-seekers to target appropriate employers and tailor wage bids, improving match quality through better sorting and demonstrating the power of cheap talk for talent matching.

\section{Introduction}

 Today's workforce repeatedly needs to look for jobs in a changing marketplace. This is most evident for freelancers, who make up nearly 40\% of the US workforce in 2022 and who frequently change whom they are working for \citep{Bloomberg2022}, but this might also apply to the non-trivial subset of workers in traditional employment who need to look for new employment at sub-annual frequency.\footnote{A recent set of papers have explored heterogeneity in job-loss rates among employed workers (\cite{hall2022unemployed,ahn2023dual,gregory2021alpha}). \cite{gregory2021alpha} explicitly consider employment in the traditional sense that excludes self-employment, contracting work, or any other form of employment that
is not covered by Unemployment Insurance. They find that 17\% of that workforce has twice the likelihood of losing employment in the first year than to continue beyond the first year. For another 26\%, that chance is roughly even.} Freelancers and short-duration workers face a non-trivial problem: since their time on a job is short they cannot spend weeks and months on job search. Rather, they need to quickly screen through a wealth of possible jobs in order to narrow in on those that are most promising given their own skill-set. Especially in these fast-moving markets it is critical to design the market such that workers find valuable matches easily. 


 
 This paper investigates whether cheap-talk in the sense of \cite{crawford1982strategic} is a conducive tool to help navigate this challenge and improve labor market matching. Recent theory papers have pointed out that labor markets could become much more efficient when firms can use cheap talk to indicate how important it is for them to hire \citep{menzio2007theory, kimKircher2015}. Yet, there has been no attempt to investigate the usefulness of cheap-talk empirically, and the existing theories are remarkably stylized in their reliance on homogeneous workers. This paper aims to fill this gap by providing evidence from a large-scale field experiment that facilitates cheap talk on an online job search site for freelancers where firms were enabled to more easily express their preference for rookies vs. experts. We document that this leads to stronger ability sorting in applications and hires, and drives wage adjustments. We extend existing cheap talk theories of the labor market by allowing for complementarities across jobs and worker skills and by comparing across information environments, and use its basic insights as a guide for our empirical investigation. 
 


 Consider an online matching platform that brings together workers with short-term jobs. The market under consideration for our empirical analysis \citep{Horton2010} exhibits a market structure that resembles the theoretical structure in \cite{kimKircher2015}: workers who choose to enter the market screen the job openings and choose where to apply. In their application they include a wage demand as is common on most freelance platforms. Firms then select the applicant they like best and pay the demanded wage. The market designer understands that employers prefer workers with more experience ~\citep{pallais2010inefficient, stanton2011landing} and are often willing to accept higher wage bids over lower wage bids \citep{hortonVasserman2020} to improve worker quality. But firms likely differ in how strong this preference is, even within a narrow category of work:
Some jobs are willing to pay more than others for more experienced, expert workers because of the importance or complexity of their tasks. This is important information for the workers, but it is hard to obtain objective information on this. So the planner contemplates facilitating cheap talk by introducing an easy way for firms to express such preferences for workers to search across these expressions. 
 
 We extend existing theories to capture the empirically relevant setting where firms are not only interested in hiring \emph{per se}, but also differentially care about worker ability. On each dimension preferences across firms are aligned: all firms like to hire and pay low wages, and all firms like more able workers. How much firms are interested in either direction might allow them to differentiate themselves. While it might seem trivial that those firms that are particularly interested in high-ability workers should reveal this, it turns out not to be obvious for two reasons: first, they might not want to reveal it since their revelation turns out to drive up wage demands by prospective freelance workers holding their ability constant. Second, even if these firms would like to reveal their preference for talent, other firms might simply state the same if this brings more high skilled applicants. On the other hand, some firms might like to reveal that they are not looking for the highest talent if that reduces wage demands, holding ability constant. While an equilibrium with informative cheap talk is not always guaranteed due to these effects, truthful cheap talk can be supported for sufficient differences in valuations for talent. The theory predicts that informative cheap-talk messages increase ability sorting of skilled workers to jobs that value them, redirects applications away from low towards high type firms if jobs differ mainly in valuations, and a given worker increases wage demands at high-valuation firms and reduce them at low-valuation firms. This raises efficiency, and persists from partial to full market roll-out. 

 
We apply these insights as a guide for our empirical analysis of a cheap-talk intervention on a large online freelance platform. Cheap talk is introduced by asking firms to click for each job posting whether they want ``entry level workers at the lowest rate'' or ``experts at higher rates.'' There is also a third, intermediate option. Jobs are randomized into different treatment arms. In the explicit arm, a random set of firms is informed that that their selection is visible to job seekers, so they have strategic incentives vis-\`a-vis their potential applicants. Another set of firms is informed that their selection is not revealed to job seekers, so they have no strategic incentives vis-\`a-vis their potential applicants. If lack of incentives makes truth-telling a focal point, we would expect firms in the latter treatment to reveal their true preferences. If strategic incentives favor mis-representation of preferences in the first treatment, one might expect differences in the distribution of answers relative to the second treatment. Across nine broad occupational categories and the three choice options within each, there is no statistical difference in answers even though choice probabilities are tightly estimated. 

This suggests that even when firms know that their answer is shown to applicants they reveal their preferences truthfully. An alternative interpretation is that all firms always ``babble'' and the content of the messages has no real meaning, which induces workers to simply ignore the messages. Such ``babbling equilibria'' are always present in cheap talk games. So do workers react to the messages? This can be seen by comparing how they apply for different types of jobs and what wages they ask for across these two treatments. One can also study this in an alternative treatment arm where firms make their selection and only afterwards the experimenter randomly decides which ones are shown to workers and which ones are hidden.\footnote{The advantage of the third treatment arm is that firms who choose, e.g., ``experts at higher rates'' and their message is revealed are by construction on average similar to firms that choose ``experts at higher rates'' but their choice is hidden. This is true also in the other two treatment arms if firms indeed answer truthfully, but could be violated if they do not.} Either way, the empirical results show clearly that workers do not ignore the messages: For firms that selected ``experts at higher rates'' revealing the messages increases the average measured ability of applicants (proxied either by cumulative prior earnings or experience on the platform) relative to their counterparts that had their selection hidden by 7\%. The opposite holds for firms who select ``entry level workers at lowest rates,'' where measured ability decreases by an entire 18\%. Ability sorting therefore increases, raising the average log-ability gap between firms wanting ``experts'' and those wanting ``entry level'' by 50\% compared to the sorting that arises in the absence of the messages. These results approximately carry over to the quality of the worker that is actually hired.

 For jobs that have their selections revealed to workers, the number of applications shifts away from those who selected ``entry level'': compared to comparable counterparts with hidden selection, applications drop by 15\%. The applicants are now less costly because of their low skills, but even a given worker reduces her/his wage demands when s/he sees that the firm selected ``entry level'' compared to her/his bids at comparable firm where the information is hidden. This conforms with our basic theory, though the size of the effect might be surprising: Using worker-fixed effects to control for worker quality shows a substantial reduction of 10\% when ``entry level'' is revealed rather than hidden even though firms in either case can obviously use their usual job description to convey any message they like. On the contrary, for firms that select ``experts'' the visibility of this selection increases wage demands compared to bids of the same worker at comparable firms where the selection is hidden, increasing wage demands of a given worker by roughly 10\%. So asking for and attracting better workers is partially offset by higher wages for a given worker's skill, while asking for low-skilled workers is rewarded by lower wage demands for a given worker's skill.  
 
 The market seems to perform better when messages are revealed, in the sense that overall hours increase (by nearly 5\%) and total wage payments increase (by 2.5\%). The same amount of new hires is achieved with less job applications, indicating that workers are better able to target their search and reduce ``wasted'' applications. And willingness to recommend the platform increases. The fraction of firms that choose each of the answers remains remarkably stable over time, even when the first treatment arm (selection is shown to workers) is rolled out to the whole market. This shows that the market does not unravel in any direction once firms gather experience with this new tool. 

Why is the additional cheap talk feature successful in allowing better sorting in the market? Could firms not write whatever they please in the job description? Job descriptions have two downsides: they are messy and hard to read through at a glance, and they lack a clear vocabulary. Firms that require an expert might alternatively say that they value experience, look for high skills, appreciate quality work, or have complex tasks to perform. The lack of a common language that can be easily searched makes it difficult and time consuming to spot the right jobs. Nevertheless, higher ability workers already manage to apply more to firms that desire them, even without the standardized cheap-talk option. But the institutionalized cheap talk feature provides one common vocabulary, and is visible and easily searchable. Sorting improves further, and wage demands adjust accordingly. 

Obviously many online platforms already experiment with options that allow participants to provide easy messages to the rest of the market. But on the academic side this is yet an understudied effort. We view our contribution as a starting point. It addresses an important market---freelance work, projected to become the dominant form of work within this decade \citep{Upwork2017}---and focuses on a crucial domain for information provision: the matching of talent to the jobs that require it, which is at the heart of efficient labor allocations. While we think that our study is a starting point for bigger questions on re-designing cheap talk in platform-mediated marketplaces more generally, we believe that even its more limited application to skill-matching in freelance labor markets offers important initial contributions: the market seemingly did not exploit the full extent of cheap talk possibilities prior to the intervention, and facilitating cheap talk has powerful implications for talent sorting and wages.

\section{Relation to Existing Literature}\label{sec:literature}

Using cheap talk for market design purposes is still in its infancy, despite the arguably abundant possibilities it offers. Since babbling equilibria are always present and can interfere with actual real-life applications, studying cheap-talk interventions is not just a theoretical but also a very applied endeavour. 

Arguably, the most famous contribution is the establishment and analysis of signals introduced in the junior market for academic economists \citep{coles2013preference, kushnir2013harmful}. The key aspect of that intervention is to introduce private messages that can be sent to a limited number of counter-parties to show interest. The messages are not observed by the overall market, and were initially intended to allow for horizontal differentiation where those who like a particular location over another for random reasons can make this known. The idea of restricting the number of messages to some small number is to make cheap talk ``scarce'' in the sense that it is not possible to send a signal to everyone, and thereby lending it credibility. Similar attempts have been introduced into other markets such as the dating market \citep{lee2011propose}.
 

Our focus on searchable cheap talk messages about general preferences is novel. Here, messages are intended for the entire market, and declare preference on a horizontal dimension like experience that all employers might prefer, but arguably to different degrees. Cheap talk is not facilitated by making it ``scarce,'' but by making it particularly easy and by providing a unifying language that can be easily understood and searched over. Due to the global nature of the messaging the type of theory necessary to understand its impact is therefore also different. 

Perhaps the closest related paper is \cite{tadelis2011information}, but in this case, it was the platform that invested in the costly collection of additional objective information about quality to thicken markets and encourage sorting, as opposed to participants themselves using cheap talk for organization.
\cite{belot_etal2018} study information provision about occupational fit for job-seekers, but it is their platform that determines the definition of ``fit.'' 

Another contribution of our paper is to show that a market-designing platform can substantively improve market information with low-cost market interventions.
In contrast to conventional models that take information limitations as essentially a fixed feature, our paper shows these limitations are mutable.  
Furthermore, the exact intervention could be implemented by any other computer-mediated job board that controls how job posts are initially posted and displayed to job seekers.\footnote{
E.g., Monster, Indeed, SimplyHired, LinkedIn, Craigslist, Facebook Jobs, Upwork, etc. 
}
As more of economic life and the job search process becomes computer-mediated \citep{kuhn2004internet,kuhn2014internet,varian2010computer}, the opportunity to shape matching markets through purely informational interventions is likely to grow \citep{horton2017effects, belot_etal2018, gee2019more, bhole2021}.

Recent papers have started to describe the job postings of firms and the applications by workers: for example, how posted wages and posted ability requirements in online job postings changed over time \citep{choi2020wage} and how wage announcements or job titles affect workers' job applications \citep{banfi2019high, belot2022wage, marinescu2020opening}; or how many job postings on a Chinese job board ask for a specific gender \citep{kuhn2013gender} and how this shapes applications by gender and employer call-backs \citep{kuhn2020gender}. In contrast to our contribution, these papers usually rely on observational data without experimental variation (see \cite{belot2022wage} for an exception), and do not explicitly discuss the incentives for firms to state their preferences truthfully. Moreover, none of these papers studies the question whether additional cheap talk possibilities could further alter the market allocations and help with ability sorting, which is at the heart of our work. Closest in this spirit might be the work by \cite{kuhn2023happens} that studies the consequences of a sudden ban of specific gender requests on Chinese job boards, which is akin to the total elimination of cheap talk across a particular dimension.\footnote{Theoretical models of cheap talk usually do not specify what message is and any other talk could constitute the same message. In practice, however, a commonly used language is key to convey even cheap-talk information.} In our setup we increase cheap talk options, which might seem particularly hard because firms could express preferences for worker skills in their job descriptions already before the intervention and workers had search functions to screen among them. Still, this did not exhaust screen talk options as our interventions had large effects on sorting and wages, arguably by providing an even easier commonly understood way of communicating. 

A final contribution of the paper is providing strong evidence in favor of a directed search characterization of the matching process.\footnote{For an overview of directed or competitive search models, see \cite{wright_etal2021}, with fundamental contributions going back to, e.g., \cite{peters_1991}, \cite{peters_1997}, \cite{moen1997competitive}, \cite{acemoglu_shimer_1999}, and \cite{burdett_etal2001}. Apart from \cite{menzio2007theory} and \cite{kimKircher2015} discussed above, noteworthy recent contributions include \cite{kim_2012} who studies market segmentation with a focus on adverse selection, and \cite{albrecht_etal2016} who study signaling in the housing market where messages entail some real costs.\\ Sorting based on costly signals has also been studied in matching games without search frictions where individuals can ``burn money'' to signal their types (see, e.g., \cite{hoppe2009theory}). Clearly such signalling games where different announcements carry different costs are very different from the pure cheap-talk setting that we analyze and that characterizes many online markets.}
As we can observe applications in our empirical setting, we can observe how much of the search is already directed, with workers responsive to the observable attributes of jobs, not simply in where they apply, but in how they bid.
Yet the explicit messages introduce a significant additional effect broadly in line with our model predictions.
Although the sorting between workers and jobs that can both be ordered along one ``quality'' dimension has long been studied both in random search models (e.g., \cite{shimer2000assortative}, \cite{teulings2004right, gautier2006large}, \cite{eeckhout2010sorting}, \citep{bagger2019empirical}) as well as in directed search models (e.g., \cite{shi2001, shi2002}, \cite{shimer_2005}, \cite{eeckhout2011identifying}, \cite{cai2021search}), no paper that we are aware of has explored how cheap talk could be embedded in a job search platform to facilitate sorting. On a technical level this requires comparative statics of the equilibrium across information environments, which we have not seen in existing contributions, and an analysis of incentives for truth-telling. Our theory borrows the basic multilateral meeting and one-on-one production structure from \cite{shi2002} and \cite{shimer_2005}, but instead of wage posting our firms only post messages. Workers instead ask for particular wages, as we document for the empirical setting that we analyze and as modeled in \cite{kimKircher2015} (who abstracted from sorting considerations). Although our empirical context is an online freelance labor market and might be most appropriate to this fast-growing market segment, the search and matching process is quite similar in its fundamentals to the matching process in more conventional markets.  




\section{A Model of Cheap Talk in a Directed Search Labor Market} \label{sec:theory}

We first present a stylized model of a labor market that captures some of the salient features in our experimental setting. The salient features we have in mind are: (1) workers can see a large number of employment options on an online platform; they have to select a small number (in the model, just one) to apply for work and include a wage demand. The feature that those who supply labor make a wage demand is a pervasive feature of the gig economy (common to the biggest platforms like Taskrabbit, Upwork, Fiverr, Freelancer, etc.), maybe because this is easier than for the employer to post a skill-dependent wage schedule.\footnote{In markets where skills are important the wage possibly become a possibly non-linear function of ability---as in the wage-schedule-posting model of \cite{shimer_2005}. If a skill-dependent wage schedule is too complicated, employers might resort to posting a rather broad wage interval as is common to many traditional labor markets, but does not allow a quick wage resolution that is desired in the gig economy.} (2) For a platform to attract workers it has to offer expected benefits that at least meet the expected utility that workers can obtain elsewhere off- or on-line. (3) Employers on the platform select the applicant they like best and pay the wage demanded.
Worker differ in ability and associated wage demands, and jobs differ in how much they benefit from a more able worker.
(4) Job openings have text that entails a signal about the job and how much it relies on high ability, but this information is not codified and is hard to search. Cheap talk is explicitly introduced by asking employers to choose one from a discrete set of options in the spirit of ``looking for the highest ability even at high wages'' or ``looking to pay lowest wages, with less concern for ability'', which are codified and easy for workers to search over. 

The theory is intended to capture these features in a simple way but does not attempt to provide the largest theoretical generality.
Rather, we aim for tractability to help organize our thoughts regarding the kind of analysis and results we might expect to find in the experimental setting where cheap talk is or is not facilitated. The key insights are collected in our main proposition (Proposition \ref{comparison1}). For our derivations, we assume that the researcher can observe the true type of the firm, even though in the model this is the firms' private information that workers need to infer through cheap talk or signals. The subsequent experimental setting uses several treatment arms to achieve this. We next lay out the market environment in full detail, followed by our main proposition and an intuitive discussion of the driving forces behind it. The deeper mathematical details, robustness to more general formulations, and extensions are delegated to the Online Appendix.

\subsection{Setup}\label{sec:setup}

\textit{Players and Production}: There is an endogenous mass $W(a)$ of workers
with heterogeneous abilities weakly below $a$. Each worker knows his/her ability and is looking for one job. Firms post jobs openings. Sometimes a given job involves tasks that rely on high worker ability, sometimes less so. We capture this through differences in the marginal valuation $v_{T}$ of workers' ability: Each job can hire at most one
worker, and a match between job type $T$ and worker ability $a$ produces
output $v_{T}(a-c_{T}),$ where $c_{T}$ allows more complex jobs to have higher ability requirements (i.e., ``costs'') to achieve positive output.\footnote{This production function seems natural and allows for easy comparative statics later on. Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:general_production} lays out a more general production function $\phi(a,T)$ that is increasing and concave in ability. When the elasticity of $\phi_a$ is constant in $T$, our comparative statics continues to apply if $\phi$ is strictly supermodular and log-supermodular. Incentive compatibility for truth-telling continues to apply if the valuation of high types and low types are sufficiently far apart.}
For simplicity we focus on two types of jobs: $T\in \{H,L\}.$ High types value
ability more: $v_{H}>v_{L}>0,$ and their jobs might have higher requirements: $%
c_{H}\geq c_{L}\geq 0$. The probability of a high job type is $\delta _{H}$, and $\delta_L = 1-\delta_H$ is the fraction of low type jobs. The firm knows the job type when it posts it. Workers have to learn about it as we explain below. For ease of description we will associate each firm with a single job, and use the word ``job'' and ``firm'' interchangeably, though in principle firms could have multiple jobs of which some are more sensitive to ability than others.

\textit{Payoffs for market participants:} If a worker is hired for a job, the firm's profit is the output
minus the wage it pays to the worker. Therefore, a worker of type $a$ who
gets paid wage $b$ at job $T$ generates firm profits:%
\begin{equation}
\pi (a,b|T)=v_{T}(a-c_{T})-b.  \label{firm-profit}
\end{equation}%
A worker who gets hired has utility equal to his wage $b$. The payoff of jobs
and workers who remain unmatched is normalized to zero.

\textit{Market entry:} Workers can look for jobs on the platform that we analyze, but alternatively they can look for work in other marketplaces on- and off-line. Denote the expected utility they can generate in other markets be $u(a)$. Therefore, the platform attracts workers only if the resulting utility from the market interaction delivers their non-negative outside option $u(a)$,
which we assume to be increasing and strictly convex, with Inada conditions $%
u^{\prime }(0)=0$ and $u^{\prime }(a)$ unbounded. For some proofs, it will be
convenient to assume that the elasticity of $u^{\prime }$ is bounded away
from zero and infinity. We further assume that for each job type $T$ there is at least one worker type $%
a$ for which surplus of entering is strictly positive $%
(i.e.,v_{T}(a-c_{T})-u\left( a\right) >0).$ It will also be convenient to
assume that the difference in requirements between the types of firms is not
too large, and in particular that $c_{H}-c_{L}<u(c_{H})/u^{\prime }(c_{H}).$

%\bigskip

\textit{Market Interaction and Timing}: With i.i.d.\ probability $\rho$, each firm $i$ is asked to choose a message $m_{i}\in\{L,H\}$, where the binary message space is without loss of generality given that there are only two types of firms. With probability $1-\rho$ the firm cannot send a message, which we denote by $m_{i}=\emptyset$. For notational convenience let $M=\{\emptyset,L,H\}$. Each firm also generates two signals $s_{1,i},s_{2,i}\in\{L,H\}$, drawn i.i.d.\ conditional on the true firm type $T_{i}$, matching that type with probabilities $\psi_{1}$ and $\psi_{2}$ respectively (both exceeding one-half). Workers observe the first signal easily, but it is noisy; the second is obtained only after careful reading of the job description and is extremely precise ($\psi_{2}$ close to $1$). Under truthful cheap talk these signals play no role, but they let workers condition their search and bids when cheap talk is absent or uninformative. The role of the second signal — and the reason we can often take $\psi_{2}\to 1$ as a simplifying limit — is discussed in more detail in Appendix~\ref{online_app:imprecise_second_signal}.

Workers decide whether to enter. Those who enter observe the joint distribution of
messages and first signals, and choose a job announcement with desired
message and first signal $(m,s_{1})\in M\times \{L,H\}$. Since they lack
further information at this point, they choose some firm $i$ from the firms
with observable $(m,s_{1})$ at random. In a large market this randomness
leads to a Poisson distribution of workers at a given firm, with Poisson
parameter equal to the ratio of workers to firms at a given $(m,s_{1})$
combination.\footnote{%
This way of introducing search underlies most of the micro-founded work on
directed search; careful derivations that start with a finite population
and take limits are presented in \cite{peters_1991}, \cite{peters_1997}, and \cite{burdett_etal2001}, or the summary in \cite{wright_etal2021}.} The worker then prepares a bid for
this job and learns its second signal $s_{2,i}$. At that point the worker
has already sunk her preparation time and cannot choose another firm. She
submits a bid $b$ to this job that can condition on the message and both
signals, or chooses to remain unmatched. We assume that workers only submit
a bid if they believe that their bid has strictly positive probability of being
accepted.

Each firm observes all its bids and the ability of the associated workers,
hires the most profitable among these workers, and pays a wage equal to this
worker's bid. It can choose to reject all bidders.

\textit{Equilibrium Concept:} Our equilibrium concept follows the literature on large games where each individual's payoff depends on his own actions and the distribution of actions across all other players (e.g., \cite{mas1984theorem} and its applications to directed search in \cite{eeckhout2010sorting} and \cite{peters2010noncontractible}). To summarize the distribution of actions by firms, let $\mu (m,T)$ denote the equilibrium probability
that firms of type $T$ send message $m\in \{H,L\}$ conditional on being
selected to send a message. This uniquely determines the probability $\Psi
(T|m,s_{1})$ of facing firm $T$ after observation $(m,s_{1})$ whenever $m$
is played with positive probability. If $m$ is not played with positive
probability, $\Psi (.)$ is a belief that agents hold in case a firm sends
such a message off-equilibrium. Given such beliefs, Bayes' rule generates
the joint probability $\Psi (s_{2},T|m,s_{1})$ of the second signal and
type, and a conditional belief $\Psi (T|m,s_{1},s_{2})$ after observing all
signals.

The easiest way to sum up the workers' behavior is through the ratio $\Lambda(a,b_{L},b_{H}|m,s_{1})$ of workers with ability weakly above $a$ who will bid weakly above $b_{L}$ after a low second signal and above $b_{H}$ after a high second signal, per job with initial observables $(m,s_{1})$. This is also called the queue length of workers. Sometimes it is useful to focus on the marginal $\Lambda_{a}(a|m,s_{1})$ of workers with ability above $a$ per firm with observable $(m,s_{1}),$ or on the conditional $\Lambda(a,b|m,s_{1},s_{2})$ that provides the ratio of workers with ability-bid above $(a,b)$ to firms with observables $(m,s_{1},s_{2}).$

From the perspective of a firm with type $T$ and observables $(m,s_{1},s_{2})$, this generates a queue of workers bidding weakly more than $\pi$ according to
\begin{equation*}
\Gamma(\pi|m,s_{1},s_{2},T)=\int_{\{a,b|\pi(a,b|T)\geq\pi\}}d\Lambda(\cdot|m,s_{1},s_{2}).
\end{equation*}%
We denote by $\Gamma_{+}(\pi|\cdot)$ the queue of workers that bid strictly more than $\pi$, and by $\bigtriangleup(\pi|m,s_{1},s_{2},T)$ the mass of workers who offer exactly the same profit.\footnote{Formally, $\Gamma_{+}(\pi|m,s_{1},s_{2},T)=\int_{\{a,b|\pi(a,b|T)>\pi\}}d\Lambda(\cdot|m,s_{1},s_{2})$.} We will later rule out the existence of such mass points in equilibrium. The chance that no worker offers a profit weakly above $\pi$ under a Poisson distribution is $\exp(-\Gamma(\pi|\cdot))$, so the chance of having at least one worker who offers such profit is the complement. A firm of type $T$ that sends message $m$ therefore earns expected profit
\begin{equation}
\Pi (m|T)=\sum_{s_{1},s_{2}}\left[ \psi (s_{1},s_{2}|T)\int_{\pi \geq 
0,s_{1},s_{2}}\pi d(1-e^{-\Gamma (\pi |m,s_{1},s_{2},T)})\right] ,
\label{profits}
\end{equation}%
where $\psi(s_{1},s_{2}|T)$ denotes the probability of the signal pair given the firm type. 
From the perspective of a worker who applies to a job with observables $(m,s_1)$
and plans to bid $b_L$ and $b_H$ after observing a low or high second signal,
respectively, the expected utility is
\begin{equation}
V(m,s_{1},a,b_{L},b_{H})=\sum_{s_{2},T}\Psi(s_{2},T|m,s_{1})\,b_{s_{2}} \left(
  e^{-\Gamma_{+}(\pi(a,b_{s_{2}}|T)|m,s_{1},s_{2},T)} \right) .
\label{value}
\end{equation}
as long as the worker generates weakly positive profits (i.e., $\pi (a,b_{s_2}|T)\geq 0$) and there are no mass points of workers that generate the same profit (i.e., $\bigtriangleup (.|m,s_{1},s_{2},T)=0$).\footnote{
If the worker generates negative profits firms of type $T$ would not hire this worker. If there is a mass point of workers that generate exactly the same profit (i.e., $\bigtriangleup (\pi
(a,b_{s_2}|T)|m,s_{1},s_{2},T)=0$ for some $s_2$ and $T$) profits would be strictly lower than in (\ref{value}) because the firm would randomize its hiring among these equally-attractive workers. Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:analysis} gives the exact expression that captures these cases.}
The first factor is the probability of facing signal $s_{2}$ and firm type $T$;
the second is the wage the worker collects if hired; the third is the Poisson
probability that no other worker at the firm generates a strictly higher profit.
We can now define an equilibrium.

\begin{definition*}[Equilibrium Definition]
An equilibrium consists of firm behavior, worker behavior, and beliefs
$(\mu(\cdot),\Lambda(\cdot),\Psi(\cdot))$ such that
\begin{enumerate}[label={[\arabic*]}]
\item beliefs $\Psi(\cdot)$ are consistent with Bayes' rule given firm
behavior $\mu(\cdot)$;
\item firms maximize profits, so $\Pi(m|T)=\max_{m'}\Pi(m',T)$ whenever
$\mu(m,T)>0$; and
\item workers' decisions to enter and bid are optimal, so
$V(m,s_{1},a,b_{L},b_{H})\leq u(a)$ for all $(m,s_{1},a,b_{L},b_{H})$, with
equality whenever $(a,b_{L},b_{H})$ is in the support of
$\Lambda(\cdot|m,s_{1})$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition*}

The last condition ensures that workers obtain their outside option, or else the queue length is zero as no worker would want to join the platform. 
We conclude with the immediate observation that there cannot be meaningful
mass points in equilibrium, i.e., mass points at firm types to which workers assign a non-zero probability. 
Workers at the mass point have strictly positive probability that another worker that generates the same profit for the firm gets hired instead of them. 
A marginally lower wage bid (i.e., a marginally higher profit for the firm) would have ensured being hired, inducing a profitable upward jump in the hiring probability. This rules out that mass points can be optimal for workers, simplifying the notation going forward.

\begin{lemma*}
\label{no-mass-point-lemma}
There are no meaningful mass points in equilibrium, i.e.,
$\bigtriangleup(\pi|m,s_1,s_2,T)=0$ for all $\pi\geq 0$ and all
$(m,s_1,s_2,T)$ with $\Psi(s_2,T|m,s_1)>0$.
\end{lemma*}

\noindent Proof: See Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:analysis}.





\subsection{Analysis of Truthful Cheap Talk}\label{sec:truthful}

To understand this market, consider first the firms that can send a cheap-talk message. Their message space is $\{L,H\}$. As we will see, the following analysis is valid whether these firms constitute the entire market ($\rho=1$) or only part of it ($0<\rho<1$). Recall that firms chosen to send a message must declare a type. Assume for now that these firms declare their type truthfully in equilibrium. To investigate the market outcome for these firms, we impose truthful messages ($\mu(T|T)=1$) and equilibrium conditions (1) and (3). After characterizing this outcome, we additionally impose equilibrium condition (2) on firm optimality to study the conditions under which firms are willing to report truthfully. We study firms that cannot send a cheap-talk message in the next subsection.

Under the assumption that firms report truthfully, workers do not rely on signals to infer the firm type. That is, $\Psi(T|T,s_{1},s_{2})=1$ for all $s_{1}$ and $s_{2}$, and it is easy to show that there is no benefit from conditioning visit strategies or bids on the signals. In what follows we suppress reference to the signals, so that $b=b_{H}=b_{L}$ and we write $\Lambda(a,b|m)$ instead of $\Lambda(a,b,b|m,s_{1})$. We can then express the value for a worker of ability $a$ who bids $b$ at a job with message $m\in\{L,H\}$ (regardless of the signals) as
\begin{equation}
V(m,a,b)=e^{-\Gamma (\pi (a,b|m)|m)}b,
\label{value-truth}
\end{equation}%
as long as the worker generates weakly positive profits $\pi (a,b|m)\geq 0$.
Since the worker infers perfectly which firm it is facing, instead of
bidding wage $b$ we can reformulate the problem as one of leaving profit $%
\pi \geq 0$ to the firm and keeping the output minus this profit as pay. For
worker-firm pairs that have strictly positive output so that $%
v_{m}(a-c_{m})\geq \pi \geq 0$ this delivers expected utility 
\begin{equation}
W(m,a,\pi )=e^{-\Gamma (\pi |m)}\left[ v_{m}(a-c_{m})-\pi \right] .
\label{value-profit-truth}
\end{equation}%
Clearly $V(m,a,b)=W(m,a,\pi(a,b|m))$. By equilibrium condition (3), if workers with ability $a$ and bid $b$ enter at message $m$ — i.e., if $(a,b)$ is in the support of $\Lambda(\cdot|m)$ — then $b$ must maximize (\ref{value-truth}). It follows that $\pi(a,b|m)$ must be weakly positive and maximizes (\ref{value-profit-truth}).

We can therefore study the problem in which the worker's choice variable is $\pi$, chosen to maximize (\ref{value-profit-truth}). This objective is supermodular in $(a,\pi)$, indicating that it is less costly for high-ability types to offer high profits to firms. Consequently, higher-ability workers at a given message leave higher profits in equilibrium. And since there are no mass points, they leave strictly higher profits almost everywhere.

For ease of notation, let 
\begin{equation}
U(a|m):=\arg \max_{\pi }W(m,a,\pi )  \label{value-profit-truth2}
\end{equation}%
denote the equilibrium utility for a worker of type $a$ who approaches message $m$. Free entry then requires that $U(a|m)\leq u(a)$, with equality if $a$ is in the support of $\lambda(a|m)$.

We can use this together with the definition of (\ref{value-profit-truth})
to prove that at a given message $m$ the worker abilities that enter in
equilibrium form a non-empty interval $[\underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}],$ and
no single type enters with positive mass (see proof in Appendix \ref{online_app:analysis}). The
reason is that there is no mass point in terms of profits that are offered
(see earlier Lemma) and for each profit level there is a
different ability type that finds it optimal to enter.

So the task is to characterize the non-empty interval $[\underline{a}_{m},%
\bar{a}_{m}].$ Within this interval, consider a worker with ability $a$ that
leaves profit $\pi $ at message $m$ in equilibrium.\footnote{%
Formally this means that there exists $(a,b)$ in the support of $\Lambda
(a,b|m)$ such that $\pi =\pi (a,b|m).$} Then the marginal increase in
equilibrium utility with respect to ability is%
\begin{equation}
e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}v_{m}=u^{\prime }(a).  \label{first derivative}
\end{equation}%
The left-hand side follows from differentiating (\ref{value-profit-truth}) with respect to ability, where any change in profits can be neglected by the envelope theorem, and then substituting $\Lambda(a|m)$ for $\Gamma(\pi|m)$, which is valid since higher types leave higher profits. The right-hand side must hold because the equilibrium utility equals the forgone outside option. Differentiating with respect to ability leads to 
\begin{equation}
\lambda (a|m)e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}v_{m}=u^{\prime \prime }(a)
\label{second derivative}
\end{equation}%
where $\lambda (a|m)=-d\Lambda (a|m)/da$ is akin to a ``density'' of type $a$
workers queueing at this message. Dividing this by (\ref{first derivative})
yields 
\begin{equation}
\lambda (a|m)=\frac{u^{\prime \prime }(a)}{u^{\prime }(a)},
\label{equilibrium density}
\end{equation}%
which ties down the type density at any ability level. The key question
concerns the end-points of the interval. Clearly at the highest type (\ref%
{first derivative}) reduces to the end-point condition%
\begin{equation}
v_{m}=u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{m}),  \label{equilibrium highest type}
\end{equation}%
which uniquely determines $\bar{a}_{m}$. One can then recover the lowest ability type that approaches message $m$ as follows: at any candidate $\underline{a}_{m}$, the queue length $\Lambda(\underline{a}_{m}|m)=\int_{\underline{a}_{m}}^{\bar{a}_{m}}\lambda(a|m)\,da$ is uniquely pinned down by (\ref{equilibrium density}) and (\ref{equilibrium highest type}). Moreover, the lowest-ability type also leaves the lowest profits, which implies that this type leaves zero profits — if not, she could lower her bid until she leaves zero profits and would still win with the same probability. Hence
\begin{equation}
e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}v_{m}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})=u(%
\underline{a}_{m}),  \label{equilibrium lowest type}
\end{equation}%
which uniquely ties down $\underline{a}_{m}.$

Finally, we briefly consider how wage bids $b_{m}(a)$ are determined for
worker $a\in \lbrack \underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}]$. In equilibrium, the
utility of such a worker is $e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}b_{m}(a)$. We can totally
differentiate this with respect to the worker type, and set it equal to $%
u^{\prime }(a)$ which needs to hold due to free entry. Using (\ref{first
derivative}) this reduces after cancellation to the following differential
equation: 
\begin{eqnarray}
b_{m}^{\prime }(a) &=&v_{m}-b_{m}(a)\lambda (a|m)  \notag \\
&=&v_{m}-b_{m}(a)u^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a)  \label{wages}
\end{eqnarray}%
which together with the end-point condition that the lowest type extracts all
surplus:
\begin{equation}
b_{m}(\underline{a}_{m})=v_{m}[\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m}]
\label{wages boundary}
\end{equation}%
determines the wage bids uniquely. To sum up:

\begin{prop}
\label{equilibrium-characterization}\label{workers}Under truthful cheap talk, the interval of worker types $[\underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}]$ that approach message $m\in\{L,H\}$ and the queue $\Lambda(a|m)$ are fully determined by (\ref{equilibrium density})--(\ref{equilibrium lowest type}), and the wages they ask for are fully determined by (\ref{wages}) and (\ref{wages boundary}). On $[\underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}]$, the wages for such a firm with arbitrary $v_{m}$ and $c_{m}$ can be expressed relative to the wages $B(\cdot)$ of a ``normalized'' firm with valuation $v=1$ and cost $c=0$ (so that the lowest-type ability equals $0$) according to
\begin{equation}
b_{m}(a)=v_{m}[B(a)-B(\underline{a}_{m})+\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m}].
\label{bidding1}
\end{equation}
\end{prop}

\bigskip

It is worth noting that Proposition~\ref{equilibrium-characterization} fully characterizes the application and bidding behavior faced by firms that send messages, and that these results hold irrespective of whether all firms send messages ($\rho=1$) or only some fraction do ($\rho<1$). The characterization makes no reference to the number of firms that have the ability to message, nor to the behavior at firms that do not. This property of directed-search models with entry is known in the literature as ``block-recursiveness'' and arises because workers trade off entry against the number of firms present in the market: since workers can choose which firms to approach, what happens at firms that do not send messages is irrelevant for those who approach firms that do. We now turn to those firms.



\subsection{Analysis Without Cheap Talk}

The firms that cannot send a message could
be in a different market where cheap-talk is impossible (i.e., $\rho =0)$,
or they could be in the same market as the firms that can send messages
(i.e., $0<\rho <1).$ Workers can only rely on the signals about these firms
when they decide which firm to approach and which wage bid to place. For
ease of analysis, we assume that the second signal is perfectly revealing ($\psi_{2}=1$), which lets us leverage the derivations developed under truthful cheap talk. This assumption can be relaxed: by continuity, the insights of this section hold when the second signal is sufficiently but not perfectly precise, and we discuss the role of this assumption in more detail in Appendix~\ref{online_app:imprecise_second_signal}.

Now consider workers who see signal $s_{1}$ and believe that the fraction of
low firms at this signal is $\Psi (L|s_{1})$ where we suppressed the message
because it is absent ($m=\emptyset ).$ The fraction with high type is
evidently the complement. Note that these fractions are exogenously given;
for example, $\Psi (L|L)=\psi _{1}\delta _{L}/(\psi _{1}\delta _{L}+(1-\psi
_{1})\delta _{H}),$ and do not depend on the equilibrium interaction.

It turns out that workers approach these firms as if facing firms whose valuation is the average of $v_{L}$ and $v_{H}$ weighted by the workers' beliefs. That is, workers approach firms as if their productivity were averaged according to
\begin{equation*}
v_{\Psi}=\Psi v_{L}+(1-\Psi)v_{H},
\end{equation*}%
where $\Psi=\Psi(L|s_{1})$ represents the belief that the firm is of low type given its signal. Workers also approach firms as if the cost were averaged, though the exact expression requires some re-arranging. Writing production as $v(a-c)=va-vc$, in this setting workers expect the average of $vc$ as the second term. To express production in expectation as $v_{\Psi}(a-c_{\Psi})$, we therefore define
\begin{equation*}
c_{\Psi}=\frac{\Psi v_{L}c_{L}+(1-\Psi)v_{H}c_{H}}{\Psi v_{L}+(1-\Psi)v_{H}},
\end{equation*}%
where $c_{\Psi}$ is in $[c_{L},c_{H}]$ and increases monotonically from $c_{L}$ to $c_{H}$ as $\Psi$ decreases from one to zero. Workers with types in $[\underline{a}_{\Psi},\bar{a}_{\Psi}]$ then approach such firms, and their bids are conditional on the second signal, which reveals whether the firm is of low or high type. Bids are still driven by (\ref{bidding1}), except that the lowest ability type who extracts all surplus is determined by the first signal rather than the second. We therefore have:

\begin{prop}
\label{equilibrium-notalk}Firms with $m=\emptyset $ and signal $s_{1}$
induce belief $\Psi =\Psi (L|s_{1}).$ The interval of worker types $[%
\underline{a}_{s_{1}},\bar{a}_{s_{1}}]$ that approach them and the queue $%
\Lambda (a|s_{1})$ are fully determined by $(\ref{equilibrium density})-(\ref%
{equilibrium lowest type})$ when using for the valuation the average given
by $v_{\Psi }$ and for the cost one uses $c_{\Psi }.$ After the second
signal $s_{2}$ these workers bid according to%
\begin{equation}
b_{s_{2}}(a)=v_{s_{2}}[B(a)-B(\underline{a}_{s_{1}})+\underline{a}%
_{s_{1}}-c_{s_{2}}].  \label{wages-general}
\end{equation}
\end{prop}

Proof idea: The key challenge is to prove that workers who approach a firm will actually always bid there, which is not obvious: they approach under uncertainty and learn additional information from the second signal. Under our assumptions, this turns out to hold. The averaging then obtains exactly because the second signal is so precise, and it does not obtain otherwise, as we elaborate in the discussion section. For the full proof see Appendix~\ref{online_app:analysis}.$\square$\bigskip

Regarding the wage equation, it is worth noting that the wage conditions on the true valuation and cost $(v_{s_{2}},c_{s_{2}})$ revealed through the second signal, but the lowest type $\underline{a}_{\Psi}$ who extracts all surplus remains the one that expects average quality. This is because the decision of which firms to study in detail is made when the worker has less information.


\subsection{Comparing Firms With and Without Messages}\label{sec:comparing}

To understand the empirical implications of the results, we now compare firms. For this, we assume that the empiricist has elicited the true firm type for both the firms that send messages and those that do not. There are many ways to achieve this, and in reality the message can take many forms. For example, one could ask firms a fictitious question: Do you prefer (``rookie'', \$) or (``expert'', \$\$\$)? Firms might interpret this as a choice between two combinations of ability and wage bid, $(a_{1},b_{1})$ or $(a_{2},b_{2})$ with $a_{1}<a_{2}$ and $b_{1}<b_{2}$. If there are no consequences to the question, firms might simply pick the option that would generate more profit; if two firms choose differently, then the $L$ type chooses the first and the $H$ type the second, so the answer to the question reveals the type. In the empirical implementation we carefully discuss how we make firms comparable.

To compare firms, it is useful to study the comparative statics on the types of workers who approach these firms and on their bidding. The worker types are governed by the valuation $v$ and cost $c$, which equal the true type for firms with truthful messages and equal the average (interim) type after workers have observed the first signal for firms without messages.

\begin{corollary}
\label{corollary}Consider a firm with interim expected valuation $v$ and cost 
$c.$

\begin{itemize}
\item The maximal worker type $\bar{a}$ strictly increases in valuation $v$
(but does not depend on $c$).

\item The minimal worker type $\underline{a}$ strictly increases in the
threshold $c$ (but does not depend on $v$).

\item For interior worker types in $(\underline{a}, \bar{a})$ the density $\lambda (a|.)$ is independent of $v$ or $c$.
\end{itemize}
\end{corollary}

Proof: See Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:analysis}.

We can directly apply these comparative statics, noting that $v$ and $c$ are lowest for firms with a truthful low message, higher for firms without a message but with a low first signal, even higher for firms without a message but with a high first signal, and highest for firms with a truthful high message. Both the lower and the upper bound of the interval $[\underline{a},\bar{a}]$ of workers who approach these firms therefore increase in this order. Whenever these intervals overlap across firms with different messages or signals, the worker types in the overlap generate the same queue at each firm according to (\ref{equilibrium density}). This means workers can implement this queue length by choosing among these firms at random. An individual worker might not choose at random, but the aggregate outcome is indistinguishable from one in which they do.

Figure \ref{messages} illustrates graphically how workers choose between
firms. It is easiest to explain by thinking about two separate markets.
Panel A illustrates a market where firms can send messages, and do so
truthfully. In this market, the lowest type workers only approach low
message firms because the ability threshold $c_{H}$ at high firms is too
high. Workers with intermediate ability choose ``as if'' at random between
firms at both messages. Here $a^{\prime }=\bar{a}_{m=L}$ and $a^{\prime
\prime }=\underline{a}_{m=H},$ so that the interval between indicates the
overlap between $[\underline{a}_{m=L},\bar{a}_{m=L}]$ and $[\underline{a}%
_{m=H},\bar{a}_{m=H}]$. High worker types again are fully directed, this
time towards high message firms which have sufficiently high valuation to
warrant their entry cost. Whether high or low-message firms attract more
applicants overall depends on parameters, but it is clear that the
distribution of worker abilities at high message firms first order
stochastically dominates that at low message firms.

Panel B considers a market where firms cannot send messages and workers rely on the first signal to determine where to go. At the high signal, there are mostly high-type firms, but some low-type firms also emit this signal. Similarly, at the low signal there are mostly low-type firms, but some high-type firms also emit it. Between high- and low-signal firms, workers choose in a similar fashion as in Panel A. But relative to Panel A, in Panel B the lowest and highest worker types stay out of the market, as the average cost is too high at the low end and the average valuation is too low at the high end. Moreover, the range of worker types who act as if they choose randomly expands at both ends. So in Panel B the types that are directed form a strict subset of the types in Panel A, while the types that visit at random form a strict superset.

As mentioned earlier, the same insights carry over even if firms without messages interact in the same market as firms with messages. Note that workers whose ability types lead them to approach multiple messages/signals continue to do so at random, even though this is not visible from Figure~\ref{messages}. For example, workers slightly above $a'$ apply to high-message firms as well as to no-message firms with either a low or high first signal. Their queue length is the same at all of them, so they can implement this queue by choosing between them at random. They do not apply to low-message firms, however.

If one conditions on high firms in the right picture, most of them get
applicants according to the high signal but some get applicants according to
the low signal. Both are first order stochastically dominated by the
distribution of worker abilities that high message firms obtain. For low
type firms the opposite holds.

What is the cost of attracting higher worker types? Since they produce more output, they are generally more attractive, and without a counteracting force all firms that can send messages might pretend to be high types (we discuss the exact incentives for truth-telling at the end of this section). It turns out that wage demands rise — not only because workers with higher ability tend to ask for higher wages, but, more importantly, because the same worker tends to ask for more. This is shown in the following comparative statics.

\begin{corollary}
\label{corollary 2}Consider a firm with type $T$ and associated valuation $v_{T}$ and cost $c_{T}$ that attracts workers with abilities in $[\underline{a},\bar{a}]$. For a given worker with ability $a$, her wage bid at this firm increases in $v$, decreases in $c$, and increases in $\underline{a}$.
\end{corollary}

Proof: From (\ref{wages-general}), the equilibrium bid at a worker of ability $a$ is $b_{s_{2}}(a)=v_{s_{2}}[B(a)-B(\underline{a})+\underline{a}-c_{s_{2}}]$, where we have written $\underline{a}$ for $\underline{a}_{s_{1}}$. The wage bid is strictly increasing in $v_{s_{2}}$ and strictly decreasing in $c_{s_{2}}$ by inspection, since the bracketed term is positive (the worker is hired and generates non-negative profits only if $a\geq\underline{a}$ and $\underline{a}\geq c_{s_{2}}$). For the dependence on $\underline{a}$, differentiating with respect to $\underline{a}$ gives
\[
\frac{\partial b_{s_{2}}(a)}{\partial \underline{a}} = v_{s_{2}}\bigl[1 - B'(\underline{a})\bigr].
\]
The wage ODE (\ref{wages}) with the boundary condition (\ref{wages boundary}) implies $B'(\underline{a}) = 1 - B(\underline{a})\cdot u''(\underline{a})/u'(\underline{a})$. Since $B(\underline{a})\geq 0$ and $u'',u'>0$, we have $B'(\underline{a}) < 1$, so $\partial b_{s_{2}}(a)/\partial \underline{a} > 0$.$\square$

\bigskip

For a given firm type, the identity of the lowest worker type matters. This worker type extracts all surplus, and workers slightly above the lowest type still extract nearly all surplus. Firms therefore retain surplus — and thus face low wages — only for workers sufficiently far above the lowest worker, with wages falling in this distance. In our context this means that a given firm faces lower wages the lower $\underline{a}_{s_{1}}$. This is lowest for low-message firms, higher for low-signal firms, higher still for high-signal firms, and highest for high-message firms. So, comparing a high-type firm that sends a truthful message with a high-type firm that cannot send a message, for any given worker the wage demand is slightly lower at the no-message firm in the event of a high first signal, and substantially lower in the event of a low first signal. Conversely, comparing a low-type firm that can send a truthful message with a low-type firm that cannot, for any given worker the wage demand is slightly higher at the no-message firm in the event of a low first signal, and substantially higher in the event of a high first signal. Thus wages rise at high-type firms when they can send a message and fall at low-type firms.

The next proposition collects these insights, and provides the predictions which will guide our investigation in the empirical exercise which follows:

\begin{prop}
\label{comparison1} The following compares firms that engage in truthful
cheap-talk with otherwise identical counterparts that cannot.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Ability sorting: Truthful cheap talk induces more assortative matching, in the sense that the distribution of ability for a randomly selected applicant increases (decreases) in first-order stochastic dominance for high-type (low-type) firms.

\item Number of applications: In general, the effect on the number of applications is theoretically ambiguous. For given $v_{L},c_{L},c_{H}$, and $u(\cdot)$, if $v_{H}$ is sufficiently large then truthful cheap talk reduces applications at low-type firms relative to their ``no-message'' counterparts, and across all firms, applications are lower for firms with messages than for firms without. When $v_{H}$ is close to $v_{L}$, cheap talk instead increases the number of applications at low-message firms, without clear implications across all firms with messages relative to those without.

\item Wage bidding: Any given worker type bids less at low-type firms under truthful cheap talk than in its absence. The average wage demand at such firms and the average wage of the selected worker fall under truthful cheap talk. The opposite holds for high-type firms.

\item Efficiency: Social surplus per firm is higher at firms that send truthful messages than at their counterparts that cannot send messages.

\item Market-wide rollout: Incentives to use different messages do not depend on the fraction of firms that use cheap talk, since equilibrium wage bids and queue lengths conditional on $(m,s_{1},s_{2})$ do not depend on the fraction $\rho$ of firms that can message.
\end{enumerate}
\end{prop}

Proof: Parts 1 and 3 follow directly from Corollaries~\ref{corollary} and~\ref{corollary 2}. For parts 2 and 4 see Appendix~\ref{online_app:analysis}. Intuitively, the effect on the number of applicants is ambiguous because cheap talk shifts both the upper and lower bounds of the types that approach a firm, and which shift is larger depends on parameters. For the surplus result, note that the equilibrium implements the planner's allocation given the information frictions, but the planner can assign worker types in a more targeted way when he has more information about the firm type. Part 5 follows directly from the ``block-recursivity'' property established in Subsection~\ref{sec:truthful}.$\square$

\subsection{Truth-telling}\label{sec:truth-telling}

Throughout Subsections~\ref{sec:truthful}--\ref{sec:comparing} we assumed that firms report their type truthfully through their messages, imposing only equilibrium conditions [1] and [3]. We now additionally impose condition [2] on firm optimality to investigate whether truth-telling is indeed incentive compatible and therefore an equilibrium.

The basic trade-off is the following. A low-type firm that deviates from the candidate equilibrium and sends message $H$ is taken for a high-type firm, because in equilibrium only high-type firms send that message on the equilibrium path. Such a deviant firm would attract better workers, but each of these workers demands a higher wage. The opposite happens for a high-type firm that pretends to be a low-type firm. Since high-type firms value worker ability more and low-type firms value paying a low wage more, sufficient differences in valuations imply the existence of an equilibrium with truthful cheap talk. As in many cheap-talk games, however, if firm types are very similar, full type revelation generically fails.

\begin{prop}
\label{prop:incentive_compat} For given $u(a)$, $v_{L}$, and $c_{H}$, truth-telling is incentive compatible if $v_{H}$ is sufficiently large. For $c_{H}=c_{L}=0$, when $v_{H}$ approaches $v_{L}$, incentive compatibility for truth-telling generically fails.
\end{prop}

\noindent Proof: See Appendix~\ref{online_app:analysis}.$\square$\bigskip

Proposition~\ref{prop:incentive_compat} shows that incentives for truth-telling are present in some situations, but they are not guaranteed in all situations. Beyond sufficient differences in firm types, incentives for truth-telling also rely on the presence of the second signal; we discuss the role of the second signal for incentive compatibility in Appendix~\ref{online_app:imprecise_second_signal}, and the extension to a broader class of production functions in Appendix~\ref{online_app:robustness}. Reassuringly, we do not have to speculate on truth-telling: the empirical exercise directly investigates proxies for it, and part 2 of Proposition~\ref{comparison1} delivers testable implications that distinguish truth-telling from alternatives by exploiting differences in valuations between high- and low-type firms.

Moreover, our main comparison insights do not rely on perfect type revelation; they apply even to partially revealing equilibria:

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:partially_revealing}For any partially revealing equilibrium in which messages carry some information (i.e., $\Psi(T|L,s_{1}) \neq \Psi(T|H,s_{1})$), the comparison of firms with and without messages is characterized by parts (1)--(5) of Proposition~\ref{comparison1}.
\end{corollary}

\noindent Proof: Parts 1--3 of Proposition~\ref{comparison1} rest on comparative statics that apply just as well to averaged valuations and costs, so they continue to hold — see Appendix~\ref{online_app:partially_revealing_eq}. Part 4 remains valid because workers continue to direct their search and equate market payoffs to their outside option, so the efficiency argument proceeds in analogous steps as in the fully revealing case. Part 5 likewise carries over because block-recursivity only relies on workers directing their search and equating their market payoff to their outside option at each $(m,s_{1})$, which does not depend on whether messages fully or partially reveal type.$\square$\bigskip

Any equilibrium in which messages transmit some information therefore shares the same qualitative empirical implications as the fully revealing equilibrium discussed in Proposition~\ref{comparison1}. This arises because these implications depend primarily on comparative statics involving averaged valuations and costs.


\section{Empirical Context} \label{sec:empirical_context}

The empirical setting for our analysis is a large online labor market.
In these markets, employers hire workers to perform tasks that can be done remotely.
Markets differ in their scope and focus, but common services provided by the platform include soliciting and promulgating job openings, hosting user profile pages, processing payments, arbitrating disputes, certifying worker skills, and maintaining a reputation system \citep{Horton2010, horton2015reputation}.

In the online labor market we use as our empirical setting, would-be employers write job descriptions, self-categorize the nature of the work and required skills, and then post the job openings to the platform website.
Job openings are learned about by workers via electronic searches or email notifications.
Employers can also search worker ``profiles'' and invite workers to apply for their openings \citep{horton2017effects}.
Worker ``profiles''  are similar to resumes, containing the details of past jobs completed by the worker, education history, skills, and so on.
For both workers and employers, some of the information available to the other side of the market is ``hard'' in the sense that it is verified by the platform.
Examples of verified, public information include hours-worked, hourly wage rates, total earnings, and feedback ratings from past trading partners.

If a worker chooses to apply to a particular job opening, they submit an application, which includes a wage bid (for hourly jobs) or a total project bid (for fixed-price jobs) and a cover letter.
In our analysis, we only make use of hourly job openings, as the preference revelation opportunity was only available for hourly job openings. 

After a worker submits an application, the employer can choose to interview the applicant.
They can also hire an applicant at the terms proposed in the application, or make a counteroffer, which the worker can counter, and so on.
The process is not an auction and neither the employer nor the worker are bound to accept any offer. 
Despite the possibility of back-and-forth bargaining, it is fairly rare, with about 90\% of hired workers being hired at the wage they initially proposed \citep{barach2016info}.
In this market, employers typically collect a more-or-less complete pool of applicants and then select a subset to interview and ultimately hire (which also seems to characterize the process in conventional markets \citep{davis2021}).

To work on hourly contracts, workers must install custom tracking software on their computers. 
The tracking software essentially serves as a digital punch clock.
The software records not only the time spent working (to the second), but also the count of keystrokes and mouse movements.
The software also captures an image of the worker's computer screen at random intervals.
All of this captured data is sent to the platform's servers and then made available to the employer for inspection, in real time.
These features give employers tools to precisely monitor hours-worked, and to an extent, effort.
As employers can end contracts at will, the employer can be thought of as the party choosing hours-worked.

The marketplace we study is not the only market for online work, and so it is important to keep in mind the ``market'' versus ``marketplace'' distinction made by \cite{roth2018}.
Relatedly, a concern with treating job openings as our primary unit of analysis is that every job opening we see on the platform could be simultaneously posted on several other online labor market sites and in the conventional market.
However, survey evidence suggests that online and offline hiring are only very weak substitutes and that multi-homing of job openings is relatively rare.
When asked what they would have done with their most recent project if the platform were not available, only 15\% of employers responded that they would have made a local hire.
Online employers report that they are generally deciding among (a) getting the work done online, (b) doing the work themselves, and (c) not having the work done at all.
The survey also found that 83\% of employers said that they listed their last job opening only on the platform in question.

The stylized fact of these information disclosures is that they redirect buyers to ``better'' sellers, and, in the shadow of this effect, improve seller quality.\footnote{
  As an example of how this works in another online market, \cite{lewis2011} shows that on eBay, the revelation of information about quality (through descriptions and prices) and the contracts created by these disclosures largely overcome the adverse selection problem.

In the two-cell ``explicit arm,'' employers knew for certain, \emph{ex ante}, whether their tier choice would be revealed.
We use an indicator variable, $\textsc{ShownPref}$, to indicate whether preferences were revealed. 
Because the value of $\textsc{ShownPref}$ was known by employers \emph{ex ante} in the explicit arm, tier choice cannot be considered exogenous: 
an employer might claim ``high'' preferences when they know the choice will not be shown, but ``medium'' when they know the choice will be shown.
This conditioning is not a concern in our other experimental arm, the two-cell  ``ambiguous arm,'' in which employers were told that their choice \emph{might} be shown to job-seekers.
In this arm, employers were then randomized to either have their choice revealed or not. 
For these employers, tier choice can be regarded as exogenous, as it is chosen before $\textsc{ShownPref}$ is determined.
If the employer's preferences were to be revealed, their job opening was labeled with the employer's vertical preference in the interface shown to workers.
The labeling was prominently displayed to make it salient to applying workers.\footnote{
  In the ambiguous arm, among those employers shown preferences, employers were further split to have a notice about whether the worker was able to condition upon their signal.
  The idea motivating this treatment was that employers might infer that bids were more shaded up/down if they knew the worker knew the signal.
  However, we find no evidence this was the case, and so for simplicity, we pool these observations together, ignoring this feature of the design.
  As it is, it appeared to have no effect on any outcome it could have affected. 
}
Randomization was effective---see Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:balance}. 

It is important to note that with our experimental design, workers could simultaneously see and interact with job openings by employers in different cells. 
As such, the SUTVA condition is inherently---and intentionally---violated.
This kind of violation is a typical concern in marketplace experiments \citep{blake2014marketplace}.
However, we \emph{want} ``interference'' both in our experiment and in equilibrium, as a goal of the signaling opportunity is to induce workers to sort, by applying to some job openings and not applying to others.
This feature of our experimental design does require care when generalizing the results to a market equilibrium.

Employers can and do post multiple job openings, though they are not allowed to have multiple listings for the same position. 
During the five month experimental period, all subsequent job postings received the same treatment assignment as the original posting, to prevent employer ``hunting'' for a better cell.
This feature of our data can potentially give us more statistical power, though as experimental group assignment could affect the probability an employer posts a follow-on opening---or the attributes of that opening---we generally restrict our analysis to the first job opening by an employer after the start of the experiment.  
However, when assessing the effects of the signaling feature on match outcomes, we will use all the job openings to gain more statistical power.

\section{Results} \label{sec:results}

\subsection{Informative Messages} \label{sec:results_existence} % 4.1
At the end of the theory section we showed that truthful cheap talk can be sustained in equilibrium in some circumstances, but that this is not always possible (see Proposition \ref{prop:incentive_compat}). Here we investigate whether truthful cheap talk seems to be present in this market. We exploit that employers with \textsc{ShownPref}$=0$ in the explicit arm of the experiment have no strategic incentive since they know that their choice is not shown to potential applicants, and we assume they tell their true preferences. We will see that these employers clearly do make conscious choices as their responses display substantial variation across different types of jobs, in line with intuition. We compare their distribution of choices with those employers where \textsc{ShownPref}$=1$ in the explicit arm of the experiment, so that a strategic motive is present as they know that their choices will be shown to the other side of the market. If they are not telling the truth, their distribution should be tilted relative to the previous group.

As such, the ambiguous and explicit arms can be safely pooled. 

\subsection{Sorting} \label{sec:results_sorting} %4.2

The first implication of Proposition~\ref{comparison1} in our theory is that applicant ability should increase at high tier job openings and decrease at low tier job openings when messages are revealed. The same should hold for actual hires. We now empirically investigate how message revelation affected applicant pool composition, when prior experience is used as a proxy for experience.
For this analysis, we use the two-cell ambiguous arm of the experiment.
Recall that in the ambiguous arm, the tier was chosen \emph{ex ante} by the employer, without knowing whether it would be revealed to job-seekers.
As such, differences in the applicant pool composition are causally attributable to revelation.

To measure changes in the applicant pool composition, we estimate the \emph{application level} regression 
\begin{align} \label{eq:app_reg}
 \log y_{ij} = \sum_k \beta^s_k \cdot \textsc{Tier}_{kj} + \epsilon_j \big| s = \textsc{ShownPref}_{j}, 
\end{align}
where $y_{ij}$ is some outcome of interest for worker $i$ applying to job opening $j$, $\textsc{Tier}_{kj}$ is an indicator for whether employer $j$ selected signal tier $k$ and $\textsc{ShownPref}_j$ is an indicator for the treatment status of employer $j$. 
We estimate this model separately for $\textsc{ShownPref} = 1$ and ${\textsc{ShownPref} = 0}$, giving coefficients $\beta^1_k$ and $\beta^0_k$ for these two samples, respectively.
In both regressions,  we use weighted least squares, weighting each observation by the inverse of the total number of applicants to the associated job opening.
This weighting ensures that all job openings count equally towards the point estimate. 
We cluster standard errors by the job opening. 

The collection of point estimates of $\beta^s_k$ from (\ref{eq:app_reg}) are plotted in Figure~\ref{fig:compare_experience}, illustrating the sorting of job-seekers with and without access to the message.
The left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:compare_experience} plots both sets of $\hat{\beta}^s_k$ coefficients from (\ref{eq:app_reg}) where the outcome is the applicant's log total prior earnings at the time of application.
Workers with no experience at the time of application are dropped from the sample. 
For each of the three tiers, the difference between the two coefficients for the two regressions i.e., $\hat{\beta}_k^1 - \hat{\beta}^0_k$, is labeled, with the standard errors reported below the point estimate.\footnote{
  The standard error for the difference is calculated directly from the point estimates for the two tiers, without considering the covariance, which should be mechanically zero because of the randomization of $\textsc{ShownPref}$.
}
This difference is the effect of message revelation on applicant pool composition.

If we look at the $\textsc{ShownPref} = 1$ coefficients, $\hat{\beta}^1_k$, we can see that revelation increases sorting in the expected directions according to Proposition~\ref{comparison1}: revealing the message increases applicant quality---proxied by prior earnings~\citep{pallais2010inefficient}---at firms in the high tier and decreases it for those selecting the low tier.

%% \subsection{Worker sorting alters the composition of applicants, but low-type workers sort less} \label{sec:applicant_pool_quantiles}

Figure~\ref{fig:compare_experience} shows the average effects of message-revelation on applicant ability. This is much weaker than the statement in Proposition \ref{comparison1}, which claims that ability should change in the sense of first order stochastic dominance. 
That is, if one measures the $n$-th decile of the ability of applicants at a given opening, information revelation should move ability in the same direction no matter which decile we consider. So for high jobs the median ability (5th decile) should weakly increase when messages are revealed, but also the 10th percentile or the 90th percentile of applicant ability. 

A deeper theoretical prediction (Corollary \ref{corollary} in Online Appendix \ref{online_app:analysis}) is that differences in the applicant pool should primarily be driven by high-ability workers when valuation differences dominate and by low-ability workers if cost differences are key. 
As our theoretical context in the previous subsection emphasized valuation differences, we expect significant sorting at higher quantiles of the ability distribution. 
We show that this is indeed the case in Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:empirical_analysis}, where we present empirical evidence based on quantiles of applicant experience, our proxy for ability.


We have studied the application and bidding behavior of workers, but the final hiring decision lies with employers.
In the model, changes in the application pool composition are passed through into hires.
However, in the experiment, it is possible that employer selection could ``undo'' any changes in the applicant pool composition. 

To test whether revelation affects the characteristics of hired workers, we estimate (\ref{eq:app_reg}), but with the sample restricted to hired workers.
Now we return to the right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:compare_experience}, which showed how the composition of the applicant pool changed with revelation.
In this panel, labeled ``Outcome: Hired worker prior earnings''  the outcome is still the job-seeker's log cumulative earnings at the time of application, but the sample is restricted to hired applicants.\footnote{
As before, standard errors are clustered at the level of the job opening.
Observations are weighted by the inverse of the number of workers who were hired for that opening, so as to count all job openings equally (though the vast majority of employers hired only one).}
Note that we are not considering whether revelation affected the probability a match was formed---we will return to this question later, but to preview results, there is no strong evidence of a change in match formation probability. 

In the right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:compare_experience}, we can see that although there is the same separation between the tiers when preferences are not shown, hired workers are---compared to applicants---systematically more experienced.
For example, in the high tier, the prior cumulative earnings of applicants is about $\exp(8) \approx \$3,000$.
In contrast, for hired workers, prior experience is closer to $\exp(8.6) \approx \$5,400$.

Employers that had their message revealed hired workers that were more like the kinds of workers they stated they were interested in. 
In the low tier, we can see that signal revelation caused hired workers to have \EffectEarningsExperienceLowTierHiredRegression{}\% lower cumulative prior hourly earnings.
In the other tiers, the effects of revelation are positive and broadly similar in magnitude to what was observed for the change in the applicant pool composition but the point estimates are quite imprecise, due to the much smaller samples when restricted to hires. 

\subsection{Number of Job Applications Per Opening, by Message} %% 4.3

Part 2 of Proposition~\ref{comparison1} established theoretically that cheap talk shifts applications relatively more towards high type firms away from low type firms if the main difference between firms is their valuations. The opposite effect arises if the main differences is in the minimum ability (i.e., the cost) needed to produce positive output. Opposite effects can arise for high type firms. To test these predictions we estimate
\begin{align} \label{eq:appsi}
  \log A_{j} & = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \textsc{ShownPref}_j + \beta_2 \textsc{MedTier}_j + \beta_3 \textsc{HighTier}_j  + \nonumber\\  
           & \beta_4 \left(\textsc{MedTier}_j \times \textsc{ShownPref}_j \right) + \nonumber \\  
           & \beta_5 \left(\textsc{HighTier}_j \times \textsc{ShownPref}_j\right) + \epsilon,
\end{align}
where $A_j$ is the number of applications received by opening $j$, and $\textsc{MedTier}_j$ and $\textsc{HighTier}_j$ are indicators for the medium and high tier employers, respectively.
The low tier is the omitted category.

Figure~\ref{fig:app_counts} plots tier means from (\ref{eq:appsi}) for both arms along with 95\% confidence intervals.\footnote{
For both arms, the samples are restricted to only those job openings receiving at least one applicant.
This restriction removes about 1\% of job openings.}$^,$\footnote{There is no evidence that the fraction of openings differs by $\textsc{ShownPref}$ status. Sometimes job openings receive no applicants because the employer removes the job post shortly after posting. As this could be affected in principle by the experimental group assignment, we make no attempt to drop these openings from our sample, with the exception of removing them for this specific purpose. 
}

However, the most likely interpretation of the data is that revelation of the message had a modest negative effect on applications sent overall.


\subsection{Wages} %% 4.4

We now explore how message revelation affected the wage bidding of applicants.
Theoretically, part 3 of Proposition \ref{comparison1} establishes that message revelation increases wage bids relative to what workers would usually bid without message revelation at high type firms, and the opposite at low type firms. 

Empirically we first estimate (\ref{eq:app_reg}) but with the log wage bid as the outcome.

high type firms get higher bids and low type firms get lower bids. 
And as before, revelation of the tier intensified the effect:
revelation caused wage bids to be \EffectWageBidAppHighTierRegression{}\% higher in the high tier, \EffectWageBidAppMedTierRegression{}\% higher in the medium tier and \EffectWageBidAppLowTierRegression{}\% lower in the low tier.

The empirical analysis of wages so far confounds two effects, though: Revealing messages changes the types of workers that a firm attracts, and the bids that these workers demand. Recall Figure~\ref{fig:compare_experience}):
high tier employers who have their message revealed attract and hire more experienced applicants. Since more experienced workers tend to ask for higher pay, this alone could explain the wage effect. But composition does not have to be the only explanation: workers could also directly condition their bid on perceived employer willingness-to-pay.
This is the prediction of Proposition~\ref{comparison1}, bullet 3. 

One way to disentangle the two effects on wage bidding---composition and conditional bidding---is to look at changes in the applicant {\em profile rate} (i.e., the rate declared on their profile) and compare it to changes in the wage bid.
The profile rate is not likely to be conditioned on the job opening, whereas the wage bid can be conditioned on the specific features of the job opening, including the employer's tier choice, if available. 
The profile rate is set by the worker at his or her desired level, but it tends to closely follow a worker's typical hourly wage bid.
This correlation is due in part to employers consider the profile rate when recruiting, and so workers have an incentive to keep it ``honest.''

Using the log profile rate as the outcome in the second panel from the right of Figure~\ref{fig:compare_wb_to_pr}---labeled ``Outcome: Applicant profile rate''---we see the same sorting pattern and revelation effect as we have for all outcomes.
However, the message revelation effects are much smaller for the profile rate than they were for the wage bid: 
revelation raised the profile rates of applicants to high tier openings by \EffectProfileRateAppHighTierRegression{}\%, raised them by \EffectProfileRateAppMedTierRegression{}\% to medium tier openings, and lowered them by \EffectProfileRateAppLowTierRegression{}\% for low tier openings.
Note that these low and high tier revelation effects for the wage bid are about twice as large in magnitude compared to the profile rates. 
Finding smaller effects for profile rates than for wage bids is suggestive that workers are marking up or marking down their wage bids directly in response to the tier choice.\footnote{
  As a direct measure of wage bid conditioning, we can use as an outcome the ``markup'' in the wage bid, or the difference between the wage bid and profile rate, divided by the profile rate.
  See Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:markup} for an analysis showing higher markups in response to revelation of a high-type signal and lower markups in response to revelation of a low-type signal.
}

%\subsection{Within-worker approach for detecting wage bid conditioning}
We can directly test for wage bid conditioning by exploiting the fact that workers on the platform apply to multiple job openings.
To do this, we estimate the application-level regression  
\begin{align} \label{eq:bidding}
\log w_{ij} & = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \textsc{ShownPref}_j + \beta_2 \textsc{MedTier}_j + \beta_3 \textsc{HighTier}_j  + \nonumber\\  
           & \beta_4 \left(\textsc{MedTier}_j \times \textsc{ShownPref}_j \right) + \nonumber \\  
           & \beta_5 \left(\textsc{HighTier}_j \times \textsc{ShownPref}_j\right) + \epsilon.  
\end{align}
where $\alpha_i$ is a worker-specific fixed effect. 
This ``within'' estimator allows us to compare the decision-making of workers that applied to job openings with the same tier, but that differed in $\textsc{ShownPref}$, as well as jobs that differed in their tier.

It is easier to appreciate the interaction of the tier and \textsc{ShownPref} by plotting the mean predicted values from the estimate of (\ref{eq:bidding}) when the outcome is the log wage bid, which we do in Figure~\ref{fig:bidding_effect}, in the left panel. 
The sample consists of all applications to job openings in the ambiguous arm of the experiment. 
We can see that even when workers cannot observe the tier choice, \mbox{$\textsc{ShownPref} = 0$}, they still ``pick up'' some of the employer's vertical preference, 
bidding more when facing a higher tier employer.
The coefficient on \textsc{MedTier} implies workers increase their wage bids by \BidIncreaseMedWorkerFE{}\%, and the coefficient on \textsc{HighTier} implies a \BidIncreaseHighWorkerFE{}\% increase in the wage bid.
Note again these results are from a regression with a worker-specific fixed effect, and so these changes in bids are not due to changes in composition.

When the message is revealed, workers adjust their wage bids much more strongly.
They bid \BidChangeLowRevealedWorkerFE{}\% less when $\textsc{ShownPref = 1}$ when they know it is a low tier opening; if the worker learns it is a high tier job opening, they bid an additional \BidChangeHighRevealedWorkerFE{}\% more, on top of the \BidIncreaseHighWorkerFE{}\% increase noted above.

If our within-worker approach removes worker composition effects, neither the tier nor the revelation of the tier should matter much for outcomes that are quasi-fixed attributes of the applicant.
In the middle panel of Figure~\ref{fig:bidding_effect} the outcome is the applicant's log profile rate. 
In the rightmost panel the outcome is the worker's cumulative hours-worked on the platform at the time of application, if any (note the smaller sample).
For both of these quasi-fixed worker attributes, experience and profile rates are slightly increasing in the vertical preference tier, but do not seem to depend strongly on \textsc{ShownPref}. 
This slight increase over tiers reflects that over the 5 month course of the experiment, workers gain experience and shift their applications to more demanding job openings ``organically'' and increase their profile rates.
However, the effect sizes are only 1/10th of the size of the effects on the wage bid.
This implies our within-worker approach effectively nets out composition effects. 

The pattern of wage bidding results are consistent with workers directly conditioning on employer willingness to pay.
However, there are alternatives explanations.
For example, perhaps workers perceive the message as indicating the worker's likely costs.
However, we view these alternative explanations as implausible. 
We show in Online Appendix~\ref{online_appendix:compensating_differentials}, there is no evidence that high tier employers were harsher reviewers when giving feedback even when preferences were not revealed.
This suggests that high tier employers did not have costlier expectations for workers that would require a compensating differential. 
It is also unlikely that some firms are viewed as more attractive.
The relatively impersonal nature of these online interactions, along with their short-duration and lack of brand-name firms all make it unlikely workers have strong non-monetary preference over firms \emph{\`{a} la} \cite{sorkin2018ranking}. 
More generally, it is hard to square the sorting and bidding effects with compensating differential argument. 
For example, workers should submit \emph{lower} bids to employers they preferred to work with, implying that low tier employers are the most desirable, and yet this was precisely the tier that applicants avoided applying to (recall Figure~\ref{fig:app_counts}).

For the effects of revelation on the wage bids and profile rates of hired workers, we return to Figure~\ref{fig:compare_wb_to_pr}.
From the left, the second and fourth panels have samples restricted to hired workers. 
Hired worker profile rates were higher in the medium tier and high tier and about the same in the low tier, though again the effects are fairly imprecisely estimated.
For the wage bid, we see that revelation raised the wage bids of hired worker in the medium and high tiers, and lowered the wage bid in the low tier by \EffectWageBidAppLowTierHiredRegression{}\%.

\subsection{Match Outcomes and Efficiency} % 4.5

We now examine whether revelation of the employer's tier affected the quantity and characteristics of matches formed.
In the theory, the introduction of truthful cheap talk messages improves market efficiency (see Proposition \ref{comparison1}, Part 4).
Efficiency gains are important \emph{per se}, but they are also important for a matching platform itself, as it can eventually profit from improvements, either by increasing fees or by attracting more business. 

%\subsection{Message revelation alters the characteristics of hired applicants}

%\subsection{Inferential challenges of assessing match quality} 
A challenge with assessing match quality effects is that we only observe match characteristics, such as hours-worked, if a match is formed.
As such, we are inherently selecting samples that could be influenced by treatment assignment.
This selection could matter, biasing ``downstream'' measures.  

Selection forces us to be cautious in interpretation, but as we will see, there is no evidence that revelation affected the \emph{quantity} of matches formed.
Furthermore, there is no strong evidence that the kinds of job openings that filled differed by \textsc{ShownPref} with respect to pre-treatment attributes.\footnote{In Supplementary Empirical Appendix~\ref{supp_app:soo}, we show that job openings where a match was made had good balance on pre-treatment characteristics by treatment status, consistent with idiosyncratic factors affecting which openings were actually filled.}

An additional inferential issue is that slightly less than half of all job openings are filled, and so we have less power than for outcomes that we always observe.
To increase statistical power, we pool both the explicit and ambiguous arms.
Furthermore, we include not only the first job opening, but all subsequent openings by that employer during the experimental period, adjusting for the hierarchical data that results.
This gives us a total sample size of \TotalSampleSize{} jobs openings, of which  \TotalFilledSampleSize{} were filled. 

Although our preferred estimates for match outcomes are made with the full sample, in Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:trt_effects_robust} we report estimates for all the different possible sample combinations (e.g., explicit arm, first openings; ambiguous arm, first openings, all arms, all openings, and so on).
The point estimates differ with the sample, but the same general pattern of results is the same as reported when using all arms and all openings. 

To measure match outcomes, our regression specification is 
\begin{align} \label{eq:match_outcomes}
  y_j = \beta^k_0 + \beta^k_1 \textsc{ShownPref}_j + \epsilon  \big| k = \textsc{Tier}_{j}
\end{align}
where $\textsc{Tier}_{j}$ is the associated tier for the opening. 
We estimate separate regressions for each tier.
We also estimate the regression with all job openings pooled together, which we label ``Pooled.'' 
To account for the nested structure of the data, we cluster all standard errors at the level of the employer. 

The introduction of the cheap talk signaling opportunity changed many things about the match---the identity of the hired worker, the wage bid, and even the competitive environment. 
There are various ``objective'' measures of match quality we could observe, but perhaps one of the more straightforward measures is those that simply asked both sides how they felt. 
We split our analysis of outcomes into objective and subjective measures.

In looking at objective match outcomes, with our larger and different sample, we recapitulate some of the results from earlier---namely the number of applications and the hired worker wage. 
But we also add new outcomes, such as whether the job opening was filled. 
Using the full data, we report estimates of the coefficient on {\textsc{ShownPref}} from (\ref{eq:match_outcomes}) in Figure~\ref{fig:trt_effects}, using as outcomes:
(1) the log number of applications,

and (5) the log total wage bill.

For the filled openings, the sample is all job openings for which hired workers worked at least 15 minutes at a wage greater than 25 cents per hour.\footnote{
  We make this restriction on hours-worked and wages because a small number of employers (against the platform's wishes) create very low wage contracts to simply use the hours-tracking feature but not process payments through the platform.
}

For each estimate, we report the number of observations (``$n=\ldots$'') and for the pooled regression, the number of distinct employers (``$g=\ldots$'').

In the top panel of Figure~\ref{fig:trt_effects}, we see a reduction in applicant pool sizes from revelation in both the high and low tiers (recapitulating Figure~\ref{fig:app_counts}, but with a larger sample).

\subsection{Separating Equilibrium in the Long-run} 

A limitation of our experimental design is that it does not directly shed light on the repeated choices of firms over time, nor on the full market equilibrium when all firms are treated. Our variation comes from a setting where some firms get treated and others do not. If the block-recursive nature of our theory is valid, the insights from the within-market setting should carry over to a treatment of the full market, as the fraction of treated individuals did not matter. A particular implication in Part 5 of Proposition \ref{comparison1} is that the incentives to send particular messages should not be affected by the amount of firms that are treated in the market.

At the conclusion of the experiment, message revelation was rolled out to the entire market: all employers received an experience identical to the ${\textsc{ShownPref} = 1}$ cell in the explicit arm, meaning that all employers now knew their preferences would be revealed (and they were revealed).
There are two empirical approaches that allow us to investigate whether a separating equilibrium persisted:
we can (1) look at the fraction of job openings selecting the various tiers in the post period, and (2) look at trends within employer in the tier choice.


To investigate the evolution of choices into the post-experimental period, Figure~\ref{fig:tiers_over_time} shows the fraction of employers choosing the various tiers over time, with the end of the experiment indicated. The figure shows that the share of high-tier choices remains remarkably constant over time. It does not seem to be affected by the fact that only parts of the market could show their messages during the experiment compared to the roll-out over the whole market after the experiment, in line with Part 5 of Proposition \ref{comparison1}. There is a bit more volatility in the shares of medium and low-tier messages, with perhaps some evidence of an immediate post roll-out increase in low tier selections, but this does not persist and the long-run pattern seems to be one of relatively stable shares for each of the tiers.

We also looked at firms that posted jobs repeatedly during the experimental period. Recall that in the previous sections we only used the initial job posted by each of these firms. Our theory presumed that firms understand all features of the environment right away. It is conceivable that firms only slowly understand the strategic aspects of their environment, and even though they are truthful in their initial message they become more strategic over time, which could lead to pooling on particularly beneficial messages over time. In Online Appendix \ref{online_app:choice_over_time} we document that reporting is very stable over time: there is a slight trend away from low and medium-tier choices towards high-tier choices as firms repeatedly post jobs, but even after the tenth job posting firms are only 2\% point more likely to choose the high tier. Importantly, this is not affected by whether the preference was shown or hidden, which is another indication that firms tell the truth even when the message is strategically relevant (in line with the evidence of Section \ref{sec:results_existence}). 

So overall, message frequencies stay remarkably stable even as individual employers post jobs repeatedly, but also for the whole market over time and across a large roll-out to all firms. 


\section{Conclusion} \label{sec:conclusion}


Platform-engineered cheap-talk opportunities can move designed markets to more desirable equilibria, both in theory and in practice.
In our setting, match efficiency was improved and the quantity transacted in the market increased via a platform intervention that had essentially zero marginal cost.
Given the platform's pricing structure of applying an \emph{ad valorem} charge, the market intervention raised platform revenue by nearly 3\% if the experimental estimates generalized. 
Despite this positive result, there are several open questions, such as whether the method could be applied to other preference dimensions and other market settings.

A feature of the cheap-talk opportunity described here is that workers were able to apply cross-tier. 
It would be straight-forward to design a version of the cheap-talk opportunity in which workers would have to choose a tier and only apply within a tier for some period of time.
The tier selection could also be made centrally by the platform, using prior experience or feedback to create cut-off scores rather than allowing workers to self-select, which would turn the message game away from a cheap-talk game into a signalling game. 
This could lead to more sorting and more ``refined'' pools, but at the cost of greater intervention by the platform and the greater chance of leaving jobs under- or over-filled if supply is not managed.

In addition to determining which workers are allowed in which tier, another possible direction could be for the platform to define what different tiers ``mean,'' such as by labeling them with experience requirements. Theory provides little guidance on the actual lables that are used in cheap-talk, but in reality the ability to easily identify with particular labels might alter the ability of the market to coordinate on different cheap-talk messages. Labels with experience requirements might get more informed separation, though it also increases the burden on the platform in deciding what are reasonable tier labels. 

Our context is an online freelance labor market, which arguably requires particularly quick assessments of the types of jobs that workers want to engage in. As argued in the introduction, this market is by now no longer a niche part of the labor market, but a substantial and fast growing segment that is due to become the dominant one. We therefore view the insights for this market as relevant in its own right, even though our insights might apply more broadly since the matching process resembles in many elements those found in more conventional markets. The cheap-talk opportunity in this paper is framed as an expression of vertical preferences concerning the skills of employees. Skill matching is arguably one of the most important aspects of the labor market, and again we view the insights as valuable in their own right. But future work might consider the framing of cheap talk along other potentially relevant dimensions. For example, if employers could choose to describe their project as ``urgent'' could we get a similar sorting equilibrium? 
Employers could also signal information about their management ``style'' (e.g., closely managed or hands-off),  their degree of confidence in what works needs to be done, the degree of contract completeness, and so on.  
Essentially any feature of the economic relationship for which buyers and sellers have heterogeneous preferences or attributes and have imperfectly aligned incentives is a potential candidate for a cheap talk intervention, and how well cheap talk works along each of them likely depends on the incentives to mis-represent ones preferences, market conventions about ``babbling'', and bounds on how much cheap-talk can be facilitated before things become too complex. All this is part of a larger research agenda.

\section{Proofs and Analytic Derivations} \label{online_app:analysis}


\subsection{General Expression for the Worker Value Function and Proof of Lemma in Subsection~\ref{no-mass-point-lemma}}

Expression~(\ref{value}) in the main text assumes that the worker generates
weakly positive profits and that there is no mass point in the distribution
of profits. In general,
\begin{align}
&V(m,s_{1},a,b_{L},b_{H}) \label{value1} \\   &=\sum_{s_{2},T}\Psi (s_{2},T|m,s_{1})b_{s_{2}}\left( e^{-\Gamma
_{+}(\pi (a,b_{s_2}|T)|m,s_{1},s_{2},T)} \right) \mathbf{1}[\pi (a,b_{s_2})|T)\geq 0] \, M(\bigtriangleup (\pi
(a,b|T)|m,s_{1},s_{2},T)). \notag 
\end{align}
The first three factors inside the summation are unchanged with respect to (\ref{value}). The forth factor comprises an indicator function that takes the value of one if the argument is true and zero otherwise, capturing the fact that the worker only gets hires and generates non-zero expected payoff if his bid generates non-negative profits (otherwise the worker would not be hired). In the presence of mass points the worker not only has to worry about other workers who generate strictly higher profits for the firm, but also about workers who generate exactly the same profits. If such workers are present, then one of them gets selected at random. The final factor $M(\bigtriangleup )$ captures the probability that this worker is hired rather than any worker generating exactly the same profit. It is
strictly below 1 if there is a mass of competitors ($\bigtriangleup >0$) and is exactly 1 otherwise, and is constructed as follows.


The probability $M(\bigtriangleup )$ consists of several elements:
probability $e^{-\bigtriangleup }$ that no worker with equal profit is
present, the probability $\bigtriangleup e^{-\bigtriangleup }\frac{1}{2}$
that exactly one other worker with equal profit is present, but the other
worker is not chosen, the probability $\frac{\bigtriangleup
^{2}e^{-\bigtriangleup }}{2}\frac{1}{3}$ that two other workers with equal
profit is present, but the other workers are not chosen, and so forth. The probability
of being hired (conditional on no strictly more profitable bidders) is then
the infinite sum $M(\bigtriangleup )=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty }\frac{\bigtriangleup
^{k}e^{-\bigtriangleup }}{k!}\frac{1}{k+1}=\frac{1-e^{-\bigtriangleup }}{%
\bigtriangleup }.$
See \cite{burdett_etal2001} and \cite{wright_etal2021} for further references. \\


\textit{Proof of Lemma in Subsection~\ref{no-mass-point-lemma}:} 
Assume to the contrary
that $\bigtriangleup (\pi |m,s_{1},s_{2},T)>0$ at a point where $\Psi
(s_{2},T|m,s_{1})>0$ and $\pi \geq 0$. Then there must exist $%
(a,b_{L},b_{H}) $ in the support of $\Lambda (\cdot |m,s_{1})$ with $\pi
(a,b_{s_{2}}|T)=\pi .$ In fact, there must exist such combination with $%
b_{s_{2}}>0:$ Clearly $b_{s_{2}}\geq 0$ as otherwise the worker would make a
loss when hired and would improve his utility by raising the wage demand at
least to zero. It is not possible to have a mass of workers offering the
same $\pi $ and all offering $b_{s_{2}}=0,$ as in this case only a single
worker type solves (\ref{firm-profit}), and a single worker type does not
create a mass point.

Now that we know that $b_{s_{2}}>0,$ it is easy to see that this is not
optimal for the worker to demand this wage. If the worker chooses this
demand, the fact that $\bigtriangleup (\pi )>0$ implies that $%
M(\bigtriangleup (\pi ))<1$, where for ease of readability we suppressed
that this all conditions on $(m,s_{1},s_{2},T).$ Alternatively, we can find
for any $\varepsilon >0$ a bid $b_{s_{2}}^{\prime }\in
(b_{s_{2}}-\varepsilon ,b_{s_{2}})$ slightly below the original one with
associated profit $\pi ^{\prime }>\pi $ such that $\bigtriangleup (\pi
^{\prime })=0.$ This is due to the fact that only a countable number of
points can have a positive mass. This implies $M(\bigtriangleup (\pi
^{\prime }))=1,$ and the hiring probability captured by the third term in (%
\ref{value}) jumps up discretely relative to the original wage demand$.$ The
second term in (\ref{value}) weakly increases because $\pi ^{\prime }>\pi ,$
and the first and fifth term do not change. So the only negative comes from
the forth term that captures the wage conditional on getting hired. Since $%
b_{s_{2}}^{\prime }$ can be made arbitrarily close to $b_{s_{2}},$ the loss
in the wage can be made arbitrarily small and, thus, less than the gain from
the discrete jump in the hiring probability. $\square $



\subsection{Proof of Claim in Subsection~\ref{sec:truthful}}

Subsection~\ref{sec:truthful} claimed the following, which we prove here: \textit{In a truthful equilibrium, for each message $m\in\{L,H\}$ the worker abilities that enter form a non-empty interval $[\underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}]$, and no single worker type enters with positive mass.}

\bigskip

\textit{Proof of Claim}: We will first show that there is a positive mass of workers that
enter and approach message $m.$ Assume not, and consider worker type $a$
with $v_{T}(a-c_{T})-u\left( a\right) >0.$ This worker could enter, approach
firms with message $m,$ and bid $b=v_{m}(a-c_{m}).$ Since he would be hired
for sure as no better workers are present at any firm with such message, he
obtains $V(m,a,b)>u(a),$ contradicting the third equilibrium condition.

Next, we rule out that only a single type $a$ with mass $\delta >0$
approaches firms with messages $m$. Assume this were true. Since we have
proven that the distribution of profits that is offered has no mass point,
they have to offer different profits in equilibrium. Let $\pi _{l}$ be the
infimum and $\pi _{h}$ the supremum of such profits. We know by free entry
that $u(a)=e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{l}|m)}\left[ v_{m}(a-c_{m})-\pi _{l}\right]
=e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{h}|m)}\left[ v_{m}(a-c_{m})-\pi _{h}\right] =:U(a|m),$
where the last term denotes the utility conditional on entering and
approaching firms with message $m.$ Let $U_{-}(a|m)$ be the left derivative
and $U_{-}(a|m)$ be the right derivative of $U(a|m)$ with respect to $a.$ We
can apply the envelop theorem to obtain:%
\begin{eqnarray*}
U_{-}^{\prime }(a|m) &=&e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{l}|m)}v_{m} \\
U_{+}^{\prime }(a|m) &=&e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{h}|m)}v_{m}
\end{eqnarray*}%
We also know that $u^{\prime }(a)\leq U_{-}^{\prime }(a|m),$ since types $%
a^{\prime }$ below $a$ have $u(a^{\prime })\geq U(a^{\prime }|m)$ as
required by the third equilibrium condition and in order to reach $%
u(a)=U(a|m)$ the utility after entering has to rise at least as fast as the
utility of the outside option. But since $U_{-}^{\prime }(a|m)<U_{+}^{\prime
}(a|m),$ we obtain $U(a^{\prime \prime }|m)>u(a^{\prime \prime })$ for types 
$a^{\prime \prime }$ that are larger but close to $a.$ This yields a
contradiction to the third equilibrium condition.

Finally we will show that the set of abilities that approach message $m$ is
an interval. Assume not. Then there exist $a_{l}$ and $a_{h}>a_{l}$ that
approach firms with message $m$ but no firms in between (i.e., $a_{l}$ and $%
a_{h}$ are in the support of $\Lambda (a|m)$ but no $a\in (a_{l},a_{h})$ is
in the support). Let $\pi _{h}$ be the (supremum of) profits offered by type 
$a_{l},$ and $\pi _{h}$ the (infimum of) profits offered by $a_{h}$ to firms
with message $m.$ Since profits are rising in ability, we have $\pi _{l}\leq
\pi _{h}.$ Free entry implies $u(a_{l})=e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{l}|m)}\left[
v_{m}(a_{l}-c_{m})-\pi _{l}\right] $ and $u(a_{h})=e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{h}|m)}%
\left[ v_{m}(a_{h}-c_{m})-\pi _{h}\right] .$

Free entry also means for types $a\in (a_{l},a_{h})$ that their utility from
entering and approaching message $m$ needs to be weakly lower than the
outside option: $U(a^{\prime }|m)\leq u(a).$ Together with the previous
equalities this requires $U^{\prime }(a_{l}|m)=e^{-\Gamma (\pi
_{l}|m)}v_{m}\leq u^{\prime }(a_{l})$ and $U^{\prime }(a_{h}|m)=e^{-\Gamma
(\pi _{h}|m)}v_{m}\geq u^{\prime }(a_{h}).$ Since the outside option is
strictly concave, this implies $e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{h}|m)}>e^{-\Gamma (\pi
_{l}|m)}.$ But since the distribution of profits has no mass points and
there are no bids between $\pi _{h}$ and $\pi _{l},$ it holds that $\Gamma
(\pi _{h}|m)=\Gamma (\pi _{l}|m),$ yielding a contradiction. $\square $




\subsection{Proof of Proposition \ref{equilibrium-notalk}}

\noindent \textit{Proof of Proposition \ref{equilibrium-notalk}:} Consider firms that
cannot send a cheap-talk message. Given that we assumed precise second
signals, we can treat $s_{2}$ and the true type $T$ interchangeably, and for
ease of exposition we will only use the type $T$ here. Since we only have
one other signal left, we can simply write $s$ instead of $s_{1}.$ As long
as we consistently refer to the same signal, we might also omit the signal
from the notation. Now consider workers who see signal $s$ and believe that
the fraction of low (high) firms at this signal is $\Psi (L|s)$ ($\Psi
(H|s)) $ where we suppressed the message because it is absent ($m=\emptyset
).$ Note that these fractions are exogenously given; for example, $\Psi
(L|L)=\psi _{1}\delta _{L}/(\psi _{1}\delta _{L}+(1-\psi _{1})\delta _{H})$
and does not depend on the equilibrium interaction.

Similar to (\ref{value-profit-truth}) and (\ref{value-profit-truth2}) we can
now write the utility of a worker with ability $a$ as%
\begin{equation}
W(a|s)=\max_{\pi _{L},\pi _{H}}\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{T}|T,s)} 
\left[ v_{T}(a-c_{T})-\pi _{T}\right] \boldsymbol{1}[a\geq c_{T}],
\label{objective-babbling}
\end{equation}%
where $\pi _{L}$ and $\pi _{H}$ are the profits that the worker offers to
the low and high type firm respectively, and $\Gamma (\pi _{T}|T,s)$ denotes
the queue of workers who offers higher profits to these firms at this
message. Workers can only submit profit bids larger than zero if their type
exceeds the threshold, and therefore $\boldsymbol{1[\cdot ]}$ is an
indicator that takes the value of one if the ability is higher than the work
requirement, and zero otherwise.

Due to the same supermodularity between $a$ and $\pi _{T},$ higher ability
workers leave higher profits. Again there cannot be any mass points nor
holes in the distribution of offers $\Gamma (\pi _{T}|T,s).$ We focus for
now on workers with ability larger than requirement $c_{H},$ and will later
show that this is the relevant range. This allows us to drop the indicator
function in (\ref{objective-babbling}). For such workers, utility increases
in ability according to%
\begin{equation*}
W^{\prime }(a|s)=e^{-\Lambda (a|s)}\hat{v},
\end{equation*}%
where $\Lambda (a|s)$ is the queue of workers at message $s$ with type $a$
or higher and $\hat{v}$ is the average valuation for their work 
\begin{equation*}
\widehat{v}=\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)v_{T}\boldsymbol{.}
\end{equation*}%
Clearly the average valuation is increasing in the belief that the firm is
of high type. We can now apply the same steps as in Subsection \ref{sec:truthful}, just with this replaced term for the value. As in (\ref%
{first derivative}) the increase in value has to equal the increase in
outside option to make free entry feasible, so 
\begin{equation*}
e^{-\Lambda (a|s)}\widehat{v}=u^{\prime }(a),
\end{equation*}%
and the second derivative yields in analogy to (\ref{second derivative}) 
\begin{equation*}
\lambda (a|s)e^{-\Lambda (a|s)}\widehat{v}=u^{\prime \prime }(a)
\end{equation*}%
so that in analogy to we obtain 
\begin{equation*}
\lambda (a|s)=u^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a).
\end{equation*}%
So tightness-density is completely governed by the outside option, exactly
as in the previous section. So in terms of types that approach a given
initial signal, the allocation remains random where the types between $s=L$
and $s=H$ overlap, and it is identical to the types that queue in the fully
revealing equilibrium we already studied. Wage bids are also relatively
straightforward. At a given first signal $s$ and a second signal that
reveals type $T,$ the wages are given by a differential equation identical
to (\ref{wages})%
\begin{equation}
b_{T}^{\prime }(a|T,s)=v_{T}B^{\prime }(a).  \label{wages2}
\end{equation}

The lowest worker type $\underline{a}_{s}$ that approaches message $s$ can
again only win if no other worker is present, no matter whether the second
signal indicates that it is a high or a low type. So at either firm type
this worker extracts all output conditional on matching, and in this case
obtains 
\begin{eqnarray*}
&&\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)v_{T}(\underline{a}_{s}-c_{T}) \\
&=&\widehat{v}[\underline{a}_{s}-\widehat{c}]\text{ with }\widehat{c}%
:=\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)[v_{T}c_{T}]/\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)v_{T}.
\end{eqnarray*}%
where the second line simply re-orders the terms. The term $\widehat{c}$
equals $c_{L}$ when workers think they face a low type for sure (i.e., $\Psi
(L|s)=1$), $\widehat{c}$ is monotonically increasing in the probability that
the firm is a high type, and it equals $c_{H}$ when workers think they face
a high type for sure. For the same reasons as in Subsection \ref{sec:truthful}
the lowest type is solely determined by the costs via implicit function 
\begin{equation}
\underline{a}_{s}=\widehat{c}+u(\underline{a}_{s})/u^{\prime }(\underline{a}%
_{s}).  \label{cutoff-general}
\end{equation}%
The lowest type $\underline{a}_{s}$ is increasing in $\widehat{c},$ and
therefore increasing in the probability of facing a high type firm.

This fully characterizes the interaction at jobs that do not have cheap-talk
available to them, except that we dropped the indicator function after
equation (\ref{objective-babbling}). We first note that $\underline{a}_{s}$
is always above $c_{H},$ because even $\underline{a}_{T=L}$ is above because
of our restriction on the difference between $c_{L}$ and $c_{H}.$\footnote{%
Equation (\ref{cutoff-general}) applied to $\widehat{c}=c_{L}$ implies that
we are looking for $\underline{a}_{T=L}=c_{L}+u(\underline{a}%
_{T=L})/u^{\prime }(\underline{a}_{T=L}).$ We assumed that $%
c_{H}-c_{L}<u(c_{H})/u^{\prime }(c_{H})$ or equivalently that $%
c_{H}<c_{L}+u(c_{H})/u^{\prime }(c_{H}).$ Therefore, $\underline{a}%
_{T=L}>c_{H}.$ Since the cutoff at any other $\widehat{c}$ is higher, it
will also be above $c_{H}.$} So the worker with lowest ability according to
our derivations so far would bid for jobs both at high and low firms in a
way that leaves non-negative profits for the firms, and this worker would
just recover his/her outside option. We also know from our previous analysis
that no worker with ability below $\underline{a}_{s}$ can recover their
outside option if this worker makes offers to both firms that leave
non-negative profits to these firms. Workers with abilities below $c_{H}$
would choose not to be hired by high firm types. They would never be able to
recover their outside option, though, either, as we now show in the final
step:

Type $\underline{a}_{T=L}$ has 
\begin{eqnarray}
&&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{s}|s)}\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)v_{T}(\underline{a}%
_{T=L}-c_{T})=U(\underline{a}_{s})  \notag \\
&\Rightarrow &e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{s}|s)}\Psi (L|s)v_{L}(\underline{a}%
_{T=L}-c_{L})<U(\underline{a}_{T=L})  \notag \\
&\Rightarrow &e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{s}|s)}\Psi (L|s)v_{L}<\underline{v}%
_{T=L}(\underline{a}_{T=L}),  \label{x}
\end{eqnarray}%
where $\underline{v}_{T=L}(a)=U(a)/(a-c_{l})$ as defined in Subsection \ref{sec:truthful}. Any type $a<\underline{a}_{T=L}$ who only bids for the low
job obtains $e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{s}|s)}\Psi (L|s)v_{L}(\underline{a}%
-c_{L})$ but has outside option $U(a)=\underline{v}_{T=L}(a)(a-c_{L}).$ For
this to justify entry we have to have $e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}%
_{s}|s)}\Psi (L|s)\geq \underline{v}_{T=L}(a),$ but this is contradicted by (%
\ref{x}) and the fact that $\underline{v}_{T=L}(\underline{a}_{T=L})$ is the
global minimum of $\underline{v}_{T=L}(\underline{a}).\square $




\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{corollary}}

\textit{Proof of Corollary~\ref{corollary}:}
The first point follows directly from (\ref{equilibrium highest type}), which depends on the firm's valuation but not its ability threshold. The third point follows from (\ref{equilibrium density}), which depends on neither valuation nor threshold. The second point states that the lowest type $\underline{a}_{m}$ is a function of the threshold but not of the valuation. This is not directly obvious from (\ref{equilibrium lowest type}). We establish it via the following auxiliary claim:

\bigskip

\noindent \textit{Claim: Holding the lowest worker type $\underline{a}_{m}$ at truthful message $m$ fixed but changing $v_{m}$ does not alter the expected utility at the lowest type.}

\bigskip

\textit{Proof of Claim:} The expected utility at $\underline{a}_{m}$ is, according to (\ref{equilibrium lowest type}), given by $e^{-\Lambda(\underline{a}_{m}|m)}v_{m}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})$. We take comparative statics of this expression with respect to $v_{m}$, holding $\underline{a}_{m}$ fixed but allowing $\Lambda(\underline{a}_{m}|m)$ to change. The derivative is
\begin{eqnarray*}
&&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})+e^{-\Lambda (%
\underline{a}_{m}|m)}\left[ -\frac{d\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}{dv_{m}}%
\right] v_{m}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m}) \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})\left[
1-v_{m}\lambda (\bar{a}_{m}|m)\frac{d\bar{a}_{m}}{dv_{m}}\right] \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})\left[
1-v_{m}\lambda (\bar{a}_{m}|m)u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{m})\right] \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})\left[ 1-\frac{%
v_{m}}{u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{m})}\right] \\
&=&0.
\end{eqnarray*}
The second line rewrites the change in the queue of workers; the third line follows by implicitly differentiating (\ref{equilibrium highest type}); the fourth line uses (\ref{equilibrium density}) to substitute out $\lambda(\cdot)$; and the last line again uses (\ref{equilibrium highest type}).$\square$

\bigskip

Equipped with this claim, we can now complete the proof of the second point. The claim shows that for a given $\underline{a}_{m}$ the utility at this ability stays constant as $v_{m}$ changes once one adjusts for the associated change in the queue $\Lambda(\underline{a}_{m}|m)$: when the valuation increases, more higher-ability types are added, leaving the utility at the low end exactly unchanged. This yields an alternative construction of the lowest type. First, define $\underline{v}_{m}(a)=u(a)/[a-c_{m}]$, the lowest valuation at which a type-$a$ worker can enter and break even. Then let $\underline{a}_{m}=\arg\min \underline{v}_{m}(a)$ — the ability level that requires the lowest valuation for entry to be possible. The first-order condition for this minimization reveals that the lowest type is the unique solution to
\begin{equation}\label{cutoff-type}
\underline{a}_{m}=c_{m}+\frac{u(\underline{a}_{m})}{u^{\prime}(\underline{a}_{m})}.
\end{equation}%
This type always enters when $v_{m}>\underline{v}_{m}(\underline{a}_{m})$, and the higher $v_{m}$, the more other types enter. The lowest type $\underline{a}_{m}$ is increasing in $c_{m}$, which follows by implicitly differentiating (\ref{cutoff-type}).$\square$



\subsection{Proof of Proposition \ref{comparison1}}

\noindent \textit{Proof of Proposition \ref{comparison1}:} Part 1 and 3 follow
directly from Corollary \ref{corollary} and \ref{corollary 2}. For Part 2, note
that effects on the number of applications are ambiguous since low firm
types who send a truthful message attract additional low ability workers but loose high ability workers relative to their no-message counterparts.
How this affects applications depends on the number of workers that they add at low abilities and the number of workers they loose at high abilities. This in turn depends on two things: how many additional ability types are added or lost, and the queue of workers at each ability level (conditional on applying at all) which by
(\ref{equilibrium density}) equals $u^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a)$. For $v_{H}\approx v_{L}$ the size of the high-ability segment that is lost shrinks to zero, and
truthful cheap-talk attracts more workers to low-message firms. The opposite
is the case for high-message firms. In the case of $v_{H}> v_L$ but $c_H \approx c_L$, the size of the low ability segment that is lost or added shrinks to zero, and truthful messages at low type firms induces a sizeable loss of high-ability workers without an offsetting gain at low-ability workers. For high type firm truthful cheap talk induces a gain in high ability workers with an offset of low ability workers. For Part 4, note that efficiency per firm with a message is given by 
\begin{equation}
\max_{\lambda _{m=L}^{P}(.),\lambda _{m=H}^{P}(.)}\delta
_{L}S(v_{L},c_{L}|\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.))+\delta _{H}S(v_{H},c_{H}|\lambda
_{m=H}^{P}(.))  \label{max1}
\end{equation}%
where 
\begin{eqnarray*}
S(v,c|\lambda (.)) &=&\int \left( v(a-c)\right) d\left(
1-e^{-\int_{a}^{\infty }\lambda (a^{\prime })da^{\prime }}\right) -\int
\lambda (a)u(a)da \\
&&\int v(a-c)\lambda (a)e^{-\int_{a}^{\infty }\lambda (a^{\prime
})da^{\prime }}da-\int \lambda (a)u(a)da
\end{eqnarray*}%
represents the output of the firm minus the forgone outside option of all
the workers who queue at this firm. Let 
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S}(v,c)=\max_{\lambda (.)}S(v,c|\lambda (.)).  \label{max-by-type}
\end{equation}%
The optimality conditions for this problem coincide nearly everywhere with
the equilibrium conditions (\ref{first derivative}) for a given firm type,
and it shares the same boundary conditions. This establishes that the
equilibrium decentralizes the planner's solution. Evidently the value of
program (\ref{max1}) is given by $\delta _{L}\mathcal{S}(v_{L},c_{L})+\delta
_{H}\mathcal{S}(v_{H},c_{H}).$

For firms who cannot send a message the maximal efficiency per firm is
attainable through queue of 
\begin{eqnarray}
\max_{\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.),\lambda _{s=H}^{P}(.)} &&\delta _{L}\left[ \psi
_{1}S(v_{L},c_{L}|\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.))+(1-\psi _{1})S(v_{L},c_{L}|\lambda
_{s=H}^{P}(.))\right]  \label{max2} \\
&&+\delta _{H}\left[ \psi _{1}S(v_{H},c_{H}|\lambda _{s=H}^{P}(.))+(1-\psi
_{1})S(v_{H},c_{H}|\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.))\right] ,  \notag
\end{eqnarray}%
where $S(.)$ still denotes the output minus the forgone outside option of
workers, but which queue a firm obtains now depends on which signal it
sends. The optimality conditions for this problem again coincide with
conditions for the decentralzied equilibrium, and thus the decentralized
equilibrium obtains the highest surplus given the information frictions.
Nevertheless, evidently value of maximization problem (\ref{max1}) is higher
than that of maximization problem (\ref{max2}) since the queue length in the
former can be chosen to maximize the surplus for each firm while in the
latter it has to partially cater to both firm types. Formally, note that
both $S(v_{L},c_{L}|\lambda _{s=L}^{\ast }(.))$ and $S(v_{L},c_{L}|\lambda
_{s=H}^{\ast }(.))$ are bounded above by $\mathcal{S}(v_{L},c_{L}),$ and $%
S(v_{H},c_{H}|\lambda _{s=H}^{P}(.))$ and $S(v_{H},c_{H}|\lambda
_{s=L}^{P}(.))$ are bounded above by $\mathcal{S}(v_{L},c_{L}).$ Therefore
the value of problem (\ref{max2}) is bounded from above by $\delta _{L}%
\mathcal{S}(v_{L},c_{L})+\delta _{H}\mathcal{S}(v_{H},c_{H}),$ which
coincides with the value of problem (\ref{max1}).

In fact, one can prove that value of problem (\ref{max2}) is strictly below
that of (\ref{max1}): the maximizer $\lambda ^{\ast P}(a|v,c)$ of problem (%
\ref{max-by-type}) is unique for given $(v,c)$ and strictly different for
different values of $(v,c)$ as it inherits the same properties as the
equilibrium queue length under truthful cheap talk. Equal value of the two
problems above would require that $\lambda ^{\ast
P}(.|v_{L},c_{L})=\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.)=\lambda _{s=H}^{P}(.)$ and $%
\lambda ^{\ast P}(a|v_{H},c_{H})=\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.)=\lambda
_{s=H}^{P}(.), $ which yields a contradiction to $\lambda ^{\ast
P}(.|v_{L},c_{L})\neq \lambda ^{\ast P}(a|v_{H},c_{H}).$

Finally, Part 5 is a direct consequence of block-recursivity. The characterization in Proposition~\ref{equilibrium-characterization} of the interval $[\underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}]$, the queue $\Lambda(a|m)$, and the wages $b_{m}(a)$ for firms that send message $m$ is pinned down by equations (\ref{equilibrium density})--(\ref{equilibrium lowest type}), (\ref{wages}), and (\ref{wages boundary}). None of these equations reference the mass of firms without messages. The same is true for the corresponding objects at firms without messages, characterized in Proposition~\ref{equilibrium-notalk}: they depend on the averaged valuation $v_{\Psi}$ and cost $c_{\Psi}$ at the given first signal, but not on the mass of firms that can send messages. Hence equilibrium outcomes conditional on $(m,s_{1},s_{2})$ are invariant to the fraction $\rho$ of firms that are asked to message, so firms' incentives to choose different messages do not depend on $\rho$.$\square$






\subsection{Role of the Second Signal, and Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:incentive_compat}}
\label{online_app:second_signal} 

The second signal captures that workers can target their bidding to more information after having read the
details of the job description. Taking the limiting case where this signal
is arbitrarily precise simplifies the mathematical representation of the
problem substantially as the bid is determined simply as the weighted
average of bids from two different differential equations.. Consider instead
a setting where workers remain unsure about the firm type at the point of
bidding, and assign probability $\Psi (T)$ to it being type $T$. It is easy
to show that their bids would follow differential equation\footnote{
See our previous working paper Horton, Johari and Kircher (2021) that
derives this and solves the related model, but at the expense of assuming
that workers do not receive any first signal. This entails the very strong
implication that workers select among firms at random in the absence of
messages. The formulation in this paper allows clear and tractable insights
that allows for selection based on some information even without signals.}

\[
\frac{b(a)}{\sum\nolimits_{T}\lambda (a)\Psi (T)/(v_{T}-b^{\prime }(a))} =1.
\]

This converges to the differential equation in (\ref{wages}) when $\Psi
(T)\longrightarrow 1$, as do the associated endpoint conditions. Working in
the limit is easier as the expected bid of a worker after the first signal
is simply the weighted average of the bids after the second signal, while
the aggregation is much more cumbersome if uncertainty remains even after
the second signal. This begs the question how workers interpret the signal
after a truthful message. We assume here that they do not rely on the second
signal if they believe the second signal to be truthful. This does matter
for the incentives of firms to mis-represente their types. In particular,
for low type firms who pretend to be high types this induces high wage
demands. If workers were to learn the actual firm type, low type firms that
mis-represented their type could attract more applicants, but these would
learn their type and then bid low. One can show that truth-telling in this
setting would not be sustained. So our formulation is best viewed as an
approximation to the setting where signals are not fully revealing. It also
highlights the value of our empirical setting: it is not obvious that
allowing for cheap-talk will increase market information, as this would fail
under different informational assumptions, and off course under different
equilibria such as the well-known ``babbling'' equilibrium where no
information is transmitted.

\bigskip



\noindent \textit{Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:incentive_compat}:} 

Consider first the limiting case where the second signal has very high precision. 

Note first that the probability
that a firm at message $m$ has no applicant with qualification above $a$ is $%
e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}.$ That means that the density that the best applicant is
of type $a$ is

$\lambda (a|m)e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}$. So the expected profit at message $m$ for
firm type $t$ is at least%
\begin{eqnarray}
\Pi (m|t) &\geq &\int_{\underline{a}_{m}}^{\bar{a}_{m}}\max
\{v_{t}(a-c_{t})-b_{m}(a),0\}\lambda (a|m)e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}da.  \notag \\
&=&\int_{\underline{a}_{m}}^{\bar{a}_{m}}\max \left\{ \frac{%
v_{t}(a-c_{t})-b_{m}(a)}{v_{m}},0\right\} u^{\prime \prime }(a)da.
\label{truth-start} \\
&=&\int_{\underline{a}_{m}}^{\bar{a}_{m}}\max \left\{ \frac{v_{t}(a-c_{t})-%
\frac{v_{m}}{v_{L}}b_{L}(a)}{v_{m}},0\right\} u^{\prime \prime }(a)da. 
\notag \\
&=&\int_{\underline{a}_{m}}^{\bar{a}_{m}}\max \left\{ \frac{v_{t}}{v_{m}}%
(a-c_{t})-\frac{b_{L}(a)}{v_{L}},0\right\} u^{\prime \prime }(a)da,  \notag
\end{eqnarray}%
where it is suppressed that $\bar{a}_{m}$ is a function of $v_{m}$ and $%
\underline{a}_{m}$ is a function of $c_{t}.$ The first line holds exactly
with equality if workers with higher ability offer more profits. In this
case the highest $a$ is always selected, and leaves profits $%
v_{t}(a-c_{t})-b_{m}(a)$ to the firm whenever this is positive. The second
line simply substitutes (\ref{second derivative}), and the third line uses $%
b_{H}(a)=\frac{v_{H}}{v_{L}}b_{L}(a)$ which follows directly from Corollary %
\ref{corollary}.

On the equilibrium path we have proven that higher types indeed offer more
profits, so the first line holds with equality when $m=t=L$ or $m=t=H.$ It
also holds with equality when high types contemplate to send a low message $%
(t=H$ but $m=L)$: since $v_{L}(a-c_{L})-b_{L}(a)$ is increasing in $a$ in
equilibrium, also $v_{L}(a-c_{L})-b_{L}(a)$ is increasing for all $a\geq
c_{H},$ and worker types that have a lower $a$ are always rejected as taken
into account in the max operator. This makes it easy to analyze these three
cases. It may not hold in the case of low firm types sending a high message $%
(t=L$ and $m=H)$ which makes its analysis more challenging.

Truth-telling requires that $\Pi (H|H)\geq \Pi (L|H)$ and $\Pi (L|L)\geq \Pi
(H|L).$

\textit{We will first consider the truth-telling condition for high types }$%
(T=H)$\textit{:}

\begin{eqnarray}
\Pi (H|H) &\geq &\Pi (L|H)  \label{truth1} \\
&\Leftrightarrow &\int_{\underline{a}_{H}}^{\bar{a}_{H}}\left[ a-c_{t}-\frac{%
b_{L}(a)}{v_{L}}\right] u^{\prime \prime }(a)da\geq \int_{\underline{a}%
_{L}}^{\bar{a}_{L}}\max \left\{ \frac{v_{H}}{v_{L}}(a-c_{t})-\frac{b_{L}(a)}{%
v_{L}},0\right\} u^{\prime \prime }(a)da  \label{truth2}
\end{eqnarray}

For $0=c_{L}=c_{H},$ it holds that $\underline{a}_{H}=\underline{a}_{L}=0.$
Fix $v_{L},$ and vary $v_{H}.$ The derivatives are%
\begin{eqnarray}
\frac{d\Pi (H|H)}{dv_{H}} &=&\left[ \bar{a}_{H}-\frac{b_{L}(\bar{a}_{H})}{%
v_{L}}\right] u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{H})\frac{d\bar{a}_{H}}{dv_{H}}=%
\bar{a}_{H}-\frac{b_{L}(\bar{a}_{H})}{v_{L}},  \label{truth3} \\
\frac{d\Pi (L|H)}{dv_{H}} &=&\int_{\underline{a}_{L}}^{\bar{a}_{L}}\frac{a}{%
v_{L}}u^{\prime \prime }(a)da,  \label{truth4}
\end{eqnarray}%
where the second equality in the first line follows from implicitly
differentiating (\ref{equilibrium highest type}). The first line goes to
infinity since $b_{L}(a)$ is bounded away from $v_{L}a$ for all $a>%
\underline{a}_{H}+\varepsilon $ for some $\varepsilon ,$ which follows from (%
\ref{wages}) and the assumption that $au^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a)$
is bounded away from zero. The second line is simply a constant. So
truthtelling is incentive compatible for high types when $v_{H}$ is
sufficiently large.

When $v_{H}\approx v_{L}$ and $c_{H}=0,$ truth-telling requires 
\begin{equation}
\bar{a}_{L}-\frac{b_{L}(\bar{a}_{L})}{v_{L}}\geq \int_{\underline{a}_{L}}^{%
\bar{a}_{L}}\frac{a}{v_{L}}u^{\prime \prime }(a)da,  \label{truth-local}
\end{equation}%
which follows since $\bar{a}_{H}\approx \bar{a}_{L}.$

\textit{Now consider truth-telling for low types }$(T=L):$

We will first consider truthtelling when valuations of high and low type
firms are roughly similar, and will show that incentive compatibility
generically fails when $v_{H}$ approaches $v_{L}$ and $c_{H}=0.$ In this
case, it has to hold that%
\begin{equation*}
\int_{\underline{a}_{L}}^{\bar{a}_{L}}\left[ a-\frac{b_{L}(a)}{v_{L}}\right]
u^{\prime \prime }(a)da\geq \int_{\underline{a}_{H}}^{\bar{a}_{H}}\max
\left\{ \frac{v_{L}}{v_{H}}a-\frac{b_{L}(a)}{v_{L}},0\right\} u^{\prime
\prime }(a)da.
\end{equation*}%
Taking the derivative with respect to $v_{H}$ and evaluating it around $%
v_{H}\approx v_{L}$ (and $\bar{a}_{H}\approx \bar{a}_{L}$ as well as $%
\underline{a}_{H}=\underline{a}_{L}=0)$ yields%
\begin{equation}
0\geq -\int_{\underline{a}_{H}}^{\bar{a}_{H}}\frac{a}{v_{L}}u^{\prime \prime
}(a)da+\left[ \bar{a}_{H}-\frac{b_{L}(\bar{a}_{H})}{v_{L}}\right] .
\label{truth-local2}
\end{equation}%
Inequality (\ref{truth-local2}) is the opposite of inequality (\ref%
{truth-local}), and generically in the space of permissable $u(.)$ and $%
v_{L} $ they do not both hold simultaneously.

Next, we consider truth-telling of low types when $v_{H}$ is large (for
fixed $v_{L})$ and will show that it is incentive compatible. We will first
build intuition under the case where firms are willing to hire all workers,
so that $0=c_{L}=c_{H}.$ It will then be easy to expand to the general case
afterwards. For this part of the proof it is convenient to denote by $\check{%
a}$ the ability type with $u^{\prime }(v)=1\,$or ability type $\bar{a}_{L},$
whichever one is larger. Let $\check{v}\geq v_{L}$ be such that $\bar{a}_{H}=%
\check{a}$ if $v_{H}=\check{v}.$ Note that if the low firm type could only
match with workers of abilities $a\in \lbrack 0.\check{a}],$ it is easy to
show that deviating and sending a high message is not profitable:

- For abilities $a\in \lbrack \underline{a}_{H},\bar{a}_{L}]$ the queue does
not change but the wage becomes $b_{H}(a)=b_{L}(a)v_{H}/v_{L},$ so the firm
pays more for these workers but does not attract more of them.

- For abilities $a\in \lbrack 0,\underline{a}_{H}]$ workers cease to approach
the firm if it sends the high signal.

- For abilities $a\in \lbrack \bar{a}_{L},\check{a}]$, let $\bar{g}%
=\sup_{a\in \lbrack \bar{a}_{L},\check{a}]}g(a)>0$ so that the wage $%
b_{H}(a)\geq \bar{g}v_{H}/v_{L},$ which exceeds $v_{L}\check{a}$ for $v_{H}$
sufficiently large. So the low type firm would make a loss hiring these
workers, and would refuse to do so. So their presence does not constitute a
profitable deviation.

Now consider also abilities beyond $\check{a}.$ In general, by raising $%
v_{H} $ from $\check{v}$ to higher values, from the perspective of a
low-type firm that contemplates to send message $H$ this has two effects:
For all ability types $a\in \lbrack 0,\bar{a}_{H}]$ the wage $%
b_{H}(a)=v_{H}g(a)$ increases when $v_{H}$ is marginally increased, which is
a negative outcome from the firm's perspective. But one also raises the
upper end of workers willing to match. We will show that even for the second
effect, the additional wage will outweigh the benefit in matching. We know
that $u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})=v_{H}.$ So $d\bar{a}_{H}/dv_{H}=1/u^{\prime
\prime }(\bar{a}_{H}).$ So the wage at the higest type increase by 
\begin{eqnarray*}
db_{H}(\bar{a}_{H})/dv_{H} &=&d(v_{H}g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right) )/dv_{H} \\
&=&g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right) +v_{H}g^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})d\bar{a}%
_{H}/dv_{H} \\
&=&g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right) +v_{H}g^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})d\bar{a}%
_{H}/dv_{H} \\
&=&g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right) +v_{H}[1-g(\bar{a}_{H})u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{%
a}_{H})/u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})]/u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{H}) \\
&=&g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right) +v_{H}/u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{H})-g(\bar{a%
}_{H})/u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})
\end{eqnarray*}%
For the low firm type who considers sending message $H,$ a higher value $%
v_{H}$ implies the following change in profits when matching with the
highest type at message $H:$ $v_{L}\bar{a}_{H}-b_{H}(\bar{a}_{H}).$ So this
changes in $v_{H}$ according to 
\begin{eqnarray*}
&&v_{L}d\bar{a}_{H}/dv_{H}-db_{H}(\bar{a}_{H})/dv_{H} \\
&=&v_{L}/u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{H})-g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right)
-v_{H}/u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{H})+g(\bar{a}_{H})/u^{\prime }(\bar{a}%
_{H})
\end{eqnarray*}%
which is definitely negative as $\bar{a}_{H}\geq \check{a},$ which implies $%
u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})\geq 1.$ This establishes that for high enough $%
v_{H} $ low types strictly prefer to send message $m=L$ to message $m=H$ in
a truthtelling equilibrium. Since high types also prefer to tell the truth,
this establishes that truth-telling is an equilibrium outcome when $%
c_{L}=c_{H}=0.$

We finally have to establish that truth-telling is incentive compatible for
low firm types when $v_{H}$ is sufficiently large even in the more general
setting where $0=c_{L}<c_{H}:$ We have already established that low firm
types would not pretend to be high firm types if costs are zero. In the
candidate equilibrium with truth-telling, note that strictly positive costs
at high types do not change the workers who approach low types or their
bids. On the other hand, at high types the set of worker types shrinks,
those who do approach high messages do so with exactly the same queue $%
\lambda (a|H)$ by (\ref{equilibrium density})$,$ and their bids increase. So
if low types do not want to deviate and pretend to be high types under $%
c_{H}=0,$ they will not want to do so when $c_{H}>0.$ For high types we had
already established incentive compatibility in the general case, which
concludes the proof for the case when the second signal has limit precision of $\psi_2=1$. 

Finally, consider the case where there remains some imprecision in the second signal ($\psi_2 <1$), but precision is sufficiently high. We established earlier that under the assumption of truth-telling equilibria converge to the limit equilibrium when the precision $\psi_2$ of the second signal increases to 1. So payoffs from truth-telling and lying converge to the limit arguments. Since we have shown above that for sufficient differences in valuations both firm types strictly prefer truth-telling in a candidate equilibrium with full type revelation, this continues to hold when the signal is sufficiently precise. Furthermore, the proof above establishes that for equal costs and valuations that are very close only a vanishingly small set of parameter values induces truth-telling, while for all others at least one type has strict incentives to pretend to be the other type. For a given set of parameters where there are strict incentives to ``lie'' (i.e., strict incentives to send the message that does not correspond to the own type) when the second signal has limit precision, a sufficiently high second signals will again also induce incentives to lie.$\square $ 

\bigskip

We omit the generic failure of truth-telling when types are very close together as this is a standard result that appears in much of the literature. In Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:ic_general_prod} we prove incentive compatibility for more general production functions.






\subsection{Discussion of the Role of Imprecise Second Signals}
\label{online_app:imprecise_second_signal} 

When we establish incentive compatibility in Proposition \ref{prop:incentive_compat} we use the limit formulation with perfect second signals, but establish that incentives for truth-telling are strict when valuations are far enough apart, and because of convergence (as shown in Online Appendix \ref{online_app:second_signal}) this carries over to settings with imprecise second signals as long as the second signal is sufficiently precise. The limit results are useful because they allow us to take averages, which makes the derivations under signals (which are imperfect initially) and truthful messages (which reveal types perfectly right away) comparable and allows for relatively simple comparative statics. 

While our results carry over when signals are sufficiently precise, it is worth discussing why we rather think of them as being imprecise and use the limit only for mathematical convenience. The reason becomes apparent when one considers what happens if there is no second signal and workers simply know the true firm type at that point. This does change incentive compatibility for the following reason: our current setup studies truthful cheap talk, and if an $L$-firm deviated and sends message $m=H$ then workers believe that this is an $H$-firm (as only these send such messages in equilibrium) and approach and bid at such firms as if it is an $H$-firm. The deviant firm would attract better workers but these would demand higher wages. If workers were to learn the actual firm type through the second message, low type firm that
mis-represents its type could attract more applicants, but these would
learn the firm's type and then bid low. One can show that truth-telling in this
setting would not be sustained. So our formulation is best viewed as an
approximation to the setting where signals are not fully revealing. It also
highlights the value of our empirical setting: it is not obvious that
allowing for cheap-talk will increase market information, as this would fail
under different informational assumptions, and off course under different
equilibria such as the well-known ``babbling'' equilibrium where no
information is transmitted.





\subsection{Partially Revealing Equilibria}
\label{online_app:partially_revealing_eq}

Truthful cheap talk is not the only
equilibrium in cheap-talk games, even if such equilibria exists. It is
well-known that there always exists a babbling equilibrium where receivers
disregard the messages because senders have chosen them at random. Whether
parameters admit a truthful equilibrium or not, there also often exist
partially revealing equilibrium where some additional information is
transmitted, but not all types are perfectly revealed. Here we briefly point
out that if a cheap-talk intervention displays any effect, it must be linked
to some information transmission. We show here that all equilibria where
cheap-talk reveals some additional information inherit the same broad
empirical implications: Part 1, 2 and 4 of Proposition \ref{comparison1} and the Corollaries \ref%
{corollary} and \ref{corollary 2} that lead up to it are based around
comparative statics. Less information essentially acts to average the
valuation and the costs. The base model does it for the extreme case of
starting with perfect information revelation, but the comparative statics
hold similarly when messages are only partially informative.\footnote{%
Parts 1, 3 and 4 of Proposition Proposition \ref{comparison1} rely on
comparative statics and carry over to partially informative equilibria. In
contrast, Part 2 of Proposition \ref{comparison1} uses a limit argument for
the the size of the high valuation, holding the amount of information that
is conveyed constant. The approach to the proof extends if there is a lower
bound on the information that a partially informative equilibrium conveys
about firms, but the technique of the proof does not extend if the amount of
information goes to zero as the valuation of the high type firm becomes
large.}





\section{Model Extensions: General Production Functions}
\label{online_app:robustness}

This section briefly discusses an extension from Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:analysis} to more general production functions. We outline conditions under which comparative statics and incentive compatibility results similar to those in the main paper still hold.

\subsection{More General Production Functions}
\label{online_app:general_production}

Our production function is a
special case of increasing and concave production function $\phi (a,T)$ that describes the
output of a worker of type $a$ at a firm of type $T.$ Assume that this function is increasing in its arguments. Assume that it is concave in $a$. Similar steps as in
the main body yield under fully revealing messages in analogy to (\ref{equilibrium density}) that 
\begin{equation}\label{generalized-queue-length}
\lambda
_{m}(a)=u^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a)-\phi _{aa}(a,m)/\phi _{a}(a,m),
\end{equation}
with upper and lower end-point conditions $\phi _{a}(\bar{a}%
_{m},m)=u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{m})$ and $e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}
_{m}|m)} \phi_{T}(\underline{a}_{m},m) = u(\underline{a}_{m}).$ The upper
endpoint continues to be strictly higher for higher firm types if $\phi $ is
strictly supermodular in $a$ and $T,$ which was assured in our baseline
specification. The queue length $\lambda _{T}(a)$ was independent of $T$ in
our baseline specification, which made the setup particularly tractable as
it implied that workers choose among firms ``as if'' at random. From the
earlier equation it is apparent that this only continues to apply more
generally if the semi-elasticity of $\phi _{a}$ with respect to worker
ability is independent of $T,$ as is the case for example for productions $ 
\phi(a,T)=\ln (T(a-d_1))+d_2$ or $
\phi(a,T)=f(T)g(a-d_3)+h(T),$
where $d_j$ are
positive constants and $f(.)$, $g(.)$ and $h()$ are non-negative and
increasing functions (and, to preserve concavity in ability, $g(.)$ needs to be concave). We assume this for the rest of this section (things become substantially more involved when the semi-elasticity of $\phi
_{a}$ depends on the firm type, which exceeds the bounds of this paper.) 

It remains to be clarifies when the lower bound on ability $\underline{a}_T$ is higher under for high than for low firm types. To study this, it is easiest to think of $L$ and $H$ as real numbers and to define our production function for all $T' \in [L,H]$ in a way that is twice continuously differentiable, preserves the assumptions we had placed on $\phi(a,T)$ for all $T$, and preserves the true functions $\phi(a,L)$ and $\phi(a,T)$ at the boundaries. For our example, this is for example achieved by setting $\phi(a,T)=\left(v_L + (v_H - v_L) \frac{T-L}{H-L}\right) \left( a - c_L -  (c_H - c_L) \frac{T-L}{H-L} \right)$ so that valuations and costs rise linearly. The advantage of this extension is that we can use calculus to study the question at hand. We show that the lower bound weakly increases in $T$ whenever it is possible to such a function that is weakly increasing, as is the case in the example above.




\begin{prop}
\label{extension} Consider more general production functions $\phi (a,T)$ with a semi-elasticity of $\phi_a$ in $a$ that is independent of firm type. Strict supermodularity (submodularity) of $\phi$ ensures that the highest ability $\bar{a}_T$ is increasing (decreasing) in $T$. Strict log-supermodularity (log-submodularity) of $\phi $ ensures that the
lowest ability type $\underline{a}_T$ is increasing (decreasing) in $T$.
\end{prop}

\noindent Proof of Proposition \ref{extension}: Consider truthful cheap talk. Recall that the the queue length is given by $\lambda(a|T)=u^{\prime
\prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a)-\phi _{aa}(a,T)/\phi _{a}(a,T),$ with
end-point conditions $\phi _{a}(\bar{a}_T,T)=u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{T})$ and $%
e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{T}|T)}\phi(\underline{a}_T,T)=u(\underline{a}%
).$ 

Applying the implicit function theorem to the first endpoint condition and noting that $\phi_aa(a,T)-U''(a)<0$ establishes the comparative statics on $\bar{a}_T$.


Applying the implicit function theorem to the latter and noticing that the denominator is negative (otherwise the types above $\underline{a}_T$ would not have positive surplus and would not enter) implies that the sign of $- \partial \underline{a}_T/\partial T$ equals the sign of

\begin{eqnarray*}
&&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{T}|T)}\phi _{T}(\underline{a}%
_{T},T)+e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{T}|T)}\left[ -\frac{d\Lambda (\underline{%
a}_{T}|T)}{dT}\right] \phi (\underline{a}_{T},T) \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{T}|T)}\phi (\underline{a}_{T},T)\left[ \frac{%
\phi _{T}(\underline{a}_{T},T)}{\phi (\underline{a}_{T},T)}-\lambda (\bar{a}%
_{T}|T)\frac{d\bar{a}_{T}}{dT}\right] \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{T}|T)}\phi (\underline{a}_{T},T)\left[ \frac{%
\phi _{T}(\underline{a}_{T},T)}{\phi (\underline{a}_{T},T)}-\lambda (\bar{a}%
_{T}|T)\left[ -\frac{\phi _{aT}(\bar{a}_{T},T)}{\phi _{aa}(\bar{a}%
_{T},T)-u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{T})}\right] \right] \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{T}|T)}\phi (\underline{a}_{T},T)\left[ \frac{%
\phi _{T}(\underline{a}_{T},T)}{\phi (\underline{a}_{T},T)}-\frac{u^{\prime
\prime }(\bar{a}_{T})-\phi _{aa}(\bar{a}_{T},T)}{\phi _{a}(\bar{a}_{T},T)}%
\left[ \frac{\phi _{aT}(\bar{a}_{T},T)}{ u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{T}) - \phi _{aa}(\bar{a}%
_{T},T)}\right] \right] \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{T}|T)}\phi (\underline{a}_{T},T)\left[ \frac{%
\phi _{T}(\underline{a}_{T},T)}{\phi (\underline{a}_{T},T)}-\frac{\phi _{aT}(%
\bar{a}_{T},T)}{\phi _{a}(\bar{a}_{T},T)}\right]
\end{eqnarray*}%
A sufficient condition for this to be weakly (strictly) negative is that $\phi $ is
weakly (strictly) log-supermodular. In that case 
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\phi _{T}(\underline{a}_{T},T)}{\phi (\underline{a}_{T},T)}-\frac{\phi
_{aT}(\bar{a}_{T},T)}{\phi _{a}(\bar{a}_{T},T)} \leq \frac{\phi _{T}(\bar{a}%
_{T},T)}{\phi (\bar{a}_{T},T)}-\frac{\phi _{aT}(\bar{a}_{T},T)}{\phi _{a}(%
\bar{a}_{T},T)} \leq 0,
\end{equation*}%
where both the first and the second inequality are immediate implications of
strict log-supermodularity. The opposite holds for strict log-submodularity.$%
\square $

\subsection{Incentive Compatibility for More General Production Functions}
\label{online_app:ic_general_prod}

Proposition \ref{prop:incentive_compat} proves that in a candidate equilibrium with truth-telling each type has incentives to reveal the truth when valuations between high and low type firms are sufficiently far apart. This was done for the specific production function $v_T (a-c_t)$.
This also holds for the somewhat broader class of production functions studied in Online Appendix~\ref{online_app:general_production}. To see this, consider again a production function $\phi(a,T)$ on $\mathbb{R}^2_{+}$ that is increasing in both arguments, convex in $a$, and where the semi-elasticity of $\phi_a(a,T)$ with respect to $a$ is constant in $T$. Again assume that for each $T>\underline{T}$ at least some ability has positive surplus, and assume that the lowest firm type for which this arises remains bounded (i.e., for each $T>0$ there exists $a\leq \bar{a}$ such that $\phi(a,T)>u(a)$). Fix some low type $L>\underline{T}$. 

We will show that there is incentive compatibility if the valuation of the high type is sufficiently large. In particular, assume that $\phi_a(a,T)$ at each $a$ increases in $a$ and is unbounded. 

We then obtain the following insight: \textit{ Under the assumptions in this subsection, if high type $T=H$ is sufficiently large, then in a candidate equilibrium where each firm type sends a message equal to its type no firm has incentives to deviate to the other message.}

Establishing this follows the same logic as the proof for the specific production function of the main body of the paper. We highlight the key steps here. First, note that the same derivations that established the differential equation for the bidding function \eqref{wages} in the main body of the paper reveal that the bidding function in equilibrium at message $ m\in\{L,H \} $ is given by 
\begin{equation}
    b_{m}^{\prime }(a) = \phi_a(a,m) - b_{m}(a)\left[ u^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a) - C(a) \right]
\end{equation}
where $C(a):=\phi_{aa}(a,m)/\phi_a{a,m}$ is the semi-elasticity of $\phi_a$ in $a$ and is independent of $T$ by our earlier assumptions. 
Since the bid-equation at the high message becomes increasingly steep with higher $H$, it is easy to show that the set $A$ of worker abilities that would approach high type firms and make bid $b_H(a)$ below the output at the low firm (i.e., $b_H(a)<\phi(a,L)$) has measure that converges to zero and the highest such ability worker remains bounded.\footnote{Recall for this that the queue length of any given worker type is constant in $T$ by \eqref{generalized-queue-length}.} So for a low firm type the profit after a deviation to a high message shrinks to zero, and therefore becomes lower than their profit on the equilibrium path.

For high type firms, the benefit of sending a high message is that  of trading with an ever-expanding set of trading partners (as $H$ increases the upper bounded increases to infinity and the set of active abilities has to increase to infinity as well as otherwise the lower end-point condition could not be satisfied). Trading with ever-larger set of types become eventually more profitable than deviating and trading with the fixed set $\left[\underline{a}_L,\bar{a}_L\right]$. This establishes incentive compatibility for both types when $H$ is sufficiently large.

In Corollary \ref{corollary} we outlined theoretical predictions suggesting that the effects of revealing employer preferences on applicant pool composition depend critically on the source of firm heterogeneity (valuations versus costs). Specifically, when valuation differences dominate sorting should primarily occur at higher quantiles of the applicant ability distribution.

To empirically examine this prediction, we analyze sorting based on deciles of applicant experience (our proxy for ability) by job opening. Note that we are not using a quantile regression; instead, we compare means of quantiles across job openings. \subsubsection{Do Wage Bids Reflect Compensating Differentials?} \label{online_appendix:compensating_differentials}
A high type employer might also be a more demanding employer, expecting greater effort from their hires.
Anticipating these great expectations, workers might bid more, as they know their costs will higher, either from greater effort or perhaps the greater probability of receiving bad feedback.
As such, part of the higher wage bid observed in the high tier could reflect this anticipated greater, costly effort.
Although we have no direct test of this hypothesis, several pieces of evidence make this compensating differential explanation relatively improbable relative to the straightforward perceived willingness to pay argument.

First, the pattern of results in Figure~\ref{fig:choices_by_cat} is suggestive that employers selecting a high tier are not looking for harder work that would require more effort, but rather ``smarter'' work.
A high tier selection is commonplace in highly skilled categories such as web and software development, whereas in categories like a support---which is largely data entry---the most common selection is low tier.
Second, there is little empirical evidence for the notion that vertical preferences reflect higher employer expectations that might manifest in bad feedback if not met. 

Among employers selecting a high tier in the ambiguous arm but not having their preferences revealed, there is no evidence that high tier employers are harsher evaluators.
In Column~(1) of Table~\ref{tab:compensating_differential_argument}, the outcome is the z-score of feedback (on a 1 to 5 point scale).
Controls are included for the job category.
The key independent variable are indicators for the employers (un-revealed) vertical preference---the sample is restricted to the $\textsc{ShownPref} = 0$ cell in the ambiguous arm.
There is no evidence of systematically better or worse feedback scores by tier.

In Column~(2), we report the same regression, but use the z-score of the employer's net promoter score (NPS) for the platform.
Employers are randomly sampled to give a score, so the sample is smaller.
Again, there is no evidence of a tier-related difference.
In Columns~(3) and (4), we still use the NPS measure but expand the sample.
There is no overall effect of revelation on NPS, though there is some evidence of improved scores for employers that had medium- and high-vertical preferences revealed. 

\input{./tables/compensating_differential_argument.tex}

In addition to lack of empirical evidence that workers should ``fear'' high tier employers because of increased expectations, there is little evidence that employers would justifiably think that paying higher wages would have anything but a selection effect: 
\cite{gilchrist20163} shows via a field experiment in an online labor market that higher wages do not lead to greater measurable productivity.
This is consistent with the relatively poor empirical support for persistent gift-exchange effects in labor settings \citep{gneezy2006putting}.

\subsection{Should Workers Consider Changed Applicant Pool Size When Bidding?} \label{supp_app:condition_on_pool_size}
% Not mentioned in the text

As we saw, signal revelation had some effect on applicant pool size, particularly in the low tier.
A natural question is whether these different pool sizes influenced wage bids.
If workers thought they faced less competition, all else equal, they have an incentive to bid up.
In settings where it can be examined, endogenous entry has proven empirically important \citep{bajari2003winner}. However, in contrast to common value auctions, there is presumably a much greater role of idiosyncratic worker-specific surplus in the case of hiring, muting the effects.  

Whether this consideration is important in practice is an empirical question---the competition effects might be sufficiently small that the worker does not have to consider them from a worker's perspective.
To test whether anticipated pool size ``matters,'' we can test what workers do naturally, in the sense that we could consider how they adjust their bidding behavior on a job-to-job basis. 
Ideally we would estimate a regression of the form 
\begin{align} \label{eq:endog_bidding}
  \log w_{ij} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \log A_{j} + \epsilon
\end{align} 
where $w$ is the individual wage bid of worker $i$ to job opening $j$, $\alpha_i$ is an individual worker fixed effect and $A_j$ is the number of applications opening $j$ will receive, which is determined at random.
Of course, in practice, $A_j$ is very likely to be correlated with other factors that could affect the wage bid, such as how attractive or unattractive the job opening is to workers or how quickly a job opening is filled.
However, there are factors that affect how many applications a job opening is likely to receive that is plausibly exogenous with respect to other opening characteristics, and so an instrumental variables approach is feasible. 

To start, we ignore the endogeneity of $A_j$ and simply estimate (\ref{eq:endog_bidding}), reporting the results in Column~(1) of Table~\ref{tab:condition_on_pool_size}.
This regression uses the full set of applications to job openings in the ambiguous arm of the experiment.
We can see that a larger applicant pool is associated with a lower wage bid---a worker bids about 0.36\% less when facing a 10\% larger applicant pool. 

\input{./tables/condition_on_pool_size.tex}

To account of the endogeneity in $A_j$, we construct an instrument. 
We use the mean log applicant pool size of other job openings in that same category, posted on that same day.\footnote{
  This is conceptually similar to the instrument used by \cite{camerer1997labor}. 
}
We include day-specific fixed effects in the second stage.
The identifying assumption is that there is day-to-day variation in the number of jobs posted and the number of workers active that changes the number of applicants per job for exogenous reasons.
In Column~(2), we report the first stage of the IV estimate.
We can see that is a powerful instrument, with a conditional F-statistic of \IVcondFstat{}. 

In Column~(3) report the 2SLS estimate.
We can see that the larger the pool, the lower the wage bid, with an effect size of \PoolSizeIV{}\%. 
As expected, when the applicant pool is larger for plausibly exogenous reasons, a worker bids less.
Despite being negative, the point estimate from Column~(3) implies that that the equilibrium adjustment would be minuscule: 
 for the low tier, where pool size dropped about 5\%, workers would bid up by a bit more than 1/2 of 1\%. 
The implication of these point estimates is that the change in bidding to perceived pool size---while in the expected theoretical direction---is relatively unimportant.

\subsection{Subjective Match Quality}

If buyers are less satisfied, they might leave worse feedback for the worker or the platform.
The effects of revelation on these feedback measures is reported in Figure~\ref{fig:trt_effects_feedback}.
All feedback outcomes are transformed into z-scores, and so point estimates are interpretable as fractions of a standard deviation. 
The top panel is the employer's feedback to the hired worker, the middle panel is the worker's feedback to the hiring employer, and the bottom panel is the feedback of the employer to the platform (framed as a probability of recommending the platform to someone else).  

For the worker-on-employer and employer-on-worker feedback, parties are prompted to give feedback after the conclusion of a contract but are not obligated to, hence the sample of contracts for feedback is smaller than the number of contracts.

From Figure~\ref{fig:trt_effects_feedback}, we can see that there is little change in the feedback to the worker.

  See \cite{luca2021effect} for an interesting paper on how plausibly exogenous changes in the price of a good affect subsequent buyer ratings.
}
