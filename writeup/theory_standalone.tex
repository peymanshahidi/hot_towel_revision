\section{Theory}

\subsection{\textbf{Setup}}

\textit{Players and Production}: There is an endogeneous mass of workers
with heterogeneous abilities $a\in \mathbb{R}_{++}.$ Worker ability is known
and publicly observable. There exists a unit mass of jobs that differ in
their valuation $v_{T}$ of workers' ability: Each job can hire at most one
worker, and a match between job type $T$ and worker ability $a$ produces
output $v_{T}(a-c_{T}),$ where $c_{T}$ allows higher value jobs to have
higher ability requirements (i.e., costs) to achieve positive output. For
simplicity we focus on two types of jobs: $T\in \{H,L\}.$ High types value
ability more: $v_{H}>v_{L}>0,$ and their jobs may have higher requirements: $%
c_{H}>c_{L}>0$. There is a fraction $\delta _{T}$ of each type of job. The
type of the job is \textit{private} information of the firm that posts the
job. We will throughout use the words "job" and "firm" interchangeably.

\textit{Payoffs for market participants:} Both workers and jobs are risk
neutral. If a worker is hired for a job, the firm's profit is the output
minus the wage it pays to the worker. Therefore, a worker of type $a$ who
gets paid wage $b$ at job $T$ generates firm profits:%
\begin{equation}
\pi (a,b|T)=v_{T}(a-c_{T})-b.  \label{firm-profit}
\end{equation}%
A worker who get hired has utility equal to his wage. The utility of jobs
and workers who remain unmatched is normalized to zero.

\textit{Market entry:} Workers have non-negative outside option $u(a)$,
which we assume to be increasing and strictly convex, with Inada conditions $%
u^{\prime }(0)=0$ and $u^{\prime }(a)$ unbounded. For some proofs it will be
convienient to assume that the elasticity of $u^{\prime }$ is bounded away
from zero and infinity. If workers participate in the market they forgo this
outside option and obtain their market payoff instead. There is free entry
such that worker types who enter obtain expected payoff $u(a)$ in the
market, and those who cannot obtain this utility do not enter the market. We
further assume that for each job type $T$ there is at least one worker type $%
a$ for which surplus of entering is strictly positive $%
(i.e.,v_{T}(a-c_{T})-u\left( a\right) >0).$ It will also be convenient to
assume that the difference in requirements between the types of firms is not
too large, and in particular that $c_{H}-c_{L}<u(c_{H})/u^{\prime }(c_{H}).$

%\bigskip

\textit{Market Interaction and Timing}: With\ i.i.d. probability $\rho $
each firm $i$ is asked to choose a message $m_{i}\in \{L,H\},$ where the
message space is without loss of generality given that we only have two
types of firms. With probability $1-\rho $ the firm cannot send a message,
which we denote by $m_{i}=\emptyset .$ For notational convenience let $%
M=\{\emptyset ,L,H\}$, which accounts for the case where the firm cannot
send a signal. Each firm also generates two signals: $s_{1,i}$ and $s_{2,i},$
each of which is drawn from set $\{L,H\}.$ We assume that signals are i.i.d.
conditional on the true firm type $T_{i}.$ The signal coincides with the
true type with probability $\psi _{1}$ and $\psi _{2},$ respectively, with
the obvious requirement that these probability exceed one-half. The first
signal can be easily observed by workers but may be noisy, while the second
is observed only after careful reading and evaluation of the job description
and is extremely precise ($\psi _{2}$ close to 1). It turns out that under
truthful cheap-talk these messages play no role, but they in particular
allow workers some way of conditioning their search and their bids even if
cheap-talk is uninformative or absent.

Workers decide to enter. Those who enter observe the joint distribution of
messages and first signals, and choose a job announcement with desired
message and first signal $(m,s_{1})\in M\times \{L,H\}$. Since they lack
further information at this point, they choose some firm $i$ from the firms
with obserable $(m,s_{1})$ at random. In a large market this randomness
leads to a Poisson distribution of workers at a given firm, with Poisson
parameter equal to ratio of workers to firms at a given $(m,s_{1})$
combination.\footnote{%
This way of introducing search underlies most of the micro-founded work on
directed search, and careful derivations that start with a finite population
and takes limits are presented in \cite{peters_1991}, \cite{peters_1997}, \cite{burdett_etal2001} or the summary in \cite{wright_etal2021}.} The worker then prepares a bid for
this job, and learns its second signal $s_{2,i}.$ At that point the worker
already sunk his preparation time, and cannot choose another firm. S/he
submits a bid $b$ to this job that can condition on the message and both
signals, or chooses to remain unmatched. We assume that workers only submit
a bid if he believes that his bid has strictly positive probability of being
accepted.

Each firm observes all its bids and the ability of the associated worker,
hires the most profitable among these workers and pays a wage equal to this
worker's bid. It can choose to reject all bidders.

\textit{Equilibrium: }Let $\mu (m,T)$ denote the equilibrium probability
that firms of type $T$ send message $m\in \{H,L\}$ conditional on being
selected to send a message. This uniquely determines the probability $\Psi
(T|m,s_{1})$ of facing firm $T$ after observation $(m,s_{1})$ whenever $m$
is played with positive probability. If $m$ is not played with positive
probability, $\Psi (.)$ is a belief that agents hold in case a firm sends
such a message off-equilibrium. Given such beliefs, Bayes' rule generates
the joint probability $\Psi (s_{2},T|m,s_{1})$ of the second signal and
type, and a conditional belief $\Psi (T|m,s_{1},s_{2})$ after observing all
signals.

The easiest way to sum up the workers behavior is through the ratio $\Lambda
(a,b_{L},b_{H}|m,s_{1})$ of workers with ability weakly above $a$ who will
bid weakly above $b_{L}$ after a low second signal and above $b_{H}$ after a
high second signal, per job with initial observable $(m,s_{1}).$ This is
also called the queue length of workers. Sometimes it is useful to focus on
the marginal $\Lambda _{a}(a|m,s_{1})$ of workers with ability above $a$ per
firm with observable $(m,s_{1}),$ or on the conditional $\Lambda
(a,b|m,s_{1},s_{2})$ that provides the ratio of workers with ability-bid
above $(a,b)$ to firms with observables $(m,s_{1},s_{2}).$

From the perspective of a firm with type $T$ and observables $%
(m,s_{1},s_{2}) $ this generates a queue of workers bidding weakly more than 
$\pi $ according to 
\begin{equation*}
\Gamma (\pi |m,s_{1},s_{2},T)=\int_{\{a,b|\pi (a,b|T)\geq \pi \}}d\Lambda
(\cdot |m,s_{1},s_{2}).
\end{equation*}%
We denote by $\Gamma _{+}(\pi |.)$ the queue of workers that bid strictly
more than $\pi ,$ and the difference $\bigtriangleup (\pi |m,s_{1},s_{2},T)$
describes the mass of workers who offer exactly the same profit.$\footnote{%
The definition is $\Gamma _{+}(\pi |m,s_{1},s_{2},T)=\int_{\{a,b|\pi
(a,b|T)>\pi }d\Lambda (\cdot |m,s_{1},s_{2}).$}$\ We will later rule out the
existence of such mass points in equilibrium. The chance that no worker
offers a profit weakly above $\pi $ under a Poisson distribution has
probability $\exp (-\Gamma (\pi |.)),$ so the chance of having at least one
worker who offers such profit is the complement. So a firm $T$'s profit from
sending signal $m$ is 
\begin{equation}
\Pi (m|T)=\sum_{s_{1},s_{2}}\left[ \psi (s_{1},s_{2}|T)\int_{\pi \geq
0,s_{1},s_{2}}\pi d(1-e^{-\Gamma (\pi |m,s_{1},s_{2},T)})\right] ,
\label{profits}
\end{equation}%
where $\psi (s_{1},s_{2}|T)$ denotes the probability of signals given the
firm type. For a worker of type $a$ who considers a job with observation $%
(m,s_{1})$ intending to bid $b_{L}$ and $b_{H}$ after observing the second
signal obtains expected utility%
\begin{equation}
V(m,s_{1},a,b_{L},b_{H})=\sum_{s_{2},T}\Psi (s_{2},T|m,s_{1})e^{-\Gamma
_{+}(\pi (a,b|T)|m,s_{1},s_{2},T)}M(\bigtriangleup (\pi
(a,b|T)|m,s_{1},s_{2},T))b_{s_{2}}\boldsymbol{1}[\pi (a,b|T)\geq 0].
\label{value}
\end{equation}%
The first term in the summation on the right is the probability of facing
signal $s_{2}$ and firm type $T.$ The second term in the square bracket
ensures that no worker offering strictly higher profits is present at this
firm. The third term $M(\bigtriangleup )$ accounts for a mass other workers
with exactly the same offer, and is strictly below 1 if there is a mass of
competitors ($\bigtriangleup >0$) and is exactly 1 otherwise.\footnote{%
The probability that the worker is hired conditional on no other worker with
strictly higher profit being present consists of several elements:
probability $e^{-\bigtriangleup }$ that no worker with equal profit is
present, the probability $\bigtriangleup e^{-\bigtriangleup }\frac{1}{2}$
that exactly one other worker with equal profit is present but the other
worker is not chosen, the probability $\frac{\bigtriangleup
^{2}e^{-\bigtriangleup }}{2}\frac{1}{3}$ that two other workers with equal
profit is present but the other workers are not chosen, and so forth. The probability
of being hired (conditional on no strictly more profitable bidders) is then
the infinite sum $M(\bigtriangleup )=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty }\frac{\bigtriangleup
^{k}e^{-\bigtriangleup }}{k!}\frac{1}{k+1}=\frac{1-e^{-\bigtriangleup }}{%
\bigtriangleup }.$
See \cite{burdett_etal2001} and \cite{wright_etal2021} for further references.} The forth term $b_{s_{2}}\in
\{b_{L},b_{H}\}$ captures the payoff of the worker if he is hired, which is
equal his wage bid. Finally, firms only accept offers that generate
non-negative profits, which is captured by the indicator function which
takes the value 1 if its argument is true and is zero otherwise.

\begin{definition*}[Equilibrium Definition]
An equilibrium is consists of firm behavior, worker behavior and beliefs ($%
\mu (\cdot ),\Lambda (\cdot ),\Psi (\cdot ))$ such that

(1) beliefs $\Psi (\cdot )$ are consistent with from Bayes' rule according
to firm behavior $\mu (\cdot ),$

(2) firms maximize profits such that $\Pi (m|T)=\max_{m^{\prime }}\Pi
(m^{\prime },T)$ when $\mu (m,T)>0,$ and

(3) workers' decisions to enter and bid are optimal such that $%
V(m,s_{1},a,b_{L},b_{H})\leq u(a)$ for all $(m,s_{1},a,b_{L},b_{H}),$ and
this holds with equality if $(a,b_{L},b_{H})$ is in the support of $\Lambda
(\cdot |m,s_{1}).$
\end{definition*}

The last condition ensures that there are no jobs that offer strictly higher
returns to workers than their outside option, and those that do enter obtain
exactly their outside option.

We conclude with the immediate obseration that there cannot be meaningful
mass points in equilibrium, i.e., mass points at firm types that have
non-zero probability. Workers at the mass point have strictly positive
probability that another worker with exactly the same profit will be hired
instead of them. A marginally lower wage bid (i.e., a marginally higher
profit for the firm) would have ensured to be hired, inducing a profitable
upward jump in the hiring probability. This rules out that mass points can
be optimal for workers, which simplifies the notation going forward.

\begin{lemma*}
\label{no-mass-point-lemma}There are no meaningful mass points in
equilibrium, i.e., $\bigtriangleup (\pi |m,s_{1},s_{2},T)=0$ for all $\pi
\geq 0$ and all $(m,s_{1},s_{2},T)$ with $\Psi (s_{2},T|m,s_{1})>0$.
\end{lemma*}

Proof: See Appendix \ref{sec:proofs}.

\subsection{Analysis and Empirical Predictions\label{sec:analysis}}

\textit{Firms with messages:} To understand this market, consider first the
firms that can send a cheap-talk message. Their message space is $\{L,H\}.$
As we will see, the following analysis is valid whether these firms
constitute the entire market (i.e., $\rho =1$) or just a part of the market
(i.e., $0<\rho <1).$ Recall that firms that are chosen for a message have to
declare a type. Assume for now that these firms declare their type
truthfully in equilibrium. To investigate the market outcome for these
firms, we assume truthful messages ($\mu (T|T)=1)$ and impose equilibrium
conditions (1) and (3). After characterizing this, we additionally impose
equilibrium condition (2) on firm optimality to study the conditions under
which firms would be willing to report truthfully. In the subsequent
subsection we study the firms that cannot send a cheap-talk message.

Under the assumption that firms report truthfully, workers do not rely on
signals to infer the firm type. That is, $\Psi (T|T,s_{1},s_{2})=1$ for all $%
s_{1}$ and $s_{2},$ and it is easy to show that there is no benefit from
conditioning the visit strategies or the bids on the signal. In the
following we will suppress reference to the signals, i.e., $b=b_{H}=b_{L}$
and we write $\Lambda (a,b|m)$ instead of $\Lambda (a,b,b|m,s_{1}).$ We can
now express the value for a worker of ability $a$ who bids $b$ at a job with
message $m\in \{L,H\}$ (no matter what the signals are) by 
\begin{equation}
V(m,a,b)=e^{-\Gamma (\pi (a,b|m)|m)}b\boldsymbol{1}[\pi (a,b|m)\geq 0].
\label{value-truth}
\end{equation}%
Since the worker infers perfectly which firm it is facing, instead of
bidding wage $b$ we can reformulate the problem as one of leaving profit $%
\pi \geq 0$ to the firm and keeping the output minus this profit as pay. For
worker-firm pairs that have strictly positive output so that $%
v_{m}(a-c_{m})\geq \pi \geq 0$ this delivers expected utility 
\begin{equation}
W(m,a,\pi )=e^{-\Gamma (\pi |m)}\left[ v_{m}(a-c_{m})-\pi \right] .
\label{value-profit-truth}
\end{equation}%
Clearly $V(m,a,b)=W(m,a,\pi (a,b|m)).$ By equilibrium condition (3), if
workers with ability $a$ and bid $b$ enter at message $m$ - i.e. if $(a,b)$
is in the support of $\Lambda (\cdot |m)$ - then $b$ and must maximize (\ref%
{value-truth}). But then $\pi (a,b|m)$ must be weakly positive and maximizes
(\ref{value-profit-truth}).

We can therefore study the problem where the worker's choice variable is $%
\pi ,$ which is chooses to maximize (\ref{value-profit-truth}). This
objective is supermodular in $(a,\pi )$, indicating that it is less costly
for high ability types to offer high profits to firms. It implies that
higher ability workers at a given message leave higher profits in
equilibrium. And since there are no mass points, this means that they leave
strictly higher profits almost everywhere.

For ease of notation, let 
\begin{equation}
U(a|m):=\arg \max_{\pi }W(m,a,\pi )  \label{value-profit-truth2}
\end{equation}%
denote the equilibrium utility for worker type $a$ that approaches message $%
m,$ and free entry then requires that $U(a|m)\leq u(a),$ with equality if $a$
is in the support of $\lambda (a|m).$

We can use this together with the definition of (\ref{value-profit-truth})
to prove that at a given message $m$ the worker abilities that enter in
equilibrium form a non-empty interval $[\underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}],$ and
no single type enters with positive mass (see proof in Appendix \ref{sec:proofs}). The
reason is that there is no mass point in terms of profits that are offered
(see earlier Lemma) and for each profit level there is a
different ability type that finds it optimal to enter.

So the task is to characterize the non-empty interval $[\underline{a}_{m},%
\bar{a}_{m}].$ Within this interval, consider a worker with ability $a$ that
leaves profit $\pi $ at message $m$ in equilibrium.\footnote{%
Formally this means that there exists $(a,b)$ in the support of $\Lambda
(a,b|m)$ such that $\pi =\pi (a,b|m).$} Then the marginal increase in
equilibrium utility with respect to ability is%
\begin{equation}
e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}v_{m}=u^{\prime }(a).  \label{first derivative}
\end{equation}%
The right side follows from differentiation of (\ref{value-profit-truth})
with respect to ability, where any change in profits can be neglected by the
envelope condition, and then subsituting $\Lambda (a|m)$ for $\Gamma (\pi
|m) $ which follows since higher types leave higher profits. The right hand
side has to hold because the equilibrium utility equals the forgone entry
cost. Differentiating with respect to ability leads to 
\begin{equation}
\lambda (a|m)e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}v_{m}=u^{\prime \prime }(a)
\label{second derivative}
\end{equation}%
where $\lambda (a|m)=-d\Lambda (a|m)/da$ is akin to a "density" of type $a$
workers queueing at this message. Dividing this by (\ref{first derivative})
yields 
\begin{equation}
\lambda (a|m)=\frac{u^{\prime \prime }(a)}{u^{\prime }(a)},
\label{equilibrium density}
\end{equation}%
which ties down the type density at any ability level. The key question
concerns the end-points of the interval. Clearly at the highest type (\ref%
{first derivative}) reduces to the end-point condition%
\begin{equation}
v_{m}=u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{m}),  \label{equilibrium highest type}
\end{equation}%
which uniquely determines $\bar{a}_{m}.$ One can then recover the lowest
ability type that approaches message $m$ since at any candidate $\underline{a%
}_{m}$ the queue length $\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)=\int_{a^{\prime }}^{%
\bar{a}_{m}}\lambda (a|m)da\,\ $is uniquely determined by (\ref{equilibrium
density}) and (\ref{equilibrium highest type}). Moreover, the lowest ability
type also leaves the lowest profits, which implies that he leaves zero
profits (assume not, then this type could lower his bid so he leaves zero
profits and still win with the same probability). Then it has to hold that 
\begin{equation}
e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}v_{m}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})=u(%
\underline{a}_{m}),  \label{equilibrium lowest type}
\end{equation}%
which uniquely ties down $\underline{a}_{m}.$

Finally, we briefly consider how wage bids $b_{m}(a)$ are determined for
worker $a\in \lbrack \underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}]$. In equilibrium, the
utility of such a worker is $e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}b_{m}(a)$. We can totally
differentiate this with respect to the worker type, and set it equal to $%
u^{\prime }(a)$ which needs to hold due to free entry. Using (\ref{first
derivative}) this reduces after cancellation to the following differential
equation: 
\begin{eqnarray}
b_{m}^{\prime }(a) &=&v_{m}-b_{m}(a)\lambda (a|m)  \notag \\
&=&v_{m}-b_{m}(a)u^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a)  \label{wages}
\end{eqnarray}%
which together with end-point condition that the lowest type extracts all
suprlus: 
\begin{equation}
b_{m}(\underline{a}_{m})=v_{m}[\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m}]
\label{wages boundary}
\end{equation}%
determines the wage bids uniquely. To sum up:

\begin{prop}
\label{equilibrium-characterization}Under truthful cheap talk, the interval
of worker types $[\underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}]$ that approach message $%
m\in \{L,H\}$ and the queue $\Lambda (a|m)$ are fully determined by $(\ref%
{equilibrium density})-(\ref{equilibrium lowest type}),$ and the wages they
ask for are fully determined by (\ref{wages}) and (\ref{wages boundary}). On 
$[\underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}]$ the wages for such a firm with abritrary $%
v_{m}$ and $c_{m}$ can be expressed relative to the wages $b_{1}(.)$ of a
"normalized" firm with valuation=0 of cost=lowest-type=0 according to 
\begin{equation}
b_{m}(a)=v_{m}[b_{1}(a)-b_{1}(\underline{a}_{m})+\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m}].
\label{bidding1}
\end{equation}
\end{prop}

\bigskip

It is worth noting that Proposition (\ref{equilibrium-characterization})
fully characterizes the application and bidding behavior that firms face
when they send messages, and that hold irrespective of whether all firms
send such messages ($\rho =1)$ or only some fraction of the firms sends such
messages ($\rho <1).$ The characterization makes no reference to the number
of firms that have the ability to message, nor to the behavior at firms that
do not. This property of directed search models with entry is known in the
literature as "block-recursiveness", and comes about since workers trade-off
how to much to enter according to the number of firms that are present in
the market. Since workers can choose which firms to approach, for those who
approach firms that send messgages it is not important what happens at firms
that do not send messages. We will now turn to those firms.

\bigskip

\textit{Firms without messages:} The firms that cannot send a message could
be in a different market where cheap-talk is impossible (i.e., $\rho =0)$,
or they could be in the same market as the firms that can send messages
(i.e., $0<\rho <1).$ Workers can only rely on the signals about these firms
when they decide which firm to approach and which wage bid to place. For
ease of analysis and to be able to leverage previous derivations, assume
that the second signal is so informative as to eliminate uncertainty about
the firm type (i.e., $\psi _{2}=1)$. One can show by continuity that the
insights in this section also holds when the second message is not fully
revealing, as long as it is sufficiently precise. Assuming perfect precision
of the first signal allows us to consider the consequences of the first
message, and enables us to draw heavily on the insights already developed
earlier.

Now consider workers who see signal $s_{1}$ and believe that the fraction of
low firms at this signal is $\Psi (L|s_{1})$ where we suppressed the message
because it is absent ($m=\emptyset ).$ The fraction with high type is
evidently the complement. Note that these fractions are exogeneously given;
for example, $\Psi (L|L)=\psi _{1}\delta _{L}/(\psi _{1}\delta _{L}+(1-\psi
_{1})\delta _{H}),$ and do not depend on the equilibrium interaction.

It turns out that workers approach these firms similar to firms whose type
is the average valuation given their believes. That is, they approach firms
as if they know that their productivity is averaged according to 
\begin{equation*}
v_{\Psi }=\Psi v_{L}+(1-\Psi )v_{H},
\end{equation*}%
where $\Psi =\Psi (L|s_{1})$ represents the belief that the firm is of low
type given its signal. Workers also approach firms as if they had the
average cost, though the exact expression for this requires some
re-arranging. We used to write production as $v(a-c),$ which evidently
equals $va-vc.$ So in this new setting worker expect the average of $vc$ as
the second term. If we want to write production in expectation as $v_{\Psi
}(a-c_{\Psi }),$ we have to define%
\begin{equation*}
c_{\Psi }=\frac{\Psi v_{L}c_{L}+(1-\Psi )v_{H}c_{H}}{\Psi v_{L}+(1-\Psi
)v_{H}},
\end{equation*}%
where $c_{\Psi }$ is in $[c_{L},c_{H}]$ and monotonically increases from $%
c_{L}$ to $c_{H}$ when $\Psi $ decreases from one to zero. Workers with
types in $[\underline{a}_{\Psi },\bar{a}_{\Psi }]$ then approach such firms,
and their bids can are then conditional on the second signals that reveals
whether the firm is of low or high type. Bids are still driven by (\ref%
{bidding1}), except that the lowest ability type that extracts all surplus
is determined by the first signal and not the second$.$ We therefore have:

\begin{prop}
\label{equilibrium-notalk}Firms with $m=\emptyset $ and signal $s_{1}$
induce belief $\Psi =\Psi (L|s_{1}).$ The interval of worker types $[%
\underline{a}_{s_{1}},\bar{a}_{s_{1}}]$ that approach them and the queue $%
\Lambda (a|s_{1})$ are fully determined by $(\ref{equilibrium density})-(\ref%
{equilibrium lowest type})$ when using for the valuation the average given
by $v_{\Psi }$ and for the cost one uses $c_{\Psi }.$ After the second
signal $s_{2}$ these workers bid according to%
\begin{equation}
b_{s_{2}}(a)=v_{s_{2}}[b_{1}(a)-b_{1}(\underline{a}_{s_{1}})+\underline{a}%
_{s_{1}}-c_{s_{2}}].  \label{wages-general}
\end{equation}
\end{prop}

Proof idea: The key challenge is to prove that workers who approach a firm
will actually always end up bidding there, which is not obvious as they
approach a firm under uncertainty and learn additional information from the
second signal. Under our assumptions this turns out to be true. The
averaging then obtain exactly because the second signal is so precise, and
does not obtain otherwise, as we elaborate in the discussion section. For
the full proof see Appendix \ref{sec:proofs}.$\square $\bigskip

For the wage equation it is worth noting that the wage conditions on the
true valuation and costs $(v_{s_{2}},c_{s_{2}})$ revealed through the second
signal, but the lowest type $\underline{a}_{\Psi }$ who extracts all surplus
remains the one that expects average quality. This is because the decision
which firms add to read in detail is made when the worker has less
information.

\bigskip

\textit{Comparing firms with and without messages:} In order to understand
the empirical implication of the results, we will now compare firms. For
this, we assume that the empiricist has elicited the true firm type both for
the firms that send messages and those that do not. There are many ways of
achieving this, and in reality the message can take many forms. For example,
one could ask firms a fictitious question: Do you prefer ("rookie", \$) or
("expert", \$\$\$)? Firms might interpret this as choosing between two
combinations of ability and wage-bid: $(a_{1},b_{1})$ or $(a_{2},b_{2})$
with $a_{1}<a_{2}$ and $b_{1}<b_{2}.$ If there are no consequences to the
question, firms might simply select the option that would generate more
profits, and if two firms choose differently, then the $L$ type chooses the
first and the $H$ type the second. So the answer to this question would
reveal the type. In the empirical implementation we carefully discuss how we
make firms comparable.

To compare firms, it is useful to study the comparative statics on the types
of workers who approach these firms, and their bidding. The worker types are
governed by the valuation $v$ and costs $c,$ which are equal to the true
type for firms with truthful messages and equal to the average (interim)
type given after workers have observed the first signal for firms without
messages.

\begin{corollary}
\label{corollary}Consider a firm with interim expected valuation $v$ and cost 
$c.$

\begin{itemize}
\item The maximal worker type $\bar{a}$ strictly increases in valuation $v$
(but does not depend on $c$).

\item The minimal worker type $\underline{a}$ strictly increases in the
threshold $c$ (but does not depend on $v$).

\item For interior worker types in $(\underline{a}, \bar{a})$ the density $\lambda (a|.)$ is independent of $v$ or $c$.
\end{itemize}
\end{corollary}

Proof: The first point follows directly from (\ref{equilibrium highest type}%
), which depends on the firm's valuation but not its threshold for ability.
The third point follows from (\ref{equilibrium density}), which depends on
neither valuation nor threshold. The second point states that the lowest type $\underline{a}_{m}$ is only a
function of the threshold, but not of the valuation. This is not directly
obvious from (\ref{equilibrium lowest type}), but in Appendix \ref{sec:proofs} we show
that for a given $\underline{a}_{m}$ the utility at this ability stays
constant when $v_{m}$ changes when one adjusts for the associated changes in
the queue $\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m):$ When the valuation increases more
higher types are added to leave the utility at the low end exactly
unchanged. Because of this feature one can find a different way of
constructing the lowest type. First, define as $\underline{v}%
_{m}(a)=u(a)/[a-c_{m}].$ This is the lowest valuation at which type $a$
workers could enter and break even. Then let $\underline{a}_{m}=\arg \min 
\underline{v}_{m}(a),$ i.e., it is the ability level that needs the lowest
valuation to make entry possible. Using the first order condition for this
minimization reveals that the lowest type can be solved for as the unique
solution to%
\begin{equation}\label{cutoff-type}
\underline{a}_{m}=c_{m}+\frac{u(\underline{a}_{m})}{u^{\prime }(\underline{a}%
_{m})}.
\end{equation}%
This type always enters when $v_{m}>\underline{v}_{m}(\underline{a}_{m}),$
but the higher $v_{m}$ the more other types enter. The lowest type $%
\underline{a}_{m}$ is increasing in $c_{m},$ which follows by implicitly
differentiating (\ref{cutoff-type}).$\square $

\bigskip

One can directly apply these comparative statics noting that $v$ and $c$ are
lowest for firms with a truthful low message, higher for firms without
message but a low first signal, even higher for firms without a message but
a high first signal, and highest for firms with a truthful high message.
That means that both the lower and the upper bound of the interval $[%
\underline{a},\bar{a}]$ of workers who approach these firms is increasing in
this order. Whenever these interval overlap across firms with different
messages/signals, those workers type that overlap generate the same queue at
different firms according to (\ref{equilibrium density}). This means that
they can implement this queue length by choosing among these firms at
random. An individual worker might not choose at random, but the outcome is
indistinguishable from one where they do.

Figure \ref{messages} illustrates graphically how workers choose between

Panel A illustrates a market where firms can send messages, and do so

\prime }=\underline{a}_{m=H},$ so that the interval between indicates the

_{m=H},\bar{a}_{m=H}]$. High worker types again are fully directed, this

distribution of worker abilities at high message firms first order
stochastically dominates that at low message firms.

also send this signal. Between the high and low signal firms, the workers

the types that visit at random are a strict superset.

As mentioned earlier, the same insight carry over even if firms without

queue length is the same at all of them, so they can choose between them at
random to implement this. They do not apply to low message firms, though.

If one conditions on high firms in the right picture, most of them get
applicants according to the high signal but some get applicants according to
the low signal. Both are first order stochastically dominated by the
distribution of worker abilities that high message firms obtain. For low
type firms the opposite holds.

What is the cost of attracting higher worker types? Since they are producing
more output, they are generally more attractive. Without a counteracting
force all firms who can send messages might pretend to be high types (we
will discuss the exact incentives for truth-telling at the end of this
chapter). It turns out that wage demands go up. Not only because workers
with higher ability tend to ask for higher wages, but - more importantly -
because the same worker tends to ask for more. This is shown in the
following comparative statics.

\begin{corollary}
\label{corollary 2}Consider a firm with type $T$ and associated valuation $%
v_{T}$ and cost $c_{T}$ that attracts workers with abilities in $[\underline{%
a},\bar{a}].$ For a given worker with ability $a,$ his wage bid at this firm
increases in $v$, decreases in $c,$ and increases in $\underline{a}.$
\end{corollary}

Proof: Follows directly from (\ref{wages-general}).$\square $

\bigskip

For a given firm type, the identity of the lowest worker type matters. This
worker type extracts all surplus. Workers slightly above this type still
extract nearly all surplus. So firms only retain surplus and thus face low
wages if the worker in question is sufficiently far from the lowest worker.
The further that distance, the lower the wage. In our context this means
that a given firm faces lower wages the lower $\underline{a}_{s_{1}}.$ This
is lowest for low message firms, increases for low signal firms, increases
further for high signal firms, and it highest for high message firms. So if
one compares a high type firm that sends a truthful message with a high type
firm that cannot send a message, for any given worker his/her wage demand is
slightly lower in the no-message firm in the event of a high first signal
and substantially lower in the event of a low first signal. On the contrary,
if one compares a low type firm that can send a truthful message with a high
type firm that cannot, for any given worker his/her wage demand is slightly
higher in the no-message firm in the event of a low first signal and
substantially higher in the event of a high first signal. So for high types
wages increase if they can send a message, while they decrease at low type
firms.

The next proposition collects these insights:

\begin{prop}
\label{comparison1} The following compares firms that engage in truthful
cheap-talk with otherwise identical counterparts that cannot.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Ability sorting: Truthful cheap talk induces more assortative
matching, in the sense that the distribution of ability for a randomly
selected applicant increases (decreasing) in first order stochastic
dominance for high (low) firm types.

\item Number of applications: In general the effect on the number of
applications is theoretically ambigous. For given $v_{L},c_{L},c_{H}$ and $%
u(.),$ if $v_{H}$ is sufficiently large than truthful cheap talk reduces the
applications at low-type firms relative to their "no-message" counterparts.
Across all firms, applications are lower for firms with messages than for
firms without. For $v_{H}$ is close to $v_{L},$ cheap-talk increases the
number of applications at low message-firms, without clear implications for
applications across all firms with messages relative to those without.

\item Wage bidding: Any given worker type bids less at low firm types under
truthful cheap talk compared to the absence of cheap talk. The average wage
demand at such firms and the average wage of the selected worker fall under
truthful cheap talk. The opposite holds for high firm types.

\item Efficiency: Social surplus per firm is higher at firms who send
truthful messages than at their counterparts who cannot send messages.
\end{enumerate}
\end{prop}

Proof: Part 1 and 3 follow directly from Corollary \ref{corollary} and \ref%
{corollary 2}. For parts 2 and 4 see Appendix \ref{sec:proofs}. Intuitively, the number
of applicants is ambigous as cheap-talk moves both the upper and lower bound
of the types that approach a firm, and it depends which shift is more
significant. For the surplus, note that the equilibrium implements the
planner's allocation given the information frictions, but a planner can
assign worker types in a more targeted way if he has more information about
the firm type.$\square $

\bigskip

This central proposition provides the predictions which will guide our
investigation in the empirical exercise which follows. 

To conclude the theoretical section, it is worth noting
that our entire exposition was built around the notion that firms send truthful messages. If low firm types would pretend to be high types, they would attract better workers, but each of these workers would also raise their wage. We show in Proposition \ref{prop: incentive-combat} in Appendix \ref{sec:proofs} that sufficient differences in valuations indeed imply the
existence of an equilibrium with truthful cheap talk. As in
many cheap-talk games, this is generally not the case when payoffs of high
and low types are very close together, as we also show in Proposition \ref{prop: incentive-combat}. Comfortingly, we can not only investigate proxies for truth-telling in the empirical exercise, but part 2 of Proposition \ref{comparison1} also entails distinct testable implications for low vs high differences in valuations that can be confronted with data. We relegate to Appendix \ref{sec:app-discussion} a further discuss of robustness of our theory along three dimensions: (1) Also in partially revealing equilibria firms with messages inherit the same comparative statics outlined in Proposition \ref{comparison1} relative firms without messages. (2) Despite the precision-assumption of the second signal it is intended to approximate environments where some noise remains after seeing all signals. (3) Our environment extends to other production functions, including those that are strictly log-supermodular between $a$ and $v$, where differences in costs between firms are not needed to induce workers with low abilities to avoid high value firms.

\section{Proofs} \label{sec:proofs}

\textit{Proof of Lemma%\ref{no-mass-point-lemma}
:} Assume to the contrary
that $\bigtriangleup (\pi |m,s_{1},s_{2},T)>0$ at a point where $\Psi
(s_{2},T|m,s_{1})>0$ and $\pi \geq 0$. Then there must exist $%
(a,b_{L},b_{H}) $ in the support of $\Lambda (\cdot |m,s_{1})$ with $\pi
(a,b_{s_{2}}|T)=\pi .$ In fact, there must exist such combination with $%
b_{s_{2}}>0:$ Clearly $b_{s_{2}}\geq 0$ as otherwise the worker would make a
loss when hired and would improve his utility by raising the wage demand at
least to zero. It is not possible to have a mass of workers offering the
same $\pi $ and all offering $b_{s_{2}}=0,$ as in this case only a single
worker type solves (\ref{firm-profit}), and a single worker type does not
create a mass point.

Now that we know that $b_{s_{2}}>0,$ it is easy to see that this is not
optimal for the worker to demand this wage. If the worker chooses this
demand, the fact that $\bigtriangleup (\pi )>0$ implies that $%
M(\bigtriangleup (\pi ))<1$, where for ease of readability we suppressed
that this all conditions on $(m,s_{1},s_{2},T).$ Alternatively, we can find
for any $\varepsilon >0$ a bid $b_{s_{2}}^{\prime }\in
(b_{s_{2}}-\varepsilon ,b_{s_{2}})$ slightly below the original one with
associated profit $\pi ^{\prime }>\pi $ such that $\bigtriangleup (\pi
^{\prime })=0.$ This is due to the fact that only a countable number of
points can have a positive mass. This implies $M(\bigtriangleup (\pi
^{\prime }))=1,$ and the hiring probability captured by the third term in (%
\ref{value}) jumps up discretely relative to the original wage demand$.$ The
second term in (\ref{value}) weakly increases because $\pi ^{\prime }>\pi ,$
and the first and fifth term do not change. So the only negative comes from
the forth term that captures the wage conditional on getting hired. Since $%
b_{s_{2}}^{\prime }$ can be made arbitrarily close to $b_{s_{2}},$ the loss
in the wage can be made arbitrarily small and, thus, less than the gain from
the discrete jump in the hiring probability. $\square $

\bigskip \bigskip

\noindent \textit{Subsection \ref{sec:analysis} entailed the following claim that we
prove here in the appendix: Consider firms that can send message }$m\in
\{L,H\}$\textit{\ in a truthful equilibrium. At a given message }$m$\textit{%
\ the worker abilities that enter in equilibrium form a non-empty interval }$%
[\underline{a}_{m},\bar{a}_{m}],$\textit{\ and no single worker type enters
with positive mass.}

Proof: We will first show that there is a positive mass of workers that
enter and approach message $m.$ Assume not, and consider worker type $a$
with $v_{T}(a-c_{T})-u\left( a\right) >0.$ This worker could enter, approach
firms with message $m,$ and bid $b=v_{m}(a-c_{m}).$ Since he would be hired
for sure as no better workers are present at any firm with such message, he
obtains $V(m,a,b)>u(a),$ contradicting the third equilibrium condition.

Next, we rule out that only a single type $a$ with mass $\delta >0$
approaches firms with messages $m$. Assume this were true. Since we have
proven that the distribution of profits that is offered has no mass point,
they have to offer different profits in equilibrium. Let $\pi _{l}$ be the
infimum and $\pi _{h}$ the supremum of such profits. We know by free entry
that $u(a)=e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{l}|m)}\left[ v_{m}(a-c_{m})-\pi _{l}\right]
=e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{h}|m)}\left[ v_{m}(a-c_{m})-\pi _{h}\right] =:U(a|m),$
where the last term denotes the utility conditional on entering and
approaching firms with message $m.$ Let $U_{-}(a|m)$ be the left derivative
and $U_{-}(a|m)$ be the right derivative of $U(a|m)$ with respect to $a.$ We
can apply the envelop theorem to obtain:%
\begin{eqnarray*}
U_{-}^{\prime }(a|m) &=&e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{l}|m)}v_{m} \\
U_{+}^{\prime }(a|m) &=&e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{h}|m)}v_{m}
\end{eqnarray*}%
We also know that $u^{\prime }(a)\leq U_{-}^{\prime }(a|m),$ since types $%
a^{\prime }$ below $a$ have $u(a^{\prime })\geq U(a^{\prime }|m)$ as
required by the third equilibrium condition and in order to reach $%
u(a)=U(a|m)$ the utility after entering has to rise at least as fast as the
utility of the outside option. But since $U_{-}^{\prime }(a|m)<U_{+}^{\prime
}(a|m),$ we obtain $U(a^{\prime \prime }|m)>u(a^{\prime \prime })$ for types 
$a^{\prime \prime }$ that are larger but close to $a.$ This yields a
contradiction to the third equilibrium condition.

Finally we will show that the set of abilities that approach message $m$ is
an interval. Assume not. Then there exist $a_{l}$ and $a_{h}>a_{l}$ that
approach firms with message $m$ but no firms inbetween (i.e., $a_{l}$ and $%
a_{h}$ are in the support of $\Lambda (a|m)$ but no $a\in (a_{l},a_{h})$ is
in the support). Let $\pi _{h}$ be the (supremum of) profits offered by type 
$a_{l},$ and $\pi _{h}$ the (infimum of) profits offered by $a_{h}$ to firms
with message $m.$ Since profits are rising in ability, we have $\pi _{l}\leq
\pi _{h}.$ Free entry implies $u(a_{l})=e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{l}|m)}\left[
v_{m}(a_{l}-c_{m})-\pi _{l}\right] $ and $u(a_{h})=e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{h}|m)}%
\left[ v_{m}(a_{h}-c_{m})-\pi _{h}\right] .$

Free entry also means for types $a\in (a_{l},a_{h})$ that their utility from
entering and approaching message $m$ needs to be weakly lower than the
outside option: $U(a^{\prime }|m)\leq u(a).$ Together with the previous
equalities this requires $U^{\prime }(a_{l}|m)=e^{-\Gamma (\pi
_{l}|m)}v_{m}\leq u^{\prime }(a_{l})$ and $U^{\prime }(a_{h}|m)=e^{-\Gamma
(\pi _{h}|m)}v_{m}\geq u^{\prime }(a_{h}).$ Since the outside option is
strictly concave, this implies $e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{h}|m)}>e^{-\Gamma (\pi
_{l}|m)}.$ But since the distribution of profits has no mass points and
there are no bids between $\pi _{h}$ and $\pi _{l},$ it holds that $\Gamma
(\pi _{h}|m)=\Gamma (\pi _{l}|m),$ yielding a contradiction. $\square $

\bigskip \bigskip

\noindent \textit{Proof of Proposition \ref{equilibrium-notalk}:} Consider firms that
cannot send a cheap-talk message. Given that we assumed precise second
signals, we can treat $s_{2}$ and the true type $T$ interchangeably, and for
easy of exposition we will only use the type $T$ here. Since we only have
one other signal left, we can simply write $s$ instead of $s_{1}.$ As long
as we consistently refer to the same signal, we might also omit the signal
from the notation. Now consider workers who see signal $s$ and believe that
the fraction of low (high) firms at this signal is $\Psi (L|s)$ ($\Psi
(H|s)) $ where we suppressed the message because it is absent ($m=\emptyset
).$ Note that these fractions are exogeneously given; for example, $\Psi
(L|L)=\psi _{1}\delta _{L}/(\psi _{1}\delta _{L}+(1-\psi _{1})\delta _{H})$
and does not depend on the equilibrium interaction.

Similar to (\ref{value-profit-truth}) and (\ref{value-profit-truth2}) we can
now write the utility of a worker with ability $a$ as%
\begin{equation}
W(a|s)=\max_{\pi _{L},\pi _{H}}\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)e^{-\Gamma (\pi _{T}|T,s)} 
\left[ v_{T}(a-c_{T})-\pi _{T}\right] \boldsymbol{1}[a\geq c_{T}],
\label{objective-babbling}
\end{equation}%
where $\pi _{L}$ and $\pi _{H}$ are the profits that the worker offers to
the low and high type firm respectively, and $\Gamma (\pi _{T}|T,s)$ denotes
the queue of workers who offers higher profits to these firms at this
message. Workers can only submit profit bids larger than zero if their type
exceeds the threshold, and therefore $\boldsymbol{1[\cdot ]}$ is an
indicator that takes the value of one if the ability is higher than the work
requirement, and zero otherwise.

Due to the same supermodularity between $a$ and $\pi _{T},$ higher ability
workers leave higher profits. Again there cannot be any mass points nor
holes in the distribution of offers $\Gamma (\pi _{T}|T,s).$ We focus for
now on workers with ability larger than requirement $c_{H},$ and will later
show that this is the relevant range. This allows us to drop the indicator
function in (\ref{objective-babbling}). For such workers, utility increases
in ability according to%
\begin{equation*}
W^{\prime }(a|s)=e^{-\Lambda (a|s)}\hat{v},
\end{equation*}%
where $\Lambda (a|s)$ is the queue of workers at message $s$ with type $a$
or higher and $\hat{v}$ is the average valuation for their work 
\begin{equation*}
\widehat{v}=\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)v_{T}\boldsymbol{.}
\end{equation*}%
Clearly the average valuation is increasing in the belief that the firm is
of high type. We can now apply the same steps as in the Section \ref%
{sec:analysis}, just with this replaced term for the value. As in (\ref%
{first derivative}) the increase in value has to equal the increase in
outside option to make free entry feasible, so 
\begin{equation*}
e^{-\Lambda (a|s)}\widehat{v}=u^{\prime }(a),
\end{equation*}%
and the second derivative yields in analogy to (\ref{second derivative}) 
\begin{equation*}
\lambda (a|s)e^{-\Lambda (a|s)}\widehat{v}=u^{\prime \prime }(a)
\end{equation*}%
so that in analogy to we obtain 
\begin{equation*}
\lambda (a|s)=u^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a).
\end{equation*}%
So tightness-density is completely governed by the outside option, exactly
as in the previous section. So in terms of types that approach a given
initial signal, the allocation remains random where the types between $s=L$
and $s=H$ overlap, and it is identical to the types that queue in the fully
revealing equilibrium we already studied. Wage bids are also relatively
straightforward. At a given first signal $s$ and a second signal that
reveals type $T,$ the wages are given by a differential equation identical
to (\ref{wages})%
\begin{equation}
b_{T}^{\prime }(a|T,s)=v_{T}b_{1}^{\prime }(a).  \label{wages2}
\end{equation}

The lowest worker type $\underline{a}_{s}$ that approaches message $s$ can
again only win if no other worker is present, no matter whether the second
signal indicates that it is a high or a low type. So at either firm type
this worker extracts all output conditional on matching, and in this case
obtains 
\begin{eqnarray*}
&&\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)v_{T}(\underline{a}_{s}-c_{T}) \\
&=&\widehat{v}[\underline{a}_{s}-\widehat{c}]\text{ with }\widehat{c}%
:=\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)[v_{T}c_{T}]/\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)v_{T}.
\end{eqnarray*}%
where the second line simply re-orders the terms. The term $\widehat{c}$
equals $c_{L}$ when workers think they face a low type for sure (i.e., $\Psi
(L|s)=1$), $\widehat{c}$ is monotonically increasing in the probability that
the firm is a high type, and it equals $c_{H}$ when workers think they face
a high type for sure. For the same reasons as in Section \ref{sec:analysis}
the lowest type is solely determined by the costs via implicit function 
\begin{equation}
\underline{a}_{s}=\widehat{c}+u(\underline{a}_{s})/u^{\prime }(\underline{a}%
_{s}).  \label{cutoff-general}
\end{equation}%
The lowest type $\underline{a}_{s}$ is increasing in $\widehat{c},$ and
therefore increasing in the probability of facing a high type firm.

This fully characterizes the interaction at jobs that do not have cheap-talk
available to them, except that we dropped the indicator function after
equation (\ref{objective-babbling}). We first note that $\underline{a}_{s}$
is always above $c_{H},$ because even $\underline{a}_{T=L}$ is above because
of our restriction on the difference between $c_{L}$ and $c_{H}.$\footnote{%
Equation (\ref{cutoff-general}) applied to $\widehat{c}=c_{L}$ implies that
we are looking for $\underline{a}_{T=L}=c_{L}+u(\underline{a}%
_{T=L})/u^{\prime }(\underline{a}_{T=L}).$ We assumed that $%
c_{H}-c_{L}<u(c_{H})/u^{\prime }(c_{H})$ or equivalently that $%
c_{H}<c_{L}+u(c_{H})/u^{\prime }(c_{H}).$ Therefore, $\underline{a}%
_{T=L}>c_{H}.$ Since the cutoff at any other $\widehat{c}$ is higher, it
will also be above $c_{H}.$} So the worker with lowest ability according to
our derivations so far would bid for jobs both at high and low firms in a
way that leaves non-negative profits for the firms, and this worker would
just recover his/her outside option. We also know from our previous analysis
that no worker with ability below $\underline{a}_{s}$ can recover their
outside option if this worker makes offers to both firms that leave
non-negative profits to these firms. Workers with abilities below $c_{H}$
would choose not to be hired by high firm types. They would never be able to
recover their outside option, though, either, as we now show in the final
step:

Type $\underline{a}_{T=L}$ has 
\begin{eqnarray}
&&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{s}|s)}\sum_{T}\Psi (T|s)v_{T}(\underline{a}%
_{T=L}-c_{T})=U(\underline{a}_{s})  \notag \\
&\Rightarrow &e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{s}|s)}\Psi (L|s)v_{L}(\underline{a}%
_{T=L}-c_{L})<U(\underline{a}_{T=L})  \notag \\
&\Rightarrow &e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{s}|s)}\Psi (L|s)v_{L}<\underline{v}%
_{T=L}(\underline{a}_{T=L}),  \label{x}
\end{eqnarray}%
where $\underline{v}_{T=L}(a)=U(a)/(a-c_{l})$ as defined in Section \ref%
{sec:analysis}. Any type $a<\underline{a}_{T=L}$ who only bids for the low
job obtains $e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{s}|s)}\Psi (L|s)v_{L}(\underline{a}%
-c_{L})$ but has outside option $U(a)=\underline{v}_{T=L}(a)(a-c_{L}).$ For
this to justify entry we have to have $e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}%
_{s}|s)}\Psi (L|s)\geq \underline{v}_{T=L}(a),$ but this is contradicted by (%
\ref{x}) and the fact that $\underline{v}_{T=L}(\underline{a}_{T=L})$ is the
global minimum of $\underline{v}_{T=L}(\underline{a}).\square $

\bigskip \bigskip

\noindent \textit{The proof of Corollary \ref{corollary} entailed the claim that
holding the lowest worker type }$\underline{a}_{m}$\textit{\ at truthful
message }$m$\textit{\ fixed but changing }$v_{m}$\textit{\ does not alter
the expected utility at the lowest type, and we prove this here: }The
expected utility at $\underline{a}_{m}$ is according to (\ref%
{equilibrium lowest type}) given by $e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}v_{m}(%
\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m}).$ We will see that the equilibrium utility at the
lowest type does not change, and therefore the lowest type remains unchanged
when the valuation is altered. To do this, we will take comparative statics
with respect to $v_{m},$ holding $\underline{a}_{m}$ fixed, but allowing $%
\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)$ to change. The derivative is given by 
\begin{eqnarray*}
&&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})+e^{-\Lambda (%
\underline{a}_{m}|m)}\left[ -\frac{d\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}{dv_{m}}%
\right] v_{m}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m}) \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})\left[
1-v_{m}\lambda (\bar{a}_{m}|m)\frac{d\bar{a}_{m}}{dv_{m}}\right] \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})\left[
1-v_{m}\lambda (\bar{a}_{m}|m)u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{m})\right] \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}(\underline{a}_{m}-c_{m})\left[ 1-\frac{%
v_{m}}{u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{m})}\right] \\
&=&0
\end{eqnarray*}

The second line is obtained simply by rewrites the change in the queue of
workers, the third line by implicitly differentiating (\ref{equilibrium
highest type}), the forth line uses (\ref{equilibrium density}) to
substitute out $\lambda (.),$ and the last line again uses (\ref{equilibrium
highest type}).$\square $

\bigskip \bigskip

\noindent \textit{Proof of Proposition \ref{comparison1}:} Part 1 and 3 follow
directly from Lemma \ref{corollary} and \ref{corollary 2}. For Part 2, note
that effects on the number of applications are ambiguous since low firm
types who send signals attract additional low types but loose high types.
How this affects applications depends on the queue of such workers, which by
(\ref{equilibrium density}) depends on $u^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a)$
in these various ability regions, on which we do not have clear
restrictions. For $v_{H}\approx v_{L}$ the second effect vanishes, and
truthful cheap-talk attract more workers to low-message firms. The opposite
is the case for high-message firms, and the total can go either way
depending on entry costs. For $v_{H}$ large, the second effect obviously
dominates and low-type firms loose applicants when they send truthful
messages relative to low-type firms who cannot send a message. High type
firms with a message gain high ability workers but loose low-ability
workers, relative to counterparts that cannot send messages. But since the
elasticity of $u^{\prime }()$ is bounded by some number, say $B,$ the queue
at worker types $a$ is bounded by $B/a.$ So the additional worker types that
are added for high type firms have very little extra weight, which implies
for large enough $v_{H}$ that the effect on low type firms dominates.

For Part 4, note that efficiency per firm with a message is given by 
\begin{equation}
\max_{\lambda _{m=L}^{P}(.),\lambda _{m=H}^{P}(.)}\delta
_{L}S(v_{L},c_{L}|\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.))+\delta _{H}S(v_{H},c_{H}|\lambda
_{m=H}^{P}(.))  \label{max1}
\end{equation}%
where 
\begin{eqnarray*}
S(v,c|\lambda (.)) &=&\int \left( v(a-c)\right) d\left(
1-e^{-\int_{a}^{\infty }\lambda (a^{\prime })da^{\prime }}\right) -\int
\lambda (a)u(a)da \\
&&\int v(a-c)\lambda (a)e^{-\int_{a}^{\infty }\lambda (a^{\prime
})da^{\prime }}da-\int \lambda (a)u(a)da
\end{eqnarray*}%
represents the output of the firm minus the forgone outside option of all
the workers who queue at this firm. Let 
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S}(v,c)=\max_{\lambda (.)}S(v,c|\lambda (.)).  \label{max-by-type}
\end{equation}%
The optimality conditions for this problem coincide nearly everywhere with
the equilibrium conditions (\ref{first derivative}) for a given firm type,
and it shares the same boundary conditions. This establishes that the
equilibrium decentralizes the planner's solution. Evidently the value of
program (\ref{max1}) is given by $\delta _{L}\mathcal{S}(v_{L},c_{L})+\delta
_{H}\mathcal{S}(v_{H},c_{H}).$

For firms who cannot send a message the maximal efficiency per firm is
attainable through queue of 
\begin{eqnarray}
\max_{\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.),\lambda _{s=H}^{P}(.)} &&\delta _{L}\left[ \psi
_{1}S(v_{L},c_{L}|\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.))+(1-\psi _{1})S(v_{L},c_{L}|\lambda
_{s=H}^{P}(.))\right]  \label{max2} \\
&&+\delta _{H}\left[ \psi _{1}S(v_{H},c_{H}|\lambda _{s=H}^{P}(.))+(1-\psi
_{1})S(v_{H},c_{H}|\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.))\right] ,  \notag
\end{eqnarray}%
where $S(.)$ still denotes the output minus the forgone outside option of
workers, but which queue a firm obtains now depends on which signal it
sends. The optimality conditions for this problem again coincide with
conditions for the decentralzied equilibrium, and thus the decentralized
equilibrium obtains the highest surplus given the information frictions.
Nevertheless, evidently value of maximization problem (\ref{max1}) is higher
than that of maximization problem (\ref{max2}) since the queue length in the
former can be chosen to maximize the surplus for each firm while in the
latter it has to partially cater to both firm types. Formally, note that
both $S(v_{L},c_{L}|\lambda _{s=L}^{\ast }(.))$ and $S(v_{L},c_{L}|\lambda
_{s=H}^{\ast }(.))$ are bounded above by $\mathcal{S}(v_{L},c_{L}),$ and $%
S(v_{H},c_{H}|\lambda _{s=H}^{P}(.))$ and $S(v_{H},c_{H}|\lambda
_{s=L}^{P}(.))$ are bounded above by $\mathcal{S}(v_{L},c_{L}).$ Therefore
the value of problem (\ref{max2}) is bounded from above by $\delta _{L}%
\mathcal{S}(v_{L},c_{L})+\delta _{H}\mathcal{S}(v_{H},c_{H}),$ which
coincides with the value of problem (\ref{max1}).

In fact, one can prove that value of problem (\ref{max2}) is strictly below
that of (\ref{max1}): the maximizer $\lambda ^{\ast P}(a|v,c)$ of problem (%
\ref{max-by-type}) is unique for given $(v,c)$ and strictly different for
different values of $(v,c)$ as it inhits the same properties as the
equilibrium queue length under truthful cheap talk. Equal value of the two
problems above would require that that $\lambda ^{\ast
P}(.|v_{L},c_{L})=\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.)=\lambda _{s=H}^{P}(.)\,\ $and $%
\lambda ^{\ast P}(a|v_{H},c_{H})=\lambda _{s=L}^{P}(.)=\lambda
_{s=H}^{P}(.), $ which yields a contradiction to $\lambda ^{\ast
P}(.|v_{L},c_{L})\neq \lambda ^{\ast P}(a|v_{H},c_{H}).\square $

\bigskip \bigskip

\noindent \textit{The main body claimed that truth-telling is an equilibrium when
valuations are far apart, while generally this is not the case when
valuations are close together. Formally, we prove the following proposition: 
}

\begin{prop}
\label{prop: incentive-combat} For given $u(a)$, $v_{L}$ and $c_{H}$,
thruthtelling is incentive compatible if $v_{H}$ is sufficiently large. For $%
c_{H}=0,$ when $v_{H}$ approaches $v_{L},$ incentive compatability for
thruth-telling generically fails.
\end{prop}

\noindent Proof of Proposition \ref{prop: incentive-combat}: Note first that the probability
that a firm at message $m$ has no applicant with qualification above $a$ is $%
e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}.$ That means that the density that the best applicant is
of type $a$ is

$\lambda (a|m)e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}$. So the expected profit at message $m$ for
firm type $t$ is at least%
\begin{eqnarray}
\Pi (m|t) &\geq &\int_{\underline{a}_{m}}^{\bar{a}_{m}}\max
\{v_{t}(a-c_{t})-b_{m}(a),0\}\lambda (a|m)e^{-\Lambda (a|m)}da.  \notag \\
&=&\int_{\underline{a}_{m}}^{\bar{a}_{m}}\max \left\{ \frac{%
v_{t}(a-c_{t})-b_{m}(a)}{v_{m}},0\right\} u^{\prime \prime }(a)da.
\label{truth-start} \\
&=&\int_{\underline{a}_{m}}^{\bar{a}_{m}}\max \left\{ \frac{v_{t}(a-c_{t})-%
\frac{v_{m}}{v_{L}}b_{L}(a)}{v_{m}},0\right\} u^{\prime \prime }(a)da. 
\notag \\
&=&\int_{\underline{a}_{m}}^{\bar{a}_{m}}\max \left\{ \frac{v_{t}}{v_{m}}%
(a-c_{t})-\frac{b_{L}(a)}{v_{L}},0\right\} u^{\prime \prime }(a)da,  \notag
\end{eqnarray}%
where it is suppressed that $\bar{a}_{m}$ is a function of $v_{m}$ and $%
\underline{a}_{m}$ is a function of $c_{t}.$ The first line holds exactly
with equality if workers with higher ability offer more profits. In this
case the highest $a$ is always selected, and leaves profits $%
v_{t}(a-c_{t})-b_{m}(a)$ to the firm whenever this is positive. The second
line simply substitutes (\ref{second derivative}), and the third line uses $%
b_{H}(a)=\frac{v_{H}}{v_{L}}b_{L}(a)$ which follows directly from Corollary %
\ref{corollary}.

On the equilibrium path we have proven that higher types indeed offer more
profits, so the first line holds with equality when $m=t=L$ or $m=t=H.$ It
also holds with equality when high types contemplate to send a low message $%
(t=H$ but $m=L)$: since $v_{L}(a-c_{L})-b_{L}(a)$ is increasing in $a$ in
equilibrium, also $v_{L}(a-c_{L})-b_{L}(a)$ is increasing for all $a\geq
c_{H},$ and worker types that have a lower $a$ are always rejected as taken
into account in the max operator. This makes it easy to analyze these three
cases. It may not hold in the case of low firm types sending a high message $%
(t=L$ and $m=H)$ which makes its analysis more challenging.

Truth-telling requires that $\Pi (H|H)\geq \Pi (L|H)$ and $\Pi (L|L)\geq \Pi
(H|L).$

\textit{We will first consider the truth-telling condition for high types }$%
(T=H)$\textit{:}

\begin{eqnarray}
\Pi (H|H) &\geq &\Pi (L|H)  \label{truth1} \\
&\Leftrightarrow &\int_{\underline{a}_{H}}^{\bar{a}_{H}}\left[ a-c_{t}-\frac{%
b_{L}(a)}{v_{L}}\right] u^{\prime \prime }(a)da\geq \int_{\underline{a}%
_{L}}^{\bar{a}_{L}}\max \left\{ \frac{v_{H}}{v_{L}}(a-c_{t})-\frac{b_{L}(a)}{%
v_{L}},0\right\} u^{\prime \prime }(a)da  \label{truth2}
\end{eqnarray}

For $0=c_{L}=c_{H},$ it holds that $\underline{a}_{H}=\underline{a}_{L}=0.$
Fix $v_{L},$ and vary $v_{H}.$ The derivatives are%
\begin{eqnarray}
\frac{d\Pi (H|H)}{dv_{H}} &=&\left[ \bar{a}_{H}-\frac{b_{L}(\bar{a}_{H})}{%
v_{L}}\right] u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{H})\frac{d\bar{a}_{H}}{dv_{H}}=%
\bar{a}_{H}-\frac{b_{L}(\bar{a}_{H})}{v_{L}},  \label{truth3} \\
\frac{d\Pi (L|H)}{dv_{H}} &=&\int_{\underline{a}_{L}}^{\bar{a}_{L}}\frac{a}{%
v_{L}}u^{\prime \prime }(a)da,  \label{truth4}
\end{eqnarray}%
where the second equality in the first line follows from implicitly
differentiating (\ref{equilibrium highest type}). The first line goes to
infinity since $b_{L}(a)$ is bounded away from $v_{L}a$ for all $a>%
\underline{a}_{H}+\varepsilon $ for some $\varepsilon ,$ which follows from (%
\ref{wages}) and the assumption that $au^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a)$
is bounded away from zero. The second line is simply a constant. So
truthtelling is incentive compatible for high types when $v_{H}$ is
sufficiently large.

When $v_{H}\approx v_{L}$ and $c_{H}=0,$ truthtelling requires 
\begin{equation}
\bar{a}_{L}-\frac{b_{L}(\bar{a}_{L})}{v_{L}}\geq \int_{\underline{a}_{L}}^{%
\bar{a}_{L}}\frac{a}{v_{L}}u^{\prime \prime }(a)da,  \label{truth-local}
\end{equation}%
which follows since $\bar{a}_{H}\approx \bar{a}_{L}.$

\textit{Now consider truth-telling for low types }$(T=L):$

We will first consider truthtelling when valuations of high and low type
firms are roughly similar, and will show that incentive compatibility
generically fails when $v_{H}$ approaches $v_{L}$ and $c_{H}=0.$ In this
case, it has to hold that%
\begin{equation*}
\int_{\underline{a}_{L}}^{\bar{a}_{L}}\left[ a-\frac{b_{L}(a)}{v_{L}}\right]
u^{\prime \prime }(a)da\geq \int_{\underline{a}_{H}}^{\bar{a}_{H}}\max
\left\{ \frac{v_{L}}{v_{H}}a-\frac{b_{L}(a)}{v_{L}},0\right\} u^{\prime
\prime }(a)da.
\end{equation*}%
Taking the derivative with respect to $v_{H}$ and evaluating it around $%
v_{H}\approx v_{L}$ (and $\bar{a}_{H}\approx \bar{a}_{L}$ as well as $%
\underline{a}_{H}=\underline{a}_{L}=0)$ yields%
\begin{equation}
0\geq -\int_{\underline{a}_{H}}^{\bar{a}_{H}}\frac{a}{v_{L}}u^{\prime \prime
}(a)da+\left[ \bar{a}_{H}-\frac{b_{L}(\bar{a}_{H})}{v_{L}}\right] .
\label{truth-local2}
\end{equation}%
Inequality (\ref{truth-local2}) is the opposite of inequality (\ref%
{truth-local}), and generically in the space of permissable $u(.)$ and $%
v_{L} $ they do not both hold simultaneously.

Next, we consider truth-telling of low types when $v_{H}$ is large (for
fixed $v_{L})$ and will show that it is incentive compatible. We will first
build intuition under the case where firms are willing to hire all workers,
so that $0=c_{L}=c_{H}.$ It will then be easy to expand to the general case
afterwards. For this part of the proof it is convenient to denote by $\check{%
a}$ the ability type with $u^{\prime }(v)=1\,$or ability type $\bar{a}_{L},$
whichever one is larger. Let $\check{v}\geq v_{L}$ be such that $\bar{a}_{H}=%
\check{a}$ if $v_{H}=\check{v}.$ Note that if the low firm type could only
match with workers of abilities $a\in \lbrack 0.\check{a}],$ it is easy to
show that deviating and sending a high message is not profitable:

- For abilities $a\in \lbrack \underline{a}_{H},\bar{a}_{L}]$ the queue does
not change but the wage becomes $b_{H}(a)=b_{L}(a)v_{H}/v_{L},$ so the firm
pays more for these workers but does not attract more of them.

- For abilities $a\in \lbrack 0,\underline{a}_{H}]$ workers cese to approach
the firm if it sends the high signal.

- For abilities $a\in \lbrack \bar{a}_{L},\check{a}]$, let $\bar{g}%
=\sup_{a\in \lbrack \bar{a}_{L},\check{a}]}g(a)>0$ so that the wage $%
b_{H}(a)\geq \bar{g}v_{H}/v_{L},$ which exceeds $v_{L}\check{a}$ for $v_{H}$
sufficiently large. So the low type firm would make a loss hiring these
workers, and would refuse to do so. So their presence does not constitute a
profitable deviation.

Now consider also abilities beyond $\check{a}.$ In general, by raising $%
v_{H} $ from $\check{v}$ to higher values, from the perspective of a
low-type firm that contemplates to send message $H$ this has two effects:
For all ability types $a\in \lbrack 0,\bar{a}_{H}]$ the wage $%
b_{H}(a)=v_{H}g(a)$ increases when $v_{H}$ is marginally increased, which is
a negative outcome from the firm's perspective. But one also raises the
upper end of workers willing to match. We will show that even for the second
effect, the additional wage will outweigh the benefit in matching. We know
that $u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})=v_{H}.$ So $d\bar{a}_{H}/dv_{H}=1/u^{\prime
\prime }(\bar{a}_{H}).$ So the wage at the higest type increase by 
\begin{eqnarray*}
db_{H}(\bar{a}_{H})/dv_{H} &=&d(v_{H}g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right) )/dv_{H} \\
&=&g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right) +v_{H}g^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})d\bar{a}%
_{H}/dv_{H} \\
&=&g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right) +v_{H}g^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})d\bar{a}%
_{H}/dv_{H} \\
&=&g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right) +v_{H}[1-g(\bar{a}_{H})u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{%
a}_{H})/u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})]/u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{H}) \\
&=&g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right) +v_{H}/u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{H})-g(\bar{a%
}_{H})/u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})
\end{eqnarray*}%
For the low firm type who considers sending message $H,$ a higher value $%
v_{H}$ implies the following change in profits when matching with the
highest type at message $H:$ $v_{L}\bar{a}_{H}-b_{H}(\bar{a}_{H}).$ So this
changes in $v_{H}$ according to 
\begin{eqnarray*}
&&v_{L}d\bar{a}_{H}/dv_{H}-db_{H}(\bar{a}_{H})/dv_{H} \\
&=&v_{L}/u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{H})-g\left( \bar{a}_{H}\right)
-v_{H}/u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{H})+g(\bar{a}_{H})/u^{\prime }(\bar{a}%
_{H})
\end{eqnarray*}%
which is definitely negative as $\bar{a}_{H}\geq \check{a},$ which implies $%
u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{H})\geq 1.$ This establishes that for high enough $%
v_{H} $ low types strictly prefer to send message $m=L$ to message $m=H$ in
a truthtelling equilibrium. Since high types also prefer to tell the truth,
this establishes that truth-telling is an equilibrium outcome when $%
c_{L}=c_{H}=0.$

We finally have to establish that truth-telling is incentive compatible for
low firm types when $v_{H}$ is sufficiently large even in the more general
setting where $0=c_{L}<c_{H}:$ We have already established that low firm
types would not pretend to be high firm types if costs are zero. In the
candidate equilibrium with truth-telling, note that strictly positive costs
at high types do not change the workers who approach low types or their
bids. On the other hand, at high types the set of worker types shrinks,
those who do approach high messages do so with exactly the same queue $%
\lambda (a|H)$ by (\ref{equilibrium density})$,$ and their bids increase. So
if low types do not want to deviate and pretend to be high types under $%
c_{H}=0,$ they will not want to do so when $c_{H}>0.$ For high types we had
already established incentive compatibility in the general case, which
concludes the proof. $\square $

\bigskip \bigskip

\begin{prop}
\label{extension} For more general production functions $\phi (a,v_{T})$
with constant semi-elasticity of $\phi _{a}$ with respect to worker ability for given firm type,
strict log-supermodularity (log-submodularity) of $\phi $ ensures that the
lowest ability type $\underline{a}$ that approaches such firms under
perfectly-revealing cheap-talk is increasing (decreasing).
\end{prop}

Proof of Proposition \ref{extension}: Note that the highest worker type that
approaches such firms is increasing in $v$, due to the boundary condition $%
\phi _{a}(\bar{a}_{m},v)=u^{\prime }(\bar{a})$ and strict concavity of $\phi 
$ in worker ability. The queue length is given by $\lambda _{m}(a)=u^{\prime
\prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a)-\phi _{aa}(a,m)/\phi _{a}(a,m),$ with lower
end-point conditions $\phi _{a}(\bar{a},v)=u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{m})$ and $%
e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}\phi _{v}(\underline{a},v)=u(\underline{a}%
).$ The latter implies $\partial \underline{a}/\partial v$ equals

\begin{eqnarray*}
&&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}\phi _{v}(\underline{a}%
_{m},v)+e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}\left[ -\frac{d\Lambda (\underline{%
a}_{m}|m)}{dv_{m}}\right] \phi (\underline{a}_{m},v) \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}\phi (\underline{a}_{m},v)\left[ \frac{%
\phi _{v}(\underline{a}_{m},v)}{\phi (\underline{a}_{m},v)}-\lambda (\bar{a}%
_{m}|m)\frac{d\bar{a}_{m}}{dv_{m}}\right] \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}\phi (\underline{a}_{m},v)\left[ \frac{%
\phi _{v}(\underline{a}_{m},v)}{\phi (\underline{a}_{m},v)}-\lambda (\bar{a}%
_{m}|m)\left[ -\frac{\phi _{av}(\bar{a}_{m},v)}{\phi _{aa}(\bar{a}%
_{m},m)-u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{m})}\right] \right] \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}\phi (\underline{a}_{m},v)\left[ \frac{%
\phi _{v}(\underline{a}_{m},v)}{\phi (\underline{a}_{m},v)}-\frac{u^{\prime
\prime }(\bar{a}_{m})-\phi _{aa}(\bar{a}_{m},v)}{\phi _{a}(\bar{a}_{m},v)}%
\left[ --\frac{\phi _{av}(\bar{a}_{m},v)}{\phi _{aa}(\bar{a}%
_{m},v)-u^{\prime \prime }(\bar{a}_{m})}\right] \right] \\
&=&e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}_{m}|m)}\phi (\underline{a}_{m},v)\left[ \frac{%
\phi _{v}(\underline{a}_{m},v)}{\phi (\underline{a}_{m},v)}-\frac{\phi _{av}(%
\bar{a}_{m},v)}{\phi _{a}(\bar{a}_{m},v)}\right]
\end{eqnarray*}%
A sufficient condition for this to be strictly negative is that $\phi $ is
strictly log-supermodular. In that case 
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\phi _{v}(\underline{a}_{m},v)}{\phi (\underline{a}_{m},v)}-\frac{\phi
_{av}(\bar{a}_{m},v)}{\phi _{a}(\bar{a}_{m},v)}<\frac{\phi _{v}(\bar{a}%
_{m},v)}{\phi (\bar{a}_{m},v)}-\frac{\phi _{av}(\bar{a}_{m},v)}{\phi _{a}(%
\bar{a}_{m},v)}<0,
\end{equation*}%
where both the first and the second inequality are immediate implications of
strict log-supermodularity. The opposite holds for strict log-submodularity.$%
\square $

\section{Short discussion of model features and robustness}\label{sec:app-discussion}

This appendix section briefly discusses the three robustness points mentioned at the end of Subsection \ref{sec:analysis} in the main body. \bigskip

\textit{Partially revealing equilibria: }Truthful cheap talk is not the only
equilibrium in cheap-talk games, even if such equilibria exists. It is
well-known that there always exists a babbling equilibrium where receivers
disregard the messages because senders have choosen them at random. Whether
parameters admit a truthful equilibrium or not, there also often exist
partially revealing equilibrium where some additional information is
transmitted, but not all types are perfectly revealed. Here we briefly point
out that if a cheap-talk intervention displays any effect, it must be linked
to some information transmission. We show here that all equilibria where
cheap-talk reveals some additional information inherit the same broad
empirical implications: Proposition \ref{comparison1} and the Corollaries \ref%
{corollary} and \ref{corollary 2} that lead up to it are based around
comparative statics. Less information essentially acts to average the
valuation and the costs. The base model does it for the extreme case of
starting with perfect information revelation. But the comparative statics
hold similarly when messages are only partially informative.\footnote{%
Parts 1, 3 and 4 of Proposition Proposition \ref{comparison1} rely on
comparative statics and carry over to partially informative equilibria. In
contrast, Part 2 of Proposition \ref{comparison1} uses a limit argument for
the the size of the high valuation, holding the amount of information that
is conveyed constant. The approach to the proof extends if there is a lower
bound on the information that a partially informative equilibrium conveys
about firms, but the technique of the proof does not extend if the amount of
information goes to zero as the valuation of the high type firm becomes
large.}

\bigskip

\textit{The role of the second signal:} The second signal captures that
workers can target their bidding to more information after having read the
details of the job description. Taking the limiting case where this signal
is arbitrarily precise simplifies the mathematical representation of the
problem substantially as the bid is determined simply as the weighted
average of bids from two different differential equations.. Consider instead
a setting where workers remain unsure about the firm type at the point of
bidding, and assign probability $\Psi (T)$ to it being type $T$. It is easy
to show that their bids would follow differential equation $b(a)/\left(
\tsum\nolimits_{T}\lambda (a)\Psi (T)/(v_{T}-b^{\prime }(a))\right) =1.%
\footnote{%
See our previous working paper Horton, Johari and Kircher (2021) that
derives this and solves the related model, but at the expense of assuming
that workers do not receive any first signal. This entails the very strong

for the incentives of firms to mis-represente their types. In particular,
for low type firms who pretend to be high types this induces high wage
demands. If workers were to learn the actual firm type, low type firms that
mis-represented their type could attract more applicants, but these would
learn their type and then bid low. One can show that truth-telling in this
setting would not be sustained. So our formulation is best viewed as an
approximation to the setting where signals are not fully revealing. It also
highlights the value of our empirical setting: it is not obvious that
allowing for cheap-talk will increase market information, as this would fail
under different informational assumptions, and off course under different
equilibria such as the well-known "babbling" equilibrium where no
information is transmitted.

\textit{More general production functions}: Our production function is
special case of concave production function $\phi (a,T)$ that describes the
output of a worker of type $a$ at a firm of type $T.$ Similar steps as in
the main body yield in analogy to (\ref{equilibrium density}) that $\lambda
_{m}(a)=u^{\prime \prime }(a)/u^{\prime }(a)-\phi _{aa}(a,m)/\phi _{a}(a,m),$
with upper and lower end-point conditions $\phi _{a}(\bar{a}%
_{m},m)=u^{\prime }(\bar{a}_{m})$ and $e^{-\Lambda (\underline{a}%
_{m}|m)}\phi _{v}(\underline{a}_{m},v)=u(\underline{a}_{m}).$ The upper
endpoint continues to be higher for higher firm types if $\phi $ is
supermodular in $a$ and $T,$ which was assured in our baseline
specification. The queue length $\lambda _{m}(a)$ was independent of $m$ in
our baseline specification, which made the setup particularly tractable as
it implied that workers choose among firms "as if" at random. From the
earlier equation it is apparent that this only continues to apply more
generally if the semi-elasticity of $\phi _{a}$ with respect to worker
ability is independent of $m,$ as is the case for example for production $%
\ln (v_{T}(a-c))+d$ or $f(v_{T})g(a-c)+h(v_{t})$ where $c$ and $d$ are
positive constants and $f(.)$, $g(.)$ and $h()$ are non-negative and
increasing functions. We note that in our base theory we introduced
differences in costs $c$ across firm types to capture that fact that higher
firm types might need higher worker types. Without this, the lowest worker
types would be the same across firms, and simply more high types are added
for higher firm types. With the more general production functions just
mentioned, low types endogeneously drop out as the firm valuation $v$
increases even with a common cost $c$ because of additional comptition from
higher worker types, iff the production is strictly log-supermodular in $a$
and $v$ as proven in Proposition \ref{extension} in Appendix Section \ref{sec:proofs}. This is
the case in the examples before if $f(.)$ is more elastic than $h(.).$
Things become substantially more involved when the semi-elasticity of $\phi
_{a}$ depends on the firm type, which exceeds the bounds of this paper.
